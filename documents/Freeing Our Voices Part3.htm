Losing the plot…

 

I'm realising there is a risk that you might lose the plot here as we return from the workout. I did when I first tried to write it yesterday. It happened like this.

 

Coming back to my office from a coaching session with a client in London I had a clear intention to write this final part and lots of ideas about what to say. Then, mentally shutting out the background noise of kids and friends on half term holiday, I worked hard all afternoon slogging at my keyboard. By 5pm, with a few thousand words printed off  in front of me, I read back what I had written and disappointment set in. Where was the voice? I had gone into my head and lost contact with my belly, spinning out theoretical, indirect language not voice.

 

Wondering how that happened, I noticed my attention had somehow locked onto the "task" of writing and I forgot about you reading this. I remembered how easily this can happen when speaking.

 

And I realised you could easily lose the plot too in stepping into Part 3.

 

Waking up this morning, I realised more.

Recovering the plot… uncovering the self-organising principle

 

I realised that behind the idea of "uncorking", "letting the genie out of the bottle", and "Where's your voice?" is an important principle that points to what good facilitators do and the promise of this book: the facilitator's way of leading.

 

I realised maybe I should have waited, not rushed in. Yes. Maybe yesterday I should have asked myself a different question before starting on the "task". Yes. What question though? How about this:

 

What is really going on here?

 

And this:

 

What and who am I leaving out that I need to include?

 

And this:

 

So what is the real task here?

 

Today I've already answered the first two questions: what was going on (I slogged at the task then felt disappointed I'd lost my belly voice) and who I am leaving out (you). As for question 3, I realise the real task here in part 3 is to show you how to get a good return on the time you have invested in the workout.

 

Now I have the real task defined, everything feels easier and I know just what to do. I'm back on track and feeling "connected" and excited.

The Self-Organising Principle

 

When we lose the plot, getting confused, disappointed, blocked, low in energy and our voice goes, there's something we're leaving out that needs to be included. Often it's the emotion, or a quiet person, or the real question behind the question, that is, the belly voice.

 

We leaves things out like this, editing all the time, and it gets us into all sorts of trouble, in meetings, teams, leading people and trying to change organisations.

 

When we include what's there, the unvoiced feeling, need, real question, belly voice, a spontaneous shift occurs, we find the plot again and the work tends to organise itself.

 

Great facilitators and leaders know their job is to release the natural energy the system has to self-organise, by including voice, uncorking the genie.

 

How?

 

We STOP slogging at the task.

 

We CHECK: how are we/they feeling right now?

 

We ASK ourselves the 3 questions:

-        What is really going on here?

-        What and who am I leaving out?

-        So what is the real task here?

 

We trust the answer will come, bringing natural energy and a way forward.

 

Each time we're delighted we have to do less than we think: as long as we remembered to leave a space for the system to spontaneously organise itself.

Growing your Third Order team

 

I'd like to invite you to think of your team as a living thing: not mechanical in its procedures and not limited in what it can accomplish. We'll look here at finding how to help your team continue to grow towards its fuller potential, releasing its natural energy to become more and more capable, and a centre of energy in the Third Order organisation (which we'll go on to say more about later).

 

You have already started awakening your team in Test 10, but of course that wasn't the end of it. There's ongoing work to do. What are your ambitions for the team? How will you set the team on a path towards continuing growth?

 

One Dialogue and Action session is only the start of an ongoing practice which develops the trust and confidence needed to release more and more natural energy.

 

Think about what you would do in a sports team to grow confidence and crank up the performance. Beyond what we explored in Test 10 to get the team going, do you have the other processes in place to support top performance such as measurement, communication, recognition, celebration, visioning, target-setting and coaching?

 

As the team gets more comfortable with the voicing practices, the facilitator and leader can continue to challenge members to reach ever higher levels of trust and truth. Apart from discovering what was unvoiced and how to include it, team members need to learn exactly how their speaking impacts others, for example when they are making and delivering promises. Each small promise delivered by a team member and each target achieved by the team represents a step up to a higher level of trust. The point is underlined by Fernando Flores who says executives often make promises they have no intention of fulfilling:

____________

 

Ikea can't get away with selling a $30 table with missing parts or bad instructions but you think you can… in multimillion dollar projects!

____________

 

As the team gets stronger and more capable, then as leader you raise the parasol, reducing the protection you provide. Remember the notion of parasol is to do with the level of risk, trust, challenge and support. If you over-protect the team, shading them from harsh external realities, making goals too small and easy, setting only narrow, well-defined tasks, then they go to sleep on the job, becoming dependent, defensive and childish. If you provide too little protection, making the work too hard, they frazzle in the harsh glare of the sunlight, and paralysed by fear they give up or fail. You aim to provide the right level of protection to let them win and as their confidence builds you reduce the protection and give them more challenge. You are finding the margin between too much and too little in which team members come alive and self-organise.

 

It is your job to keep growing your team and raising the parasol, passing out more and more responsibility to them, freeing yourself for your new culture-shaping work: bringing the inclusion principle to life in a wider Third Order organisation.

 

Stop and ask yourself:

What is your team's current level of confidence?

 

 

What is their current level of achievement?

 

 

Does the parasol need to be raised or lowered to release their self-organising capacity?

 

What will you do to continue developing the team's confidence and performance?

 

What support will you continue to offer to the team?

 

Which of your current responsibilities will you pass to the team, when the team has grown sufficiently to take them?

 

 

 

 

Freeing up your organisation

 

How do you bring the Self-Organising Principle to life beyond your team in the wider organisation? How do you create the conditions for a "system" larger than one team, to become naturally energetic and self-organising?

 

Sitting here alone in my office on a cold sunny October morning I feel remote from the dynamic hubbub of people, pushing and pulling, stretching, laughing, fighting, bumping, jostling, joshing, chasing, trying to climb their particular mountains, or squeeze out crucial decisions, or tease out solutions, or picking their teams up after a setback, or noticing colleagues empty desks and feeling sad. But when we think about freeing up the energy in an organisation, this is the context.

 

How does a leader somehow keep everyone aware of the "real work", when the goal posts are shifting daily and everyone is either busy at their pre-set tasks or distracted by what's going on around them? How much do you attempt to organise and control from the centre? When does organising and controlling stop working and start wasting energy? We'll look at how the  idea of a Third Order organisation might help you approach these challenges.

 

As my daughter and her friend come into my office asking me to help find the hamster which is lost behind the kitchen cupboards, I wonder which is my "real work": writing this or finding the hamster. I notice some options:

-        Go into the kitchen and try to get the hamster out myself (hands on, high control)

-        Ask them exactly where is the hamster and tell them exactly what to do (hands off, medium/high control)

-        Ask them what they've tried and what other ideas they have? Encourage them to try something else (hands off, medium control)

-        Tell them I'm too busy and leave them to figure it out (hands off, low control)

High control would distract me, sucking me into their problem and stops me doing this. It might give them a speedier result, but denies them the satisfaction of solving it themselves. Low control feels sort of uncaring and negligent. I find myself in the hands-off middle control zone…. Then…

 

Hearing "Fizz you stupid hamster", and "Daaaaad", I leave the office for the kitchen with a feeling of irritation, pull out the cooker, break the flimsy kickboard in my haste by mistake (surge of anger, chill pill!) then hamster exposed. Return to office leaving my daughter to retrieve the animal. I'm irritated also because seeking the magic point where my daughter and her friend could be self-organising,  drew me in more than I wanted and expected.

 

We'll be looking at what "self-organising" might mean in your own organisation, the types of control you use and the unnecessary and inhibiting controls that are there for not very good reasons (perhaps low trust, a habit of branding everything in sight, a preference for neatness, big is beautiful, a habit of centralising and standardising, dehumanising, systematising and squeezing the life out of everyone?). Why? Remember how too much or too little structure affects stress and efficiency: too much and people get lazy and inefficient, feeling small and powerless; too little and people get crazy, stressed and inefficient!

As an example of seeking the right amount of structure in a system, I have worked for several years with a small market research company, at one time as a director and more recently as a consultant. Here's the story.

____________

 

Four busy and successful years after starting up there was huge pressure on the four founders to do revenue earning work, while also managing the work and running the company. Staff enjoyed their work, but did not feel trusted, treated like grown-ups or able to grow in their jobs. Pursuing the Self-Organising principle, we got these frustrations voiced first amongst the founders. Then interviews with their clients revealed how they perceived the company's strengths, weakness, opportunities and threats. Workshop sessions with staff completed the picture.

 

It became clear the structure could not be created by everyone, so this would not be self-organising in an obvious sense. A structure was developed with the founders in response to the full set of voiced and understood  needs. Decisions to divide people into new focused teams and to pass responsibility out to a new tier of Associate Directors were not lightly taken. There was a fear of losing the fun and freedom of the early days. It was painful to see one of the original members of staff who could not accept the new organisation leave.

 

Overall we wanted engender a strong sense of initiative and teamwork in the new order, providing "a bit more and just enough" structure while still retaining lots of dynamism and bubble (= self-organising). It took time but the new order took the company into a new life phase, far from the lifeless, authoritarian bureaucracy everyone feared.

____________

 

There aren't any hard and fast rules for this: it's about finding what seems to be needed for the larger system to bring energy to the people in it, and adjusting until it seems to work. Start by identifying broadly what kind of organisation you currently have:

-        First Order: start-up, entrepreneur in charge, fast chaotic, passionate, all hands on deck, making things happen. Tends to move to Second Order to enable growth through rational decision-making, clearer structure and procedures

-        Second Order: a clear structure of roles, procedures, authority levels, control systems. Tends to be fairly centralised and somewhat hierarchical. At its best providing needed clarity and order. At its worst risk averse, fearful of change, defensive. Tends to move to third order to release energy to face new challenges and get people behind the plan

-        Third Order: continually tightening and loosening controls depending on organisational performance and market conditions, fluid, dynamic, not set in one form, team-based, fast, decentralised, self-organising, networks, decisions taken throughout, sometimes teetering on the edge of chaos.

 

The Self-Organising Principle operates in all three. Which is most like yours?

 

If it's First Order, you might compare yourself to the recent wave of dot.coms, some succeeding, many failing. Some were more autocratic, others more team-based. The Self-Organising principle operates because they are small, there is too much work and not enough people. Everyone knows their job is to muck in and do what's needed. Communication is direct and feedback is fast. It is personal and emotional. Feelings get taken into account because it's obvious when someone is being left out or depressed: everyone feels their work and the whole company suffering as a result. Even in the more autocratic First Order firms there is often a strong sense of belonging to a team, being  known to and included by the others.

 

The market research company example above is a good indication of the issues you face when moving from first to second order organisation. Gather up the voices. Develop an organisational response which provides LESS freedom, a bit more structure, certainty and room to grow in responsibility. Introduce it sensitively. Don't expect it to please anyone.

 

Although facilitators often suggests Second Order is somehow "wrong" and to be avoided, moving in this direction in the market research company example provided "a bit more and just enough" structure to keep people lively and involved. Everything got clearer. Managing the work was partly decentralised to Associate Directors, but managing the company was still quite centralised. There was a bit more formality. There were new specialisms. New procedures were installed. Not everyone could go to every meeting or be included in everything. Including the hidden voices did not mean involving everyone in everything all the time.

 

If you're a Second Order organisation, you can compare yourself to the High Street banks and government department which operated very well during more stable times. But when outside pressures of competition, privatisation, globalisation or new technology start to bear down on you, the Second Order mentality is not to notice and to keep on doing the work as it has always been laid down, or not to adapt enough, or quickly enough.

 

At the extreme your second order organisation can be change averse. Anxiety and resistance may be both deep and unvoiced. Leaders who call their people to a new agenda which requires them to behave differently, get frustrated by the lack of response. Capable but fearful people: "Ask are we capable of change?". These organisations have been good customers for facilitators when they belatedly wake-up and realise new direction, more leadership and communication, and  greater teamwork are needed to become adaptive to fast breaking events in the world outside.

 

Since as facilitators we are often brought in when Second Order organisations want to free up their communication and evolve to something more like Third Order, I suppose it's not surprising we often tend to think Third Order is "right", and is set in a highly democratic, open and teamy mode. Remember the Self-Organising Principle says find the right level of protection/direction for people, then the natural energy emerges. Many organisations, get more energy and more freed-up when leaders provide MORE structure and LESS freedom.  Also actually making a potentially scary move from a secure, comfort zone in Second Order into a less predictable oscillating Third Order form, will feel impossible unless leaders can make the transition feel safe and secure. Introducing Third Order to free up energy  might require strong top down control, which people can find confusing. There is deep paradox here that can best be resolved by being constantly connected with the belly voice of your people.

 

In Third Order there is a kind of rhythm over time: tighten then slacken, centralise then decentralise. It depends whether the organisation is performing, confident, aware of change and adapting.

 

To understand these dynamics we'll look at a leaderless organisation and later at organisations where the leaders somehow get it wrong.

Recently in workshops I have invited teams to play leaderless, swarming games. You might like to try this yourself. As a start, everyone steps out of their seats, we go outside if it's fine, or into a big, uncluttered space indoors. Playing the role of organisation architect, I state a few simple rules that determine how people find their position in the swarm:

-        In the first phase I usually get people walking randomly. The only rules are keep moving, and avoid falling into any patterns. This means for example, if everyone starts moving in one direction, then move the other way; if you see a space anywhere, move in and fill it

-        In the second phase I introduce one further rule: remain close to and equidistant from two people you have secretly chosen (without letting them know). This tends to produce a swirling, rotating wheel sort of pattern rather like birds or fish swarming. Something like a supportive team culture in a safe environment.

-        In the third phase, we up the stakes with a new rule: choose two people (without letting them know), label one a bomb (= danger) and one a shield (= protection): always make sure the shield is between you and the bomb. This causes fast jerky movement and breaking into fragments, and suggests a culture that feels challenging in a slightly hostile environment.

-        Further variations are possible. You can try out the impact of a swooping predator, or in one group we tested the effect of an invisible maverick leader, who followed his own agenda, unknown to the others who were operating using the equidistant rule (second phase). Gradually the group started to follow the maverick as if he was dragging an invisible net.  You can also experiment with more elaborate roles and rules to simulate the pulls of customers, investors, suppliers, middle managers or frontline staff.

 

When you try this notice what energises the swarm : when in phase one the rule is simple people often get bored quickly. When you make the rules over-complicated the dynamic, flowing, adaptive behaviour of the swarm slows and settles. Somewhere in the middle, you can find the right level of "management" rules for the system to operate with natural energy. How often, out of habit or worry, do we fall into control-freak mode, imposing too much direction from above, getting in the way of the natural energy of the system? Leaders who want a natural energy in their organisation have to decide how much control is just enough: a light touch not a heavy hand. How much is just enough will vary over time according to circumstances.

 

As another example of leaderless organisation, have you ever been to an open air market? I was reminded of the swarming experiment when visiting Chatachuk market in Bangkok. There, if you brave the crowds and 37 degrees of centigrade, you can find a million bargains: orchids, songbirds, textiles, T shirts, Rolex watches, fruit, books, antiques, you name it.

 

Who runs this market? Whilst no doubt each stall pays a rent to some sort of office and there may be simple guidelines, for example about where pets stalls are in relation to food stalls, it is apparent that there is no real central plan, and as such the market is quite different from say a department store with its more centralised buying, inventory and point of sale systems. Each stall-holder discovers through the harsh realities of stock sold or unsold which items they will offer. Stall-holders using all their intelligence about what to stock create a proven result: my 14 year old son's eyes light up when he sees obscure skateboarding brands at a fraction of London prices; my 11 year old daughter finds just the ideal presents she has found nowhere else to take home to her friends; my wife has a thrilling time foraging for all sorts of items and I get the new shirt I want. Chatachuk, like many other markets throughout the world illustrates a self-organising form that minimises the need for change, or for leadership.

 

Or, as another example, notice how quickly the message gets around if you pass information to a swarm of a dozen 14 year old skateboarders about say the availability of their favourite obscure brands of clothing, or the visit of one of their skateboarding heroes for a demo, or free pizza. Without anyone in charge, without any formal roles or organisation, they reorder their priorities and adapt their behaviour instantly, text message all their friends and in the blink of an eye, everyone knows and  they're on to it

 

Given that animals like ants, birds, fish and, not forgetting skateboarders, seem able to self-organise, and markets do so too, how little control could organisations cope with? Might organisations that operate with less be more adaptive, better at change? How far do we dare to take this?

The performance in an ant colony or a brain depends on two key ingredients

1.      That each cell/unit/player is making decisions in the light of conditions they find

2.      Lots of good two-way information flowing between players

 

If we carry these principles into your Third Order organisation, you are looking to

1.      Give each person the information, resources and motivation mix they need function at their best

2.      Create good relationships that maximise information sharing between players

 

The old top down model of leadership tended to work from a different premise: authority. Top knows best, with more information flowing down from top than bottom. The old fashioned boss gave out instructions and expected people to follow them, like in the army.

 

The Third order Organisation gets its natural energy from a different way of operating in which each person is well connected to everyone else. It is more like a star than a pyramid, or even a hub. The boss does not know everything that is going on. The style of each relationship is on a level, peer to peer, more friend to friend than boss to subordinate. Respect yes. Superior authority no. Each relationship, if it is built on the principles of Voice you have developed in parts 1&2, will be strong and play its full part in making the larger organisation successful.

 

Your starting point is to invest your time in the few key relationships that will determine your success. Then to start through these relationships to spread out the Voice principles, thus wiring everyone else up into a star. Within your direct reporting teams, we have already explored how to get lateral connections going through Dialogue and Action processes in the team.

 

Earlier I described the use of large group meetings in the Home Office, where a group of people working inside an organisation join with their stakeholders to identify common problems and work together on solving them, thus strengthening relationships. Something special, a new sense of responsibility can emerge when stakeholders are brought face to face like this: a connection is forged in which both parties know what they have to do, and what's more they start to want to do it because it seems to matter more. A style of large system conference embodies the self-organising principle is called Open Space. This remarkable method is useable for a conference size ranging from 5 to 1000+ people. Reframing your role as leader

 

In a recent reality TV series called "Survivor" one participant came across as a perfect leader. Confident. Extravert. Helpful. Caring. Knows about people. Knowledgeable about survival. Capable. He showed the others how to build a shelter on the first day, and gave them the drive and encouragement to do it. He displayed everything a leader needs, and he had more of it than the others. I call this the Alpha chimp model of leadership, based on what happens in ape colonies, it suggests the most capable all-rounder will be accepted by the chimps as their leader. A few days later in Survivor, the perfect leader was the first to be voted off. His colleagues found him too pushy, full of himself, in their faces. On the surface he seemed to perform excellently as a leader, but scratch the surface and you found he left people feeling small and a little aggrieved that he did not bring a better contribution  from them. In other words at some level this dominant leader was experienced by others as a psycho-toxic control freak.

 

Perhaps this is the other extreme from the leaderless swarm: a dominant leader who really leads. Although in principle a Third Order organisation might need to operate at these extremes at times, normally the natural self-organising energy available arises at some point in-between. The leader's role is to find that magic point and let the energy out.

 

Even Steven Johnson, an enthusiast for self-organising systems concludes at the end of his investigation that the leader might need to do more than leaving the organisation to evolve with just a little feedback:

_____________

 

It remains a real question whether… capitalism can model its internal organisation after ant farms or neural nets.  For one, the unpredictability  of emergent systems makes them ideal platforms for book recommending or game-playing, but no one wants a business that might spontaneously fire a phalanx of middle managers for no discernible reason. Controlled randomness is a brilliant recipe for city life and ant foraging, but it’s harder to imagine selling shareholders on it as a replacement for the CEO.

_____________

 

The question for you is if you want to free up your organisation, to build its confidence and capacity to self-organise, what sort of adjustments might be called for in your leadership? How far are you prepared to go in adapting to provide what your "system" is seeking?

 

As an example, let's look again at the health and fitness team described earlier.

During his time the organisation has moved from first order (it was bought from the entrepreneur who started it), to second order. The new MD due to take up his position shortly is known to be more hands-off  as a preference and his arrival coincides  with a company growing up towards third order, with a new management team in place recognising that how they work shapes the culture of the whole company.

Perhaps it is clear that the shift is subtle but important. It is not a question of the leader letting go of everything, and dropping all their responsibilities like a heavy weight onto the heads of the team. For example, in a mobile phone company the chosen control might be customer churn rate. Or in the Home Office a leader might choose to let go of micro-managing traditional crime reduction work and control "reducing the rate of re-offending", or in the drinks company control "production cost per hectolitre" above all else.

 

Making this kind of shift is like passing the ball in football: it requires both the giver and receiver to be ready and flexible. The Awakening Your Team work in Test 10 prepared your team for this but how do you spread such an approach across an entire company that is used to being second order. Each person through the organisation needs to start to think and behave differently.

The benefit is great too:  you can get the spontaneous shift you need from a large group of key people, releasing natural energy, stepping up to a higher level of responsibility.

They want you to coach them, asking them questions that challenge them to face their problems. They want your time. They want a sense of being in contact with you. Face to face is the only contact they really trust.

Rebuilding from ground zero

 

What sort of leadership will you show to take your organisation through a life threatening crisis and back up again afterwards?

 

Without wishing to take you into unpleasant memories, I could not complete this without drawing attention to one of the most pressing crises requiring leadership since the second world war. The atrocious events of September 11th and their aftermath, required an instant response and take us down to some of the bedrock principles of leadership.

 

Mayor Guiliani seemed instantly plugged in and "connected": able to echo back the emotions experienced by the families of victims, the survivors and the rescue services in New York. He was visible as a leader, in front of people, telling them what he knew, keeping us all updated, even as the story was changing constantly, by his presence reassuring people who were feeling deeply insecure, and later leading people back into shops and theatres to get things back to a kind of normality. To many, including me, this was a model of excellent leadership and drew huge respect.

 

It is far from a hands-off style. In more normal times Guiliano's leadership in New York has been a bit unfashionable, regarded by some as heavy- handed, ego-driven, fascist, control freakish, psycho-toxic. But in this crisis it was real, energetic, just what was needed by people caught up in the moment. He had to be making it up as he went along, but he seemed to work from the principle of Voice. And the impact of what he was doing was to give people confidence to get on and do what they had to do. It was well-judged: he released rather than blocked the natural energy in the system.

 

In the weeks following the attacks, your organisation like so many others might have faced unprecedented crises. Perhaps sales and share price have fallen through the floor, and anxiety has risen through the roof. Perhaps as in the some rescue services, hospitals, government departments, and news media the workload has soared. In some, like the postal service, work continues but with a raised level of background alertness and fear. Some people like me who work from an office at home have started to feel a bit isolated: the phone has stopped ringing.

The director ordered to reduce his salary bill by 20%, makes several staff redundant then tries to put through salary increases of 20% for those that remain. The Sales Director offering her resignation after making a round of redundancies because she just doesn't feel she is getting the leadership she needs. Lots of people blaming other people for not doing it right. Plenty of soul searching about why it took a crisis on this scale to confront tough decisions that were needed anyway. Questioning why some people were made redundant and not others. Distaste for false optimism, coupled with a distaste for continuing sadness and morbid pessimism. Impossible.

 

Tiny, tell-tale bits of hysteria, and big speeches all over the place, trying to lift fallen-over organisations back onto the rails, cajoling fearful people back into performance mode.

 

Many leaders like Guiliani may have raised their profile. Many are confused about their responsibilities, needing to show leadership while they feel fearful themselves.

Getting to grips with the change thing

 

Have you embarked on a programme of change in your organisation? Does this require anything more (than we have already explored) from you as a leader? What are the special challenges and pitfalls in leading change?

 

I was one of 12 facilitators who gathered one sunny day this summer at a cricket pavilion in a beautiful deer park near my home, to take stock of this change thing, pool experience and take a view on where it's taking us, and where we're taking it. The big statistic bandied around is that 70% of change programmes fail, so what does this tell us about the part we have to play working alongside people in change?

 

One leader I work with is hugely frustrated with his team of direct reports. He experiences them as in a paralysing pattern running through the whole organisation: people looking to each other for direction rather than getting on with the real work. Given the amazing response that's possible when a leader calls to people in a crisis, then why is it sometimes so hard to mobilise an organisation to change?

 

Richard Foster and Sarah Kaplan describe it like this:

___________

 

Unfortunately control systems can also create "defensive routines" in organisations including the failure to challenge the status quo, the failure to encourage a diversity of opinions, the failure to disagree with superiors thereby displeasing them), communicating in ambiguous and inconsistent ways, and making these failure even when know, "undiscussable". Change becomes impossible.

___________

 

You might as a leader think you have set the parasol at the right level, but something invisible is blocking, and the invisible blockage cannot easily be discussed, bringing us back to the belly voice, and stuck conversation themes from earlier.

 

Last year, knowing how many organisations fail at change, Andy Harmon and I conducted research to find out why, with ten senior level sponsors of large scale change programmes in a wide range of organisations (from 100 to 100,000 employees, in manufacturing, aerospace, arts, pharmaceuticals, government, retail and financial services).

 

We found change programmes are looked at in terms of project plans, programme management techniques and new procedure manuals, as if having these things is proof of having changed. We sought to bring to light something else entirely: the hidden make or break dynamics, what blocks or enables change to happen.

 

Our conclusion in a nutshell was that organisational change is a drama of conflict and transformation, a bit like a war that redefines the context of work for everyone in the organisation. There are two interlocking stories: the leader's, and the organisation's. The leader's story is a quest to gain stakeholders commitment to a new future. The second story is the quest of the organisation, considered collectively to bring that new future into being. The leader's work consists of a series of "critical encounters" between the sponsor and the organisational stakeholders including staff.

 

Each "critical encounter" is a face to face confrontation that contains all the principles of Voice that we have been working with here. In essence it is like resolving a stuck conversation. Getting at the underlying emotion and breaking the undiscussable grip of  power and vulnerability.

 

More on where we're taking this in a minute. Where did we get to that sunny day in the cricket pavilion? We felt more successful than the 70% statistic suggested and agreed it might not mean much, but rather than comforting ourselves into complacency, we continued an honest scrutiny of what we do. There were different views on the importance of leadership, some going more towards "let it be" and others arguing for strong leadership. We made a list of some of the things that made us angry and that we considering wrong headed Today's Crap including:

______________

 

Change management language is smarmy and high falutin'

Investors grinding the CEOs tale

Big firm consultants are "dangerous company"

Nonsense briefs for teambuilding

Internal purchasers trying to spend budget

Consultant makes client feel OK about not changing

Superficial image making like cosmetic surgery

______________

 

The two main conclusions were:

______________

 

1.      We were more interested in organisational health and energy (deep, sustainable) than we were about change(superficial, transient)

2.      It may be time to "go small", shed the grandiose idea of big programmes, short, discrete projects that make a real difference, more face to face

______________

 

Having completed the research with Andy and taken this sunny day to put things in perspective, I'm now much less interested in large scale change programmes and much more aware of  the person to person dynamics that inhibit or enable change from happening.

 

One moment when this struck me was in June when Julie Bickerton, a consultant colleague and I ran a workshop in which we used a game called the House of Change. This says there are 4 stages of change, or "rooms in the house" called: Contentment, Denial, Confusion and Renewal. When we asked people to go into the 4 rooms and take on the "attitude of the room" what happened was both fun and revealing. People in each room had huge difficulty talking to people in the other rooms. Even those in the Renewal room who were trying to enthuse the others about how great the new future is, failed completed, and looked faintly ridiculous, until they got to grips with the nature of the "critical encounter", which takes us back to the research with the ten change sponsors.

 

One product of this research with Andy is our shared interest is a method called Forum Theatre (reference Boal) which plays out the "critical encounter" live with audience participation to reveal and break the grip of power. We have conducted two trials of the method so far. It begins with a scenario acted out by two volunteers (sometimes helped by actors), that in some way contains the problem: the stuckness that is happening in relation to the change programme. The audience of perhaps 30, 50, 80 people, are full participants, called spec-actors because they take actor roles. Anyone can shout "Stop!" when the encounter being played out fails the test of "would this really happen?". Having stopped the action, spect-actors say what wouldn't happen and why, and sometimes come  to take one of the roles and play out what would happen.

 

Once a "critical encounter" has been played out, in a way that everyone accepts is real or close to reality, then we explore who identifies with each player and why. This starts to reveal where the power-vulnerability-hidden oppression is located.

 

Andy can then, working alongside me or another facilitator, lead the spect-actors in working to break the oppression. Having been involved in this method, I was able to describe some of the exercises in The Voice Workout, relating to stuck conversations, confronting the bad guys, and the spontaneous shift.

 

Working on a single "critical encounter" like this has huge potential to create a shift in the minds of all the spectat-actors. The whole of the system is represented in microcosm in a single conversation. Breaking the grip of oppression through voicing the emotion and the power issues, can release the natural energy for whole system change. There are wonderful moments in such encounters when the subtext behind the words becomes exposed and the grip of the unvoiced starts to be broken:

____________

 

Cheryl (Boss, played by Alice Mallorie): "I want to know why you made that change?"

Subtext = "I am going to give you a couple of minutes to justify yourself before telling you you're wrong.."

 

Frank (Direct report played by Roddy Maude Roxby): "If you don't mind my saying…"

subtext = "I couldn't care less what you think/feel I am going to say it anyway"

____________

 

When the emotion starts to get voiced then the energy starts to shift, and instead of a pointless low energy, recirculating conversation, everyone starts to notice new possibilities

____________

 

Cheryl (Boss played by a spect-actor): "I was really angry when I heard that you had changed the service. What on earth was going on?

 

Frank (Direct report): Well, now I'm frustrated. You sent me on that creativity course. I worked with my team to come up with a real improvement for our customers and now you're angry

 

Cheryl (Boss/spect-actor): OK let's both calm down now and understand what's been going on

 

Frank (Direct report): Well, you did want me to come up with an improvement didn't you?

 

Cheryl (Boss/spect-actor): Yes, but I need to know about them. I want you to discuss them with me. I know I'm very busy and unavailable but we do have monthly meetings and memos

 

Frank (Director report): You want me to be chasing you all the time. That doesn't make me feel very good, not the others in the team We got excited about this and wanted to get on with it…

____________

 

Now the conversation is in a new territory. The underlying feelings and needs which were unvoiced and blocking are being expressed. As it is happening the whole large group of spect-actors are figuring out the rights and wrongs of this and grappling themselves with how to communicate and form effective partnerships that enable the organisation to make change. While this was being played out the spect-actors, although there were 30 of them, were bringing a special quality of rapt attention, listening to each other and making a deep connection to what was happening. Andy and I walked away on a high knowing form the way we felt and from the feedback it had been a great session!

 

Implicit in this is that change programmes sometimes force a conversation about future vision and possibility, when the reality of the present moment is that nothing is happening because of what is unvoiced, the blocking effect of undiscussable feelings and needs. When the unvoiced is voiced then the conversation moves more easily to possibility.

 

I was running along the tow path beside the river at about 9 o'clock last Saturday morning thinking I could have been having a lie-in, getting another hour of sweet, deep sleep. I was puzzled by what made me get up out of my bed and go for an 8 mile run. It's not as if I was being paid so where was the pay-off? In fact it was costing me about 90 minutes out of my Saturday morning.

 

Well I was running with a friend and felt a duty to turn up not to let him down but it wasn't just that. I knew it was possible because I'd done the run before. I enjoy the sensation of cool wind on my face, the rich visual landscape taken at a pace when you can see everything, blades of grass, spiders webs, the outline of an oak leaf, a fallen acorn, the ripples and reflections on the water rushing by. The sound of my feet in rhythm splashing through puddles.

 

So when I was lying in bed what actually drove me out onto my feet into this new state running beside the river? When you boil it all down, it was a conversation, in which the possibility arose, just floated up in front of us, and we said, OK let's do it. The possibility arose spontaneously over a drink in a bar the night before. Naturally once onto a topic like running the conversation just moves between what happening (last went for a run 2 weeks ago) and what's possible (we could do one tomorrow morning). The desire already exists in me to stretch, achieve, make a difference to my sense of health and well-being. It just needs bringing out.

 

If you're still with me, I'd like to invite you to think about such examples in your own life. Take a moment, any moment, when you find yourself doing something out of the ordinary (in other words not from habit) and ask yourself what made you do it.

 

When I do this I find myself coming back to a simple principle about change that we could call The Reality Principle. It is the antidote to complacency, resistance, inertia, denial, avoidance, and all that gets in the way of you feeling motivation and your organisation adapting. It is at the heart of the facilitator's way of leading, and is evident in Mayor Guiliano, Jack Welch and other great leaders too. This is the Reality Principle:

______________

 

Your job is to include the reality that's there but hidden:

-        the fear, the anger, the excitement, whatever the emotion

-        the quiet people

-        the question behind the question

-        the grip of power and vulnerability

-        the belly voice

 

By doing this you create the spontaneous shift, releasing  the system's natural energies, enabling the people in the system to figure out together what they need to do next.

 

Through being prepared to uncork what's there, the people in the system find energy to explore what's possible and make change happen.

That is all.

Becoming partners in a larger team

 

Aware that this might be my last day of writing, and your last day of reading this, I've awoken to another sunny October day wondering how to round things off and the word "vulnerable" is lodged in my mind. Can this be the key to a big close that makes sense of this whole adventure?

 

Why vulnerable? It's true I'm feeling incredibly vulnerable writing this: I need to know something unknowable to me in this moment: how will you react as a reader? Will it have been useful? Worth the effort? What will be the impact on you, your work and your life? Will you think better or worse of me? Will good or bad projections come in my direction, and either way will I be able to handle these without going slightly crazy? You begin to realise that during this work together we've both been putting ourselves on the line, you by reading and me by writing.

 

The vulnerable point links back to the Reality Principle and goes much wider. I'd like to explore how pervasive it may be beneath the surface of your organisation and see how facing this may the the key to building strong alliances, partnerships and teams.

 

Talking about reality is like raising the parasol, seeing more risk, creating a sense of vulnerability and fear. For example, recently I've had three friends stuck for years in jobs they hate, reluctant to talk about it and afraid to take the first step even of checking out the market. After considerable foot-dragging all have now moved, found another job. I find I'm delighted just to notice their renewed sense of themselves. They seem capable and sparky again. Fear and vulnerability are transformed into courage, a positive result and new energy.

 

Recently coaching a young, high-flying, intelligent female partner in a law firm on conducting performance appraisals, as the moment of our practice interview approached her lively, open, free-flowing conversation became stilted and she described her total flight response (adrenaline, fear, would do anything to avoid it, wishing for a phone call interrupting, wanting to get it over with as soon as possible). Her energy went down and down during the acted out conversation, with monotone voice, frowning, fiddling with pencils and lack of eye contact. Reviewing the interview she told me how dreadful she felt it was, and I asked her if she thought there was any point in doing appraisals. She surprised me by saying how important she felt it was for the partner, the firm and the appraisee; she was just having difficulty with the constraints of the form itself, and wanted a more open, flowing dialogue. As she said this she seemed to come alive again. She said she felt much better, a cloud had lifted. She found she had a choice and did not have to slavishly go through the form like a school report. We did a second practice run and it went much better. Her fear was gone. She was reconnected to her energy source. Once again fear transformed.

 

From these and other experiences I'm reminded of why this uncorking idea is important. It is the unvoiced, bottled up fear and vulnerability that cripple us, but we are often afraid to talk about our reality. As facilitators and leaders we have to get at the unvoiced and get it out, spoken, into the open. 46 years of life have taught me something about this: we're all vulnerable and fearful on some level aren't we? Underneath it all, somewhere at our centre, in our hearts, being vulnerable is what it is to be human. Behind everything we see, feel, need and request, that's what we are: vulnerable. Behind the armour of cleverness, words, expectations, demands, rules, procedures, plans, props, that's all there is: people, us, feeling vulnerable. Seeking some form of protection and defence.

 

Why is the vulnerability and fear we experience in these situations so difficult to admit to. Have we gone so far with competing for jobs, demanning, cutting out dead wood, employability, polish up your image, brand yourself Me & Co, that somehow vulnerability suggests incompetence, you're no good, a target for cost-cutting, the next one for the scrap heap? Don't vulnerability and fear unvoiced make us feel cut-off from each other, self-justifying, often deluded in our own sense of rightness, small minded in just doing our job which includes committing anti-social acts without feeling guilt. In other words making a fragmented, toxic and hostile organisation.

 

When I listen to leaders say to their people "You've got to get out of your comfort zones", I am confused, as if you are "wrong" to feel fearful and vulnerable, even when there is nothing secure coming in our direction to cling to. So then you're trapped in Catch 22: if you voice your fear you let the leader down feeling guilt and shame, and if you don't you've somehow shut yourself in a place of pretending, but not being really, authentically on board. I want to scream "It is not wrong to feel fear! It is only natural given the circumstances! There is no real security for anyone here!" Maybe that's why I am self-employed because at least the lack of security is plain and honest: there is no hiding the real stark truth of it.

 

Maybe I'm not alone here, or maybe I am. I have often been. Even going right back to the infant school playtime, I could not join Paul's or Gary's gang when invited: I wanted to stay friends with both. Since then I have never been a joiner, always drifting to the edge, or the in-between place. Curiously, alongside this is an unexpressed wish to be included and a further fear: of being rejected. When four years ago I was invited to work alongside the Centre for Tomorrow's Company to promote an "inclusive approach" to business I put my heart and lots of unpaid days into this, but ran into familiar problems over whether I could continue when this organisation promoting inclusion did not "feel" inclusive to me. I liked the message but not the medium. So I withdrew, continuing on my own, and with a newfound ambition to set up a loose community of "in-between people", facilitators and consultants, a bit like me. Through discussions with four colleagues in my learning set (Annika, Jeremy, Peter and Tina), developing a website and a sparky partnership with Fiona Coffey, a new Third Order organisation called New Intermediaries was born. Currently we have around 100 members who are holding discussions online and face to face, giving each other inspiration and support, and making sense together of this peculiar work we do.

 

My colleagues in New Intermediaries, many of whom are mentioned in this book, (some of them consultants working inside organisations but most of them self-employed or working in small consultancy firms) are a huge source of strength to me. Like we said about teams earlier, they give me a mirror, showing me that I exist, who I am, and that I belong to something larger than myself. We have various practical discussions about how you do this or that in workshops, but there is also something expansive and inspiring in our conversations, which helps me discover a purpose from which I gain courage and strength.

 

One New Intermediary colleague Richard Chapman sent me these reflections on our work as facilitators, which I feel are echoed in some way in what I have written in this book:

____________

 

I see a world-wide concern and effort for getting back into balance with ourselves (intra-personal and inter-personal) and  in our relationships with the planet. The value shift I see is in the emergence of a spirit of "peership" in counterpoint and in equal measure to the spirit of authority that has prevailed for some time.

 

A change agency profession is required because traditional approaches to change are no longer feasible. For some millennia, the principal technology of cultural change has been war and conquest. You could argue that technology of war has been quite effective. Yet there are two consequences of change imposed in this way that establish the need for our profession:

1.      Emerging polarisation with an established order holding increasingly desperately to its position

2.      Technology of war arising with breathtaking speed during the 20th century means pyrrhic victory - a war won at a price not worth paying, an unusable and unliveable planet

 

So for me the emerging profession of change agency is about global change through a myriad of incremental changes rather than through the periodic seismic  convulsions of war.

 

The technology of change agency is dialogue, which for me is about a flow of energy in the moment. Real dialogue is of course risky: there can be not control of what might emerge.

 

To my mind the green man archetype, a pre-Celtic provider of plenty, knew all about dialogue in his relationships with people, the land, the planet and beyond and held a fundamental respect, a reverence even, for all life, that all voices be acknowledged and heard as we collectively co-create our futures…

_____________

 

As Richard suggests I do feel we are poised at a point in history where we are reconsidering leadership, authority, and how to facilitate change without the costs and risks of the old model: imposing it. I agree with this and it gives me a place to stand.

 

A famous speech has been recommended to me by more than one New Intermediary colleague when they find me diffident, holding something of myself back, wondering where my passion is. When I read this, it speaks directly to a deep place in me, takes my breath away and sends shivers up and down my spine:

_____________

 

Our deepest fear is not that we are inadequate. Our deepest fear is that we are powerful beyond measure. It is our light not our darkness that most frightens us. We ask ourselves, 'Who am I to be brilliant, gorgeous, talented, fabulous?' Actually, who are you not to be? You are a child of God. Your playing small doesn't serve the world. There's nothing enlightened about shrinking so that other people won't feel insecure around you. We are all meant to shine as children do. We were born to manifest the glory of God that is within us. It's not just in some of us, it's in everyone. And as we let our own light shine, we unconsciously give other people permission to do the same. As we're liberated from our own fear, our presence automatically liberates others.

Nelson Mandela, inaugural speech

_____________

 

The difference in this speech from "get out of your comfort zones" is it seems to understand, respect and honour people and how we relate to each other. I find it trustworthy. It seems to arise not from any single religious dogma, but from a belief that we can all make a difference to one another, that in a sense we are all related, we are all part of something greater, we can confront and overcome our fears and bring out our natural energies. It makes me trust myself. I think maybe my father was trying to say this in his Holy Spirit work before he lost his way. Partly as a result of this I lost my taste for religion, but now outside of a religious context I am finding my work is about getting in touch with what some people call "spirit".

 

This "spirit" is a kind of love, different from sexual love (eros), different from parental/motherly love (blood is thicker than water). Elsewhere it has been called "phileo": delighting to be in the presence of another. It is a warm feeling you get when you are together with others, friends, colleagues, all part of a larger group. This is the genie we're letting out of the bottle. This is like a heart that pumps blood, bringing life in all human systems. Phileo is not exclusive, it crosses cultures, religions, countries, politics, public and private sector, large and small, paid and voluntary. It is what makes an organisation a place where people want to work. It is what is often forgotten in the accountants' rush to cost-reduce and in the technologists' enthusiasm to automate. This is not about going soft. We've all got a job to do, and organisations do need to be competitive and effective. I've realised phileo is a sort of lynchpin: you can get some cheap wins by crushing "spirit" but you don't get an organisation with staying power, doing its job with energy day after day.

 

I saw eleven girls shining their light last week. If you came to the park at the end of our road you would have found this group of 12 year olds blindfold, holding onto a rope walking through the mud singing at the tops of their voices in question and answer mode like American convicts in a road gang (Q: Left right left right left right left. A: Right left right left right left right). They all had big smiles on their faces. They had just  played rounders, boomerang throwing, treasure hunt, and a pogo stick contest, sheltering under a large tree through a heavy rain storm, then the sun came out, and the played some more before hooking onto the rope, put their blindfolds on, bursting into song for the final march to the local fish and chip shop.

 

Did they feel vulnerable? Yes, at first some were hesitant, but quickly the sense of fun, play, confidence came up and the human spirit came shining out of them. Their purpose was simple: to have fun. Most of them knew each other one to one but they became a team for the afternoon. They grabbed the moment, and for participants and on-lookers alike, it was just delicious to be there.

 

For those who have been to New Intermediary meetings, there has often been a special, playful, inclusive quality which people comment on. Fear does not feature strongly. We're out of our comfort zones without anyone telling us to be, because that's where we feel most alive. For some reason you don't get this feeling online. When recently there were moves amongst some in the New Intermediaries community to develop us into a company, I found myself fighting strongly, fearing we would get seriously bogged in finance and admin meetings and lose this special energy.

 

Through work with the Home Office and its stakeholders, also between accountants and their internal customers in a financial services company, and between HR people and their internal customers in a utility company, I have experienced this natural energy arising at the boundaries, when you facilitate meetings with the "outside world". There is often initial reluctance to meet: "you can't do that", "they won't want to give up the time", "it could turn into a slanging match", "it isn't a good idea". Once people from the "two sides" get in the room together the Reality Principle is used: each group gets a chance to put together their "story  with best and worst examples", then to share the stories in small mixed groups. It is in these groups that the natural energy arises, and when you bring people back to their home groups they are buzzing, feeling bonded into a larger team that includes and embraces the other group.

 

Whether we are talking one to one, small team or large system, once you notice where the boundary is, that is where a person defines "outside world", the leader and facilitator work is getting people to step up to the boundary rather than shying away, getting the Reality Principle to voice the unvoiced, then you free things up and create potential for what needs to happen.

 

This kind of example makes me let go of complex ideas and cherished beliefs about change, leadership, pacifism, facilitation or whatever. The only thing to remember, is create these conditions where people can be there, alive, in the moment, making contact, even having fun. Then they spontaneously start asking the question you wanted them to which is: What do we want to create together?

Figuring it out for yourself

 

Inspired and growing stronger by the day, using the principles described in this book I am finding my place to stand and the courage to step onto the spot, and stand for what I believe. Rather like my friends changing their jobs, and my client finding her purpose in the appraisal interview, I am transforming my fear and vulnerability into a strong shining light.

 

Writing this book has been an act of voicing for me. Maybe my father's perplexing silence was not ignorance or confusion: perhaps he knew that the only way for this facilitator was to figure out for himself how to lead.

 

In a sense, as parents at home each day, Helen and I are figuring out how to be leader and facilitator in the development of our children and our families. A lot of it we make up as we go along, and we discover our values and principles on the way, which then help us in the challenges which follow.

 

Maybe this applies to you too in becoming a leader with facilitator awareness. What I'm saying to you is: this is the end of our journey together and it's time for you to take charge of what to do next. Remember what you've uncovered for yourself.

 

I hope this has provided you with a useful lens to look at and develop your performance as a leader. I hope you found some magic here, to release the genie in yourself, your team and your organisation. I hope you will pass this magic on to others around you.

 

Beyond what I've already given, I can't figure out for you where you take this now, so thank you, it's been a pleasure and it's time for me to say goodbye.

 

Good luck!
