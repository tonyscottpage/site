Where’s the rub?

 

Getting beyond “nice” and “nasty” in a Dialogue Workshop experiment to make an executive team’s conversation more productive

____________________________________________________________

 

Autumn 2003 has afforded us the opportunity of working with several senior executive teams facing crucial business questions about strategy, operations, performance and communications. Our emerging approach enables experienced executives to make shifts in their working conversations together leading to better results.

 

Here are a few of the principles that Dialogue coaches and facilitators might use in discussion with their clients to frame future Dialogue Workshops. Examples are included from real workshops (disguised to protect the participants).

 

1. Prepare people to experiment with their conversation

Meet participants individually or run a 2-3 hour “dialogue taster” session in a workplace meeting room before the full off-site workshop. This allows everyone to enrol themselves willingly by noticing their frustrations with what is currently taking place and identifying the opportunity to be grasped. When you ask people what they want to gain from the workshop, typically executive teams say:

–         To stop being spread too thin

–         To become more efficient

–         To get on the same page, with collective responsibility

–         To become focussed and all pushing in the same direction

–         To stop working in silos

–         To free ourselves from the treacle

–         To find the right way to communicate.

Once people have expressed such intentions their minds are engaged and you have permission to help them make their conversations more productive. But how will you do this? By letting them see how they are working, helping them identify what they might need to adjust and inviting them to experiment with working differently.

 

2. Focus on the team’s current crucial business questions

One approach is to provide “open” workshops to teach diverse people dialogue techniques prior to their applying these methods in their own conversations back at work. This gives space to learn before being thrust into the hurly burly confusion of real difficult conversations, but requires time away from work, a strong motivation to learn and a dedication to apply the practice afterwards. To very senior and busy people such an approach can seem hard to justify and artificial.

 

But by tackling real business questions, no one can say “that’s all very well, but in the real world…”. The workshop is treated by everyone as immediately important, and once enabling and disabling behaviours in an intact team are revealed, this makes the challenge to existing habits legitimate and eye-opening. The experiment cannot be dismissed as a meaningless or theoretical diversion: it is the real work.

 

3. Hold up the mirror right from the beginning

The experiment begins with the very first task: setting the agenda. When you ask the team to identify the crucial questions they need to discuss in the workshop, you notice immediately the distance between team members, expressed both through “nice” and “nasty” behaviour in how they open, who talks first, who talks most, the characteristic conversational quirks and foibles, energy levels, difficulties with listening, probing, avoiding, challenging, building ideas or deciding.

 

If you give the group ten minutes to set the agenda, and insist they stop when time is up, you can choose 3 people to be a “mirror” that reflects on how the group worked, while the others listen. Ask the mirror 3 simple questions such as:

–        what did you notice about the result?

–        what did you notice about the contribution/energy level?

–        what did you notice about the behaviour which helped/hindered?

__________

 

Roger: We haven’t got an agenda yet – but we’re on the way

David: Are we being polite still and avoiding being personal, talking about individuals

Malcolm: I feel awkward talking like this us three while being observed

David: Have you noticed Paul is talking a lot while others are quiet?

__________

 

Although cautious at first the mirror will soon reveal who is dominating, who is/isn’t listening, what is being avoided, how visible everyone is to everyone else, how without necessarily intending to, people are getting in the way of one another. The facilitator can add observations and highlight dynamics for example by asking executives to line up according to how much they speak, or listen, or contribute relative to colleagues.

 

If after 5 minutes you ask the group to propose what they want to do differently to get a better result in the next 10 minutes, you can write up a couple of their points on the flipchart then ask them to continue. The impact of the first “mirror” interlude can be dramatic and the group will typically install changes immediately but also quickly run into further difficulty, perhaps with closure.

 

Having as their first task created an “agenda of crucial questions” and reflected on how the group is working, you can ask what behaviours people want to encourage and discourage in the next phase of the experiment. This provides a “contract”.

 

Often an executive team expresses a wish to get beyond “nice” and “nasty”, in other words to encourage its level of “constructive” challenge and discourage “aggression” or “being polite” and “holding back what you’re really thinking”.  They want to get to the rub.

 

4. Set up a clear and predictable rhythm

A predictable rhythm enables a growing confidence and understanding during the morning of how to make challenges constructively. The reliable structure reduces anxiety, freeing people including the facilitator to join in while also paying attention to what is happening.

__________

 

Paul: There is quite a lot of energy now. We’re getting into the stride of the format. We can probably afford to raise the level of challenge.

David: We’re disagreeing but it is less difficult now with a set structure and framework.

Geoff: We’ve already made some important decisions and laid the groundwork so we can afford to up the anti and get into some more difficult topics.

__________

 

A suitable rhythm may be 10 minutes talking followed by 5 minutes mirror, or for more complex topics 15 minutes followed by 10 minutes mirror. Much more than 15 minutes before using the mirror lets people get lost in the talking and the observers miss or forget important moments. Whether you choose 10+5 or 15+10, depending on group size and who needs “in” the talking at any time, you can share out the observer roles so that everyone eventually gets a turn to see from the outside.

 

As facilitator in this experiment you hold the time boundaries firmly and wherever possible make your own interventions sparingly without disrupting the rhythm, in order to mobilise the team’s own capacity for self-regulation. It is worth more if team members observe something themselves rather than relying on the outside facilitator to observe it for them.

 

Towards the end of each mirror slot the group take on board the learning in making their decision about what they want to do differently and where to restart the conversation.

__________

 

Roger: It started well but then it jarred and became uncomfortable. Anxiety levels rose and energy dropped. Not sure we can work effectively from here. It started jumping around between topics. David, how did you feel about Geoff stopping you after your summary?

David: It’s a hard topic. I was OK with Geoff saying no to me.

Geoff: But I’m not sure you should be OK. What I said should bother you! Do we have sufficient challenge now?

Malcolm: Paul was certainly challenged!

__________

 

By the end of a typical day in a Dialogue Workshop 6-10 talk-mirror cycles will have been accomplished.

 

5. Work in the “here and now”, not the “there and then”

The mirror phase encourages participants to pay attention directly to what is happening between them, noticing how they are impacting one another. Even if historical matters are being discussed, such as past trading performance, the “here and now” reflection reveals defences and generates a discussion of boundaries and permissions often enabling a deeper exploration of sensitive issues.

__________

 

Paul: We are meant to be looking to the future, to next year, but if that means we have to review the past to get there then that is OK. It does mean we have to go into some personnel areas and into what we do as a team.

__________

 

6. Provide simple tools to aid observation and understanding

A few simple Dialogue Tools with everyday examples give executives a common language to use in reflecting on conversations. We have previously used the Left Hand Column, Ladder of Inference, Non-Violent Communication (Edge-work), Johari Window, Transactional Analysis and others. Beware too many tools can divert people into intellectual psychobabble, while too few leave them weighed down by “hard to express” experience.

 

7. Acknowledge the emotional journey

Typically during such workshops, executives make a journey from “I” to “we”. This takes them from accepting their current way as fixed, through cautious questioning, noticing frustrations and blocks, confusion and disorientation, arriving at a personal experience of transformation (often subtle and happy in character rather than a blinding bolt of lightening) bringing an increased sense of confidence and fulfilment in the team.

 

During this journey behaviour shifts. You get better listening, more directness, less politeness and avoidance, and also less aggression and bickering and a more even level of contribution from all. People become easier with each other, achieving greater impact with less effort, finding more flexibility and support from colleagues, realising how they depend upon one another. You get more combined thinking and meeting of minds.

__________

 

David: Currently the systems project work is  in silos

Malcolm: But if Paul gets his bit wrong, then my bit won’t work

__________

 

The first signs of this loosening appear early on, and if highlighted can fuel the process, lending momentum to what follows.

__________

 

David: It feels like we might be reaching consensus. Would someone summarise?

Julie: Blah, blah…

Geoff: Yes that’s comfortable. I feel different already than when we started

  __________

 

Where the boss is present, their emotional journey is often the most difficult. Their leadership is on the line and some criticism is inevitable. At the start they sponsor and introduce the workshop, but then step down giving space for the team to experiment with different ways of working. When they don’t give sufficient space the team will often push them out of the way and claim it for themselves, criticising the boss in the process for being too dominant or controlling.

__________

 

(The boss)… is dictating the answer

(The boss)… is summarising but not really capturing what people meant

__________

 

The good news is that very soon, after 2-3 rounds with the mirror, people start to invite the boss back in, to play a somewhat different and more effective role that gives them greater impact partly because it is wanted and welcomed.

__________

 

(The boss)… is summarising regularly, but it feels different that at the beginning, more appropriate, a relief thing, phew!

__________

 

8. Get to the rub

“The rub” reflects an everyday observation that a really good conversation goes beyond pleasantries through a tense period in which you feel the rub of one person against another, releasing an emotional force, which leaves no doubt that something has changed, for better or worse.

__________

 

Julie: Come to terms with it Roger. This is company policy. You seem to be fighting it. You’re blocking us making progress

(After a break)

Roger: OK. I’m getting the point and I’m making a conscious change and moving on.

__________

 

In this middle phase, between opening and closing on any sensitive agenda topic, people are grappling with judgements and beliefs about each other’s competence and trustworthiness, and with a tendency to scapegoat, blame and “project” responsibility onto others inside or outside the team. Often blame may go to someone/something beyond reach and outside the room such as “the company” “the culture”, “the marketplace”, “the Board”, “the government”, thus giving up by reassuring everyone that “it’s impossible do anything constructive because the world is rubbish and we’re powerless”. A constructive challenge process taking place between team members wakes them up to a renewed sense of individual and collective responsibility.

__________

 

Roger: Are there engagement issues on the team?

Paul: What do you mean?

Roger: Are the trading staff engaged? Motivated?

Paul: There were issues earlier but not now

David: (suddenly pushing in) So let’s summarise, we’ve been talking about the balance of new and existing, then we have moved into some side issues including trading performance and staff motivation

Geoff: (challenging). For me your summary comes at an inappropriate time David. You broke the flow of what was happening.

Malcolm: (challenging Geoff) But you sat there and let him do it

Roger: (Impatiently) Can we get back to where we were?

Paul: Let’s put the question differently and… (reaching for the Edge-work handout)… and ask “What do you need? This raises a lot of issues…

__________

 

Surrounding the most sensitive issues the conversation can get jumpy as individuals in the group work together (sometimes unconsciously) to hide and subvert what needs to be discussed. The mirror highlights where such diversions happen and the facilitator encourages participants to complete “edgy” exchanges in which they express feelings, needs and requests. This rubbing up against one another causes people to adjust their positions and tends to release energy for decisions and action.

__________

 

Julie: I feel frustrated that Paul did not seem to care about what was happening as trading performance declined

(Roger tries to come in and Geoff, noticing Paul is ready to respond stops Roger with a hand signal)

Paul: (Responding to Julie) You should have come and said so. I was not aware you felt frustrated. Then I could either have satisfied your concerns or taken it wider..

__________

 

9. Harvest the learning

Through the repetition of several talk-mirror rounds participants start to notice a repeating 3 part pattern within every agenda topic (Open – Rub - Close) and the Dialogue Coach helps them to develop agreed upon tactics for raising the efficiency of each part.

 

At the end of a recent Dialogue Workshop participants were invited to express their learning:

–         Greater openness – far richer conversation

–         Feel more relaxed now

–         Seem more comfortable quite quickly talking about difficult issues which before the start would have taken weeks to emerge. Things are coming out on the table. Sense of making progress.

–         Collective responsibility helped this. We are all talking about it now as collective responsibility. What this means is that 6-7 more ideas are helping each person instead of oh shit… (the boss)… is  going to bullock me.

–         Leapt forward hugely. Not actually taking away individual responsibility. We’re supporting each person.

–         We are still integrated into the same business – one cannot operate without the other

–         I liked it. We have a more caring attitude amongst ourselves.

–         Review of our contract. Good. Tick. This is the standard we expect back in the office.

 

The team can also be asked to capture their Positive Vision for the team achieving results through applying its learning, and identifying their individual contributions supporting this.

 

Participants are rarely slow to realise that the benefits of this approach extend outside the team into every one of their relationships in work and outside, and sometimes into areas of personal life.

__________

 

In the weekend after the workshop my husband and kids were asking “what’s happened to you?”. I was only paying more attention to how we talk to one another and staying out of my usual habits, not shouting at them so much. When we sat down and talked about this a couple of days later it had clearly made my husband think about his own behaviour too.

__________

 

10. Follow through

A single workshop is a start but may be insufficient to install permanent changes in an executive team’s behaviour. In the first workshop participants notice their patterns and gain confidence that they can effect a shift. They may experience the greater depth of connection that makes difficult topics easier to address together. They may identify some of their own behaviours that they want to develop or shift. But the team risks sliding back into old habits.

 

Follow through meetings can be provided both to support individuals and in further sessions in the group so that the new habits take hold in the months that follow and continuous learning from the mirror can become a way of life.
