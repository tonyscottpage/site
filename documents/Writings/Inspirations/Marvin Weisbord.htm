Some Riffs on Learning OD
by Marvin Weisbord

Marv shares some of his earlier experiences on the road to becoming one of the fathers of OD. A must-read for a glimpse into this pioneer practitioner.

I became an OD consultant by accident. I left my family business in 1968 and had no idea what I would do the rest of my life. Though I had put in self-managing work teams, I thought I had mostly wasted 10 years in business. Married, unemployed, with three kids, a mortgage, a car, a cat, a dog, a bird and a turtle, I was, as they say in the OD trade, “in transition.” In late ‘68 I planned to survive by writing.

Unlike managing, I had learned writing in college. I had been a Navy journalist and taught at Penn State. By the late 60’s I had written three books and dozens of articles on politics, civil rights, and my own family, for slick magazines like Pageant, Coronet, True, Sports Illustrated, Family Circle and Parents, and for small liberal journals—the New Republic, the Reporter and the Progressive.

One day in ‘68 I got a call from a writer friend, Bernie Asbell, to join him in helping Ford Foundation’s Education and Research Division assess their school improvement grants. Ford had given millions for teaching experiments in the ‘60’s. Now they wondered what the money had bought. Our task required interviewing and report-writing. The pay was $100 a day—my first consulting fee.

I soon started researching a book based on my work team experiments and learned that I had once done “OD.” So I wrote a series of articles on that. (Years later the book emerged as Productive Workplaces.) The Conference Board asked me to profile company presidents using behavioral science to improve their firms. A New York Times Magazine assignment took me to NTL Institute and Bethel, ME, where I met Dick Beckhard, Warren Bennis, Charlie Seashore, and Alfred Marrow, author of The Practical Theorist, a biography of Kurt Lewin. Reading Lewin, I got hooked on action research both as a social change practice and a philosophy for living. I attended my first T-group.

I got by with a little help from my friends. Howard Greenwald, a college buddy, editor of IBM’s external house organ, THINK, asked me to do a summary piece on what I had learned about OD. This became one of THINK’s most widely-reprinted articles, “What, Not Again! Manage People Better?” Suddenly, I found myself on the speaker circuit. In late 1969, as a result of my work at Ford, Dave Wagner got me my first major change project with Women’s Medical College of Pennsylvania. Don Kirchhoffer brought me into a big food service company.

In the spring of 1970 I joined a group planning the National OD Network meeting in Philadelphia. There I joined a session led by Peter Block and Tony Petrella, who had left ESSO Research and Engineering to start a consulting firm. I was struck by their practicality and empathy for managers. I asked them for help and, having supplied the necessary client, soon learned from Peter and Tony the process side of consulting. We were then among a handful of self-employed OD’ers, the second generation forging a radical form of consultancy.

Peter, Tony and I merged our firms in 1974. In 1980, we renamed the company Block Petrella Weisbord and spun off Designed Learning as a training business run by Peter and our partner Jim Maselko. Tony and I oversaw the consulting business. (I almost wrote “managed,” but in truth we liked doing the work more than managing it!) We never figured out how to market process consulting and action research.

We held that our purpose was making clients independent, so skillful at “learning how to learn” they would (not too soon, we hoped) no longer need us. My favorite referral was elicited by Frank Warland, for 15 years CEO of Atomic Energy of Canada’s Medical Products Division, which company we helped save in 1985. Before hiring us, Frank phoned our references. “They get fantastic results,” said one VP. “Only you have to understand they don’t do anything. They make you do it all yourself!”

Always we were caught in the paradox that humane processes don’t always correlate with quarterly dividends, especially in corporate giants having little control of markets. In 1982, for example, Bethlehem Steel Corporation asked us to help them solve critical leadership problems, the disastrous residue of past solutions. It seems that Frederick Taylor, father of scientific management, had preceded us in Bethlehem by 80 years.

Taylor had a contract most OD people would (secretly) envy. If managers resisted his system, he had them fired. Between 1898 and 1901 he transformed Bethlehem and helped a generation of immigrants earn a living wage. Eventually the managers whose authority he usurped rebelled and pushed him out—an object lesson for all hired guns whose passion for betterment exceeds the client’s.

Combining Lewinian action research and process skill training, we enabled Bethlehem to take off the straight jacket of time and motion study and debilitating labor-management conflict. We implemented team-based quality improvement. We established a climate of consultation between labor and management. In 1982, during a two-week maintenance shutdown, we pioneered the “whole system in the room” with 80 people from five levels in the Sparrows Point Plate Mill. It became a world class mill.

Following Taylor’s innovations the company had grown for nearly 80 years. Ours, alas, lasted fewer than 20. Bethlehem downsized from eleven plants to two, and most of the people we worked with retired or lost their jobs. In 2001 the firm filed for bankruptcy protection.

My favorite “Peanuts” cartoon has Good Ole Charlie Brown saying, “I wish I was four years old again knowing what I know now.” Would I do Bethlehem again knowing what I know now? Sure. I would do it again knowing full well that “outcomes” in a sea of non-stop change are as elusive as butterflies.

I would do it again because (a) dialogue and inquiry are good practices, whatever the outcome, (b) OD consultants symbolize hope and people need to find hope to get through, and (c) it is existentially right to enable cooperation across boundaries. In a species such as ours, chock full of demons, we should count it a good day’s work when we succeed in evoking, even briefly, a tiny glimmer of courage and camaraderie.

Marvin Weisbord is currently Co-Director of Future Search Network, a non-profit agency providing planning services worldwide for whatever people can afford. He can be reached at: mweisbord@futuresearch.net
