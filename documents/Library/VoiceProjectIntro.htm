VOICE PROJECT – Introduction

____________________________

 

 

When I first started to remark to others that the concept of Voice provided a key to good facilitation, good leadership and creating healthy organisations, I experienced little reaction followed by a mild feeling of madness. Was I being brushed aside because the idea was obvious? Had someone already said it? Was I mistaken? Was the idea dangerously subversive? Disbelieving that on this subject of all subjects I was not being heard, I felt some pain and embarrassment: this was touching something deep in me. If I felt this maybe others without a voice did too: all the evidence of my work in teams and organizations, my personal experience of being in groups and the more disturbing events in the world confirmed this. If I could see it and feel it so strongly, then why would others not join me in acknowledging its importance. The topic became raw for me like a wound: something I was constantly aware of, that guided me in my facilitation, but largely hidden from the world.

 

Holding on, in spite of little obvious support, to the truth and power of the Voice idea, I would return repeatedly to it in conversation but to no avail. For an introverted second child reared in a noisy family of four, this was proving a beast of a lesson. A familiar, upset, indignant inner voice screamed at me: "How loud do I have to shout before someone will listen?"

 

One morning early in Sept 2001 the idea returned in a different form: a poem of sorts (“Don't try to shut me up!”) that appeared in semi-wakefulness. Not being given to writing poems, I got up, wrote it down eagerly. It was angry and it felt great to get the strong words out of me and onto paper. I published it online to others in my network and got some immediate recognition for the emotions expressed. This was more like it! I felt reacted to and understood. Maybe that would be enough to lay this one to rest and move me on, but no! I was still so haunted by it that when the twin towers were destroyed the following week, my deranged mind knew in an instant why: like other problems in the world we could see this in terms of Voice!

 

So crushing was this event and so compelling was this insight, the space it created in my work diary was filled over coming weeks with a stream of writings, poems, exercises and insights attempting to articulate just how “heightened Voice awareness” could show facilitators and leaders which direction to take towards change, energy, growth, health, success. It was almost a book already. But it appears I still had not learned my lesson fully as publishers turned me down or ignored me and for 12 months the manuscript gathered dust on my shelf. My Voice had got lost again, out but not making its impact, like a parcel of goodies lost in the sorting office.

____________________________

 

Today, more than a year later the Voice idea is in me still, as it has been since my childhood. I find myself asking why don’t others get it in the gut like I do? Come to think of it this is a familiar question for anyone who fails to communicate.  Do I suffer a deeper scar in this place than other people? Was writing this just a personal catharsis for me or really offering something of value to others? Have I not explained in enough detail how this is exciting, dangerous and relevant? Should I use more examples? Should the core idea be more clearly put? What is your true reaction to this material (since it is not the one that I expected)?

 

Then last week, a colleague was giving me a career coaching session as we walked round the gardens of Hampton Court Palace, and I confided that more and more I am drawn to do work that “makes my heart sing”. He asked permission to “try something” and I warily consented. This led in the next couple of minutes to my standing in some trees back to the maze, face to the palace, hand on my heart. I was to sing something, a few notes, anything. And I stood there for a long time silent, singing nothing, with a busy inner dialogue that seemed to strip bare my defences, revealing my inner workings. What if someone heard me? What if I disturbed someone’s peaceful enjoyment of this place? Was I prepared to sing something quietly so not to be heard? What right did I have to push my noise onto people like this? Omigod, isn’t this how I operate? Don’t I tend to work within another’s expectations rather than disturbing them or challenging them in the face? What if I did sing here? Would that be a sign to myself that I can step across a new threshold and reclaim my power? What should I sing? Did I know any songs? What was suitable here? Stupid – how could anything be “suitable” here of all places?

 

So it went on, endlessly, and I stood there petrified with a crippling feeling of self-consciousness. Fortunately no one had walked right past me yet and given me one of those looks that asked what on earth I was doing. And eventually something moved in me deciding not to fail at this. I took a deep breath, readied myself and produced a single, clear, strong, long note which I vividly remember imprinted in me today (I checked on the piano just now and it’s the F# below middle C on our piano). The instant my voice ended, my legs bolted into action propelling me several yards to my right, away from the spot, hidden from the gaze of any finger-pointing passers-by. Then my colleague asked me to go back to the spot, replace my right hand on my heart and report what I was feeling.

 

I noticed my heart beating like fury, and reported a mixture of excitement and fear. Where did I feel the excitement and where the fear? Excitement easy – in my heart. Fear – not so sure. It was fear that engaged my legs a moment previously. I could not really feel the fear but felt it was probably in my stomach. Then I had to place my left hand on my stomach, keeping my right hand on my heart and stay in touch with both my excitement and my fear. Bah boom, bah boom. Heart still rushing. Acute self consciousness. Wanting to escape, hide, die. 

 

Later I asked my colleague why he had set me this particular task and he reported noticing me getting distant, going into my head, as I described my career ambition, and wanting me to stay present, in the moment, in the power of now.

____________________________

 

All in all, the Hampton Court experience disturbed me deeply, confronting me with my own defensiveness, exposing me, making me feel weak, and “making visceral” some learning that I had already completed in my head. From this I harvest insights into the Voice issue. Most importantly I realise I do still want to publish the Voice idea even if it needs some re-working, and that doing so not only represents a completion of this piece of personal learning, but an offering from an experienced facilitator to others who want to connect with their fuller power as facilitators and leaders in organisations.

 

In the ebb and flow of a group’s life those who entertain and contribute freely are welcomed (or at least tolerated) and so are the others who contribute at lower volumes. With a crisis looming, or a big decision to make, or under stress, group members fall back into their familiar habits of speaking more or less. Apparently the loud get louder and the quiet get quieter, but look inside the quiet people and you notice their Inner Voices are also screaming louder. A tension is building that resolves itself only through the Inner Voices coming out and being received by the other people who are filling the air space. As dominant members start to drive towards closure, “hot conflict” develops and the Inner Voices burst in and get included. When this process gets arrested by those fearful of conflict or for whatever reason, you get “cold conflict”, that is a disconnection between Outer and Inner Voice. This is when the facilitator is called. And what is their work? To draw out each person’s voice. To let voices react to and find their place alongside other voices. To engender true participation. 

 

Voice: a simple word, familiar, trite, self explanatory, so why would an experienced facilitator who has worked in so many organizations for so many years find it so compelling?  This book is about a special quality of voice that you can find in yourself and in others. When this quality is there the communication is compelling and demands attention. When this Inner-Outer Voice connection exists what is spoken is full, authentic and powerful. When a team or organisation makes a habit of this kind of communication then it is successful and strong. When a leader says they want people’s hearts and minds, this is what they are reaching towards.

 

Like many introverts I am competent and at times skilful and insightful in my dealing with others. My job is talking to leaders and teams who work in organisations: hotel staff, civil servants, managers, technologists, nurses, teachers, doctors... I notice frustrations people have and how often the conversation turns to “the need for change”, which now I regard as a popular code for “we’re not succeeding in our communication round here”. None of us can easily see what is happening: there is a lot of dust kicked up into eyes, by past failed change programmes, false claims, undiscussable issues, buried emotion and broken promises. Often people are able to rally round the banner of “change” colluding by not vocalising the difficult questions of what would be “better than this” and for whom. I am interesting in change that is motivated, meaningful and real and my experience is that heartfelt conversation creates this, through Voice engaging authentically with Voice.

 

So when I failed to get the Voice idea listened to was I being brushed aside because the idea was obvious? Had someone already said it? Was I mistaken? No. No. No. But I had failed to express the idea fully. I had failed to uncover really why it was important to me, why it was relevant as a gift to others who have a different make up, or how it plays itself out in organisations.

 

Was the idea dangerously subversive? Well yes, I believe it is hot, risky and challenging, but I also believe we have to go there. When the idea is fully taken on it not only challenges each of us to be true to ourselves, it challenges you as a leader, to allow a comfortable mould to be broken, to grow a new climate of transparency and honesty, in which so much that is established but not working (how about dependency, avoidance, undiscussables, emotion, power, habits, blockages…?) may be challenged directly and transformed.

 

Now I’ve set the record straight, please explore the diverse materials that have emerged in this voice project and don’t hesitate please to offer up your reactions.
