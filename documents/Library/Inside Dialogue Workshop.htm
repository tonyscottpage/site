Inside The Dialogue Workshop

 

This paper is offered as a general positioning and introduction to the Dialogue Workshop.

_____________________________________________________________________

 

One of the great enduring questions which we each face at some point in our lives is “Why do you come to work?”, and its close relation “What does work have to be like to make it truly fulfilling?”. Perhaps in a past industrial age such questions might be philosophical or self-indulgent, but for those of us engaged in a cutting-edge knowledge enterprise, in which our work requires the highest levels of personal motivation, creativity and intelligence, not to mention teamwork and coordination, these questions run to the core of what makes us and our organisations successful.

 

Henry Ford famously invited his workers to leave their brains in the locker room in order not to mess up a standardised production machine. But today, given the huge pressure to compete on a global scale, to segment, add value, customise, make step changes in performance, and survive unprecedented market turbulence, we need to constantly keep our wits about us, finding ways to dig deeply into our collective experience base in a team. We need efficiently to achieve a shared “take” on what is happening in the market, and to generate uniquely creative responses which enable sustained delivery of results while competing for necessary resources.

 

What this amounts to is unless you continue to find better ways of thinking, learning and creating together your team risks falling behind the pack and being replaced by others more adept.

 

It is in this context that I am proposing a Dialogue Workshop as a regular practice for intact teams. We will go on to look at what this means, and how it might work for you in the teams you lead and serve. We will see through case examples how the Dialogue Workshop can unravel all sorts of knots that restrict a team’s performance including:

–        the undiscussable trading loss

–        the dreaded repeating head to head conflict

–        the MD who falls asleep

–        ghosts from the past that prevent a team moving on….

….and many more!

 

I am asking you what if… what if… we could learn to use workshops really well… what if your team and every team could start to really see and take charge of its own behaviour, to become adept at finding blocks and removing them before things turn sour… what if team members were able to engage differently, more awarely, responsibly and less defensively will each other… what if we found a way easily, without deep therapy or advanced facilitation to work beneath the surface with the mostly unspoken experiences and assumptions that shape a team’s results. What then? Might that be something useful? And could we make this really accessible, jargon free and doable?

____________________

 

Before moving forward in this, it will benefit us to spend a few moments glancing back. Through the ages work has meant very different things, from hunting animals, through gathering berries, sowing and reaping crops, weaving in the home, making deliveries on pack horses to markets, and, after the industrial revolution, operating machines in a factory. It is easy to forget these origins as more recently the idea of work and workplace (as against leisure) has shifted with the emergence of white collar office and managerial jobs, the retail and leisure boom, telephone “work” in new call centres, the vast new range of highly technical/specialist jobs and most recently the “work from anywhere” freedom promised by the information super-highway. Let’s also remember that for many of us our first childhood experience of the “work” word was home-“work”,  that lonely weight you’re saddled with and would prefer to escape.

 

Sometime way back in this long evolution the word “workshop” arose presumably to mean a place where people gathered to work together, perhaps in an early village forge making ploughs and hoes, or as a place where people repair machinery, or as another name for a factory. More recently the word “workshop” has been corrupted, pulled apart from its industrial roots and dragged into our turn of the century global organisations to mean something else: a one-off event not a regular place of work, implying something strange, unnecessary, temporary and slightly artificial, a training course or indoctrination, to be avoided if at all possible, a “pretend” work that drags you from your desks, screens and phones into meeting rooms and hotel suites away from the office.

 

Well if that brings us up to date more or less, we can see quite vividly how words evolve, and how when I now introduce the term “Dialogue Workshop for Intact Teams” every single word and the combination suggested by the five words together is hugely ambiguous and open to interpretation. Today’s popular usage of “workshop” is not helping us, and the earlier form implying “a place where the really tough, gritty and useful work gets done”, is what we’re about.

 

We could, but need not, take a similar journey to trace the evolution of the word  “dialogue”. For now let us take it to mean “a flow of meaning between people leading to a meeting of minds”. Far from being a luxury, or an indulgence this concept of dialogue is a core business practice needed to achieve the shared “take” that is the basis for thinking, learning and creating together which earlier we indicated was crucial for survival and sustainable delivery in our turbulent global environments.

 

Having reflected on our use of terms, the questions we now face are less about why do dialogue workshops: we have seen that such workshops address a clear business imperative. So we turn to how to do them including what are they like exactly, how to avoid the pitfalls, how to conduct them well, who needs them, how often.

 

Last summer I was working on a leadership programme with 120 of the top 1200 managers in an international bank in Sao Paulo, Brazil. One of the activities at the end of a two day workshop was to bring two teams (manager plus direct reports) together. One team sat in a circle facing in, the other team sat  in a larger circle round them. The inner team were invited to decide on the focus they were taking away from the workshop, prior to drawing up their specific action plan. While the inner team was given ten minutes for this discussion, the outer team was to observe in silence and when time was called give feedback. Since the outer team came from a different part of the world and a different financial specialism they had little to contribute in a technical sense and as we had spent two days working on  coaching skills (including listening, questioning, summarising, challenging, supporting, clarifying, time-out, review) it was natural for them to focus on the behaviour they observed. The interesting part of this for me was the impact of the outer team’s comments. When the inner team restarted they were able to take-on-board comments made, adjusting their behaviour to achieve greater engagement, more depth, a higher energy level and a more efficient discussion process. We called this exercise a “fishbowl”.

 

This Brazil experience was a building block towards the Dialogue Workshop. My learning from this matched up with other experience of “fishbowls” that my client, Malcolm Tulloch, in an international oil and gas Company, had gained in a rather innovative leadership programme he had delivered in Boston, Amsterdam and Singapore in recent months. So when Malcolm introduced me to Bill Adamson, one of his Asset General Managers to talk about some kind of dialogue training, it was not surprising that “fishbowls” became part of our approach.

 

During Bill’s previous job in charge of the company’s operation in Argentina, he had wanted to move away from a command and control culture, take people out of their comfort zones into being more engaging, responsive, flexible. During a “dialogue coaching” workshop he attended there were various “blinding flash moments” in which Bill gained fresh insight into difficulties with his father, learned to let go of emotional baggage and to stay aligned in a conversation by stepping out of it, reflecting and flagging the conversation you want to have. By the time he left Argentina half of his local workforce of 5000 had received training in dialogue coaching. Bill did not feel the need to justify the business or personal benefits of this training: to him it was just obvious. So I asked him why he was proposing to offer dialogue training to his new team in the UK. He said:

 

“In spite of all we’re saying about becoming less hierarchical, we haven’t really got it. People revert quickly under pressure. Every Tuesday in our “no surprises” meeting you can see people are wondering what am I doing here. They turn up like planets colliding briefly then go off for weeks and months doing their own thing. They’re happiest when drawing up a list of actions on the flipchart, and have little interest in putting issues on the table or exploring the way we are thinking. It is far from a meeting of minds.”

 

What followed over coming months were a series of experiments with Bill and his direct reports, including further workshops in some of their teams. We tried out and developed tools to help a team see and get a grip of itself. We practised ways to unblock the blocked conversation, to include everyone, to open a dialogue, to put issues on the table, to get to the nub of things, to move things on to a decision, to get closure.

 

When we gathered towards the end of November for a Dialogue Workshop in Bill’s Trading Team, I checked cautiously at the beginning whether there was an appetite for what we were about to do, fearing that the group were simply falling in with their boss’s wish to hold this event. The eight managers surprised me by saying the workshop promised certain valuable possibilities:

–        To stop being spread too thin

–        To become more efficient

–        To get on the same page, with collective responsibility

–        To become focussed and all pushing in the same direction

–        To stop working in silos

–        To free ourselves from the treacle

–        To find the right way to communicate

 

To find out what happened in this and other recent workshops look at the case examples .

 

In summary, the central feature of a Dialogue Workshop is that a team sets its own agenda, thus making sure it is talking about something of real importance to all those present. While working on this chosen topic the team looks at and takes responsibility for how it is working, usually with some help from an external facilitator. As a result of these efforts to focus both on the “what” and the “how”, communication becomes unusually direct, clear and complete. By contributing your piece of the jigsaw and learning about how this fits with your colleagues you can create a meeting of minds.

 

Typically participants become excited at first and as they get into the groove they slow down, listen better, open up, delve deeper, take more risks, put the real issues on the table, challenge one another, respect and support one another through certain difficult moments, become aligned, take responsibility, take decisions which are robust and supported afterwards, increase their sense of confidence and become more capable of handling future challenges.

 

If that sounds amazing it is worth remembering that few Dialogue Workshops are entirely comfortable or quite so perfect. Yes there are magical moments when blocks seem to shift but they are not a magic cure-all. More often than not issues are raised that need further work. Since we are aiming to deal with the more difficult stuff that is avoided and brushed under the carpet in conventional meetings, we need time and  protection from interruptions. For example, after a very successful off-site overnight workshop in the Trading team, just over a month later a day spent together on-site was marred by constant interruptions, telephones, popping upstairs to check on the trading floor, and little new progress was made.

 

The key facilitator intervention in a Dialogue Workshop is to call a “time-out” which enables participants to talk briefly about how they are behaving and experiencing the meeting. Beyond this a range of possibilities exist and we are alive to the advantages of live experiment over rigid procedure or dry theory. For example an Investment Appraisal team was interested to explore how to deal with problems that arise in meetings they chair. We were able to bring actors into the room to play out a head to head conflict, giving team members a chance to try out different options and learn about what works best for them to move things on.

 

When the MD in a team session I was facilitating fell asleep we had no choice but to experiment. OK it could have happened to any of us: the room was hot, the chairs a little to comfy, it was after lunch, for personal reasons he was dog-tired, it only lasted a few minutes and when he came to he realised and apologised. You might wonder what’s the big deal. Well the damage to his tight-knit team was huge. When he awoke  and said sorry people were unsure how to respond so no one said much, we let some cool air into the room and the incident appeared to be over. But as work continued progress was slow, heads were down and over dinner it became clear the aftermath was serious. When the MD went to bed the rest of the team became mutinous, wanting to go home and end the workshop. His falling asleep had touched a raw nerve: many suspected the MD was drifting, disengaged and blamed him for the company’s recent poor performance.

 

The following morning there was no clear way forward without coming back to the issue, but to do so risked descent into blame, embarrassment and mud-slinging. So we began a live experiment in truth talking. An “open forum” allowed everyone to speak from the heart and sure enough the issue came to the surface. It took around three hours to work through but coming to terms with the MD falling asleep delivered a wider benefit by raising the core issue of why performance had been slipping, and the way this was sapping motivation. As people expressed these feelings the mood started to lift and around 11am they  reported feeling better and ready to move on.

 

Often Dialogue Workshops produce really positive outcomes, for example, here is the learning reported in a closing session with one senior executive team that was experimenting with coaching:

–        Very powerful 2 days

–        Very interesting teambuilding/bonding

–        We have to put our energy into gaining closure

–        We must clarify the question before we dive into a topic

–        In reaching closure – sub-steps are useful on a larger issue

–        Mustn’t make each other afraid to intervene

–        Set realistic agendas.

–        We need to be on the same agenda

–        People do hide their concerns and we need to get concerns on the table

–        Having an external observer means you can’t hide

–        We do need an external observer

–        Thumbs up = positive affirmation of closure

–        When we have closed something don’t keep unlocking the box again

 

How does a Dialogue Workshop produce such positive results? This is difficult to say. I can only speculate. There is no clear explanation but I believe the value arises from its being different that run of the mill meetings in the workplace. What is the key difference? Possibly it is the level of honesty. Typically people avoid, both by being nice, and at times by being nasty. Both tendencies represent a kind of cover-up. But in the Dialogue Workshop people quickly realise that almost everything is noticed. There really is nowhere to hide an issue without someone picking up on tell-tale behaviour and the loss of energy. But don’t assume this creates a harsh atmosphere in which people feel exposed: rather the opposite happens. There are generally such high levels of respect and support that people freely open up and freely bring hidden issues to the table. Table? That is another point of difference. We usually work without a table and without back-up papers, simply speaking in a circle of chairs. Importantly we work off-site where possible and remove distractions to make deep concentration possible. Taking all these differences together it is apparent that we are engaging in something deeply human, leaving behind some familiar trappings and rituals of organisational life.

 

Usually we work with an intact executive team, that is a boss and direct reports who between them are responsible for a chunk of the business, but for the duration of the meeting we are asking them to be themselves, step outside their formal roles, to take collective responsibility for the performance of their business unit and their team and to be open to what emerges. Often this entails some reworking of the boss’s role in relation to others, with direct reports reclaiming some of their own authority from the boss and finding a new and more effective way of engaging together afterwards. If the boss is up for it, this delivers them the big benefits of releasing energy, passing greater responsibility out to others, and lightening their load.

 

When we come back to our opening questions of “Why do you come to work?” and “What does work have to be like to make it truly fulfilling?”, perhaps in the Dialogue Workshop we are beginning to find some answers. The workshops themselves appear to be fulfilling, perhaps addressing some unacknowledged needs. Maybe in daily worklife we take on big expectations, fitting ourselves into corporate priorities and demands, managing how we appear, demonstrating competence, being focused while pushing to one side our deeper feelings, needs and our more off-the-wall ideas. We shut ourselves off from inner and outer distractions in one big effort to deliver. Under this kind of pressure we become less human, more driven and our teams fragment. Whilst sustainable for short periods this single-minded approach is doomed to fail. The Dialogue Workshop offers time to reconnect. People open their eyes, their ears and their hearts. They are honest about what they notice. They are authentic, true to themselves while respecting their colleagues’ honesty. This needed change releases passion, energy and creativity. The whole can become more than the parts.

 

Last year in my own midlife transition following the recent death of my parents, I lost motivation. As a freelancer I gave myself permission to wallow around for a time, digging foundations for a house extension, building a wall, exploring avenues for doing more voluntary work. Work had lost its sparkle for a time but coming out of this phase was a personal resolve to do only the work that makes my heart sing.

 

Now with this period behind me I create spaces for myself  as an independent to meet and untangle. For example with five colleagues, like a surrogate family, I meet in a learning set for half a day every 4-6 weeks. On each project I am a member of a wider team and if it fails to meet for reviews from time to time the work starts to seem pointless and shallow. Perhaps in offering Dialogue Workshops I am acknowledging a similar need in others to find meaning in their work. We all need this don’t we?  In this paper I’m also proposing that our organisations need us to find this meaning too, giving us strength and resourcefulness to remain at the cutting edge of our knowledge work. And on a very practical level you walk away from a Dialogue Workshop with renewed focus, clear priorities, needed permissions from colleagues and agreed actions.

 

As Bill says, the value is so obvious there is really no need to justify it.

 

Tony Page

March 2004
