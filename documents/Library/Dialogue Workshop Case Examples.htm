1. The team fishbowl in an international bank

 

______________________________________________

 

The Setting

Hilton Hotel, Sao Paulo. 120 top managers attending a leadership development workshop in one large meeting room sitting in teams (senior manager plus direct reports) roughly 10 per table. They are aiming to develop their coaching skills.

 

What happened?

Towards the end of a two day workshop we paired each team up with another of similar size, but from different specialisms (eg, retail banking team with wholesale team), and often from different countries. One team sat in a circle facing in, the other team sat  in a larger circle round them. The inner team were invited to decide on the focus they were taking away from the workshop, prior to drawing up their specific action plan. While the inner team was given ten minutes for this discussion, the outer team was to observe in silence and when time was called give feedback.

 

Since the outer team’s background and specialism was different they had little to contribute in a technical sense and as we had spent two days working on coaching skills (including listening, questioning, summarising, challenging, supporting, clarifying, time-out, review) it was natural for them to focus on the behaviour they observed.

 

The outcome

The interesting part of this for me was the impact of the outer team’s comments. When the inner team restarted they were able to take-on-board comments made, adjusting their behaviour to achieve greater engagement, more depth, a higher energy level and a more efficient discussion process.

 

Some lessons

We called this exercise a “fishbowl”. The experience was a building block towards the out later experiments in the Dialogue Workshop. My learning from this matched up with other experience of “fishbowls” that my client, Malcolm Tulloch, in an international oil and gas Company, had gained in a rather innovative leadership programme he had delivered in Boston, Amsterdam and Singapore in recent months.

2. The undiscussable loss in the trading team

______________________________________________

 

The setting

A hotel in the Thames Valley. Seven managers responsible for trading gather after a difficult year ending with a loss of over a million pounds sterling. There are three subgroups: 4 managers from “Front office” in charge of daily trading, 2 from “Middle office” in charge of risk management and work processes and 1 from “Back office” in charge of accounting.

 

Their weekly meetings seem to achieve needed decisions related to everyday operations but it is not clear how to get a grip of what needs to change.

 

Short pre-meetings with each individual revealed a reluctance to blame or scapegoat any one individual and a marked tendency to avoid discussion of the issue. Each person was asked to come up with two or three topics they felt were important to discuss in the team and we gathered at 6pm one evening at a nearby hotel .

 

The opening

The team assembled at 6pm and were asked to spend 10 minutes coming up with an agreed agenda of three important items to discuss the following day. Immediately their patterns of behaviour, communication and decision-making were revealed: a few people were doing all the talking, there was little obvious listening, when the boss tried to make a decision others resisted and come in from new angles. When ten minutes were up the team was nowhere near closure.

 

In the next five minutes I asked three members of the team to reflect on their experience of the last 10 minutes asking them about the results, energy levels, contributions. Reluctantly at first they began to speak in front of their colleagues about what had happened, and it was clear that they had seen all I had seen and much more. Without being invited to reflect and comment, much of what had happened would have remained un-discussed and unacknowledged.

 

The team were then given a further 10 minutes to complete the task. Almost spontaneously they made some adjustments, started listening better and incorporating each others ideas. By the end of the time they had made some progress but still not produced a result. In a further five minutes reflection a different trio noticed the team’s particular difficulties with closure. And when the team restarted they agreed upon a way of closing which involved a bar on last minute ideas and clear signals of agreement. Well within 10 minutes they produced their agreed agenda and displayed in their faces a huge sense of achievement.

 

The rub

During the course of the day the trading team developed agreed upon techniques for navigating their way through difficult topics, first opening each topic out, putting issues on the table, working through rather than avoiding tension, leading to agreed upon conclusions and actions. Basic principles of good communication and teamworking were being explored and enacted at the same time as the team was developing its strategy.

 

Their appetite for risk and their confidence to address sensitive issues increased as the day went on:

 

Paul: There is quite a lot of energy now. We’re getting into the stride of the format. We can probably afford to raise the level of challenge.

David: We’re disagreeing but it is less difficult now with a set structure and framework.

Geoff: We’ve already made some important decisions and laid the groundwork so we can afford to up the anti and get into some more difficult topics.

 

The team’s awareness became heightened and they were able to become more candid and challenging to one another:

 

Roger: It started well but then it jarred and became uncomfortable. Anxiety levels rose and energy dropped. Not sure we can work effectively from here. It started jumping around between topics. David, how did you feel about Geoff stopping you after your summary?

David: It’s a hard topic. I was OK with Geoff saying no to me.

Geoff: But I’m not sure you should be OK. What I said should bother you! Do we have sufficient challenge now?

Malcolm: Paul was certainly challenged!

 

As the day advanced the concentration seemed to deepen. By the middle of the afternoon the team began to broach really sensitive issues:

 

Julie: I feel frustrated that Paul did not seem to care about what was happening as trading performance declined

Paul: (Responding to Julie) You should have come and said so. I was not aware you felt frustrated. Then I could either have satisfied your concerns or taken it wider..

Yes I was concerned and I did care but maybe that did not show. When results are going that way in a shit market and then when the market moved we were on the wrong side of it… Did I get the support I needed? Yes and no. We had a big money loss and we were asked to do a fundamental analysis… and this is against a background of big change in the wider company so we don’t know how it will be perceived by others. Maybe our bosses are feeling about the whole trading operation the way you’re feeling about me.

Geoff: Yes, that’s right. We asked the company to put their money where their mouth is and agree to trading futures then when we do it backfires like this.

Julie: (Looking puzzled) Now I’m concerned, about your placing these equity positions – I was not part of that! In fact I slightly resent this. I was not consulted and I feel cut out of a process.

Geoff: We should not need a process. This amounts to a failure of all of us to work as a team.

Julie: Hang on! This should be the subject of discussions on our group of 7.

Geoff: And to date we’ve had all one to ones, no decisions made collectively

Julie: Yes it’s the old problem of us working in silos.

 

This was a moment in which something previously hidden, a blame held privately was voiced in the whole team, getting it off the speaker’s chest, freeing something up as it  elicited a response. Soon permissions were being offered and taken up to explore the sensitive issues directly albeit with some awkwardness:

 

Roger: Are there engagement issues on the team?

Paul: What do you mean?

Roger: Are the trading staff engaged? Motivated?

Paul: There were issues earlier but not now

Roger: The weekly meetings we did have were more demotivating than constructive. There were analysis opportunities… but some individuals feel they have given a lot of input and are not happy… the energy level on the whole floor is an issue.

David: It’s focus. Some are focused and some aren’t and there is not a common focus.

Paul: Can you name names?

David: Yes A and B can be focused but C and D are de-motivated. This means that they are not feeding off each other and electricity is not being generated between them.

Julie: Yes, the trading team feeds the rest of the floor.

 

The outcome

By the end of the day the instead of producing scapegoats the workshop had brought people together into a shared responsibility for trading performance. Comments included:

–         Greater openness – far richer conversation

–         Feel more relaxed now

–         Seem more comfortable quite quickly talking about difficult issues which before the start would have taken weeks to emerge. Things are coming out on the table. Sense of making progress.

–         Collective responsibility helped this. We are all talking about it now as collective responsibility. What this means is that 6-7 more ideas are helping each person instead of oh shit… (the boss)… is  going to bullock me.

–         Leapt forward hugely. Not actually taking away individual responsibility. We’re supporting each person.

–         We are still integrated into the same business – one cannot operate without the other

–         I liked it. We have a more caring attitude amongst ourselves.

 

Six weeks later when we worked together again the dialogue was less auspicious. This time the the team met on site, during a day when the markets were moving dramatically, raising the stakes by offering big opportunities to make profit and great risks of traders without guidance from their managers making losses. This provided a huge distraction and led to phone calls and rushing back to the trading floor during the breaks. We were a slightly different group with one new member, and one person absent on holiday. In the face of company pressure to improve performance significant doubts were emerging over the judgement of one individual in the team. The day was inconclusive: we seemed to lack concentration and made little progress.

 

Some lessons

In the first evening the simple practice of insisting the team alternates rhythmically between “what” (discussion of the topic in hand) and “how” (reflection on how the discussion is happening) had enabled the team to “see” itself and make needed adjustments in its way of working which had produced a clearly visible result. Was real work being done here? Yes – they were identifying the three most important strategic questions to address. Was it being done efficiently? Yes, within 30-40 minutes there was agreement. Was dialogue present and necessary here? There undoubtedly a flow of meaning and a meeting of minds. The work produced a clear and robust agreement on three items to be addressed the following day. This provided a foundation for work the following day and a needed boost to the team’s confidence.

 

The following day’s workshop was a fine illustration of the potential a team has to get a grip of itself. The onsite meeting one month later was hugely disappointing but showed vividly the conditions we need to create (eg. have the right people in the room, minimise distractions, get the pressing issues onto the table, allow sufficient time to work the issues thoroughly...) to make Dialogue Workshops really valuable.

3. The MD who fell asleep

______________________________________________

 

The setting

It was December. The year’s performance had been poor following an outstanding results the previous year. The new MD had arrived just over 2 years ago and had hit the ground running, getting round the world and meeting everyone, holding strategy meetings, introducing new advertising and programmes to raise the energy and lift the brand. He had made it fun by breaking rules taking the top team to new places including posh hotels in Belfast, Dublin, a chateau outside Paris and a camping weekend in Center Parcs. We were now meeting in the Lake District. The MD was disappointed, felt let down by his team and was also hitting personal problems in the breakdown of his marriage, and had been working from home a lot. At the workshop he expected everyone to push harder to raise the company’s performance.

 

What happened?

When the MD fell asleep in the session after lunch no one knew what to do. While the discussion continued there were nudges and signals, eye contact was being used to pass the message round. OK it could have happened to any of us: the room was hot, the chairs a little to comfy, it was after lunch, for personal reasons he was dog-tired, it only lasted a few minutes and when he came to he realised and apologised. You might wonder what’s the big deal.

 

When he awoke and said sorry people seemed unsure how to respond so no one said much, we let some cool air into the room and the incident appeared to be over. But as the afternoon continued progress was slow, heads were down. A side conversation during the day suggested that the MD misjudged the significance of his sleep and also felt unsupported by his colleagues during his recent personal difficulties. He blamed them for allowing the company’s performance to slip.

 

Over dinner that evening it became clear the aftermath was serious. When the MD went to bed the rest of the team became mutinous, talked about the workshop being a waste of time and thinking about going home. Others disagreed and a bitter argument ensued. It appeared that the damage to this tight-knit team was huge. The unspoken message had been loud and clear (“What are we doing wasting our time here when he can’t be bothered even to stay awake!”). The incident had touched a raw nerve: many suspected the MD was drifting, disengaged and blamed him for the company’s recent poor performance.

 

The following morning there was no clear way forward without coming back to the issue, but to do so risked descent into blame, embarrassment and mud-slinging. So we began a live experiment in truth talking. An “open forum” allowed everyone to speak from the heart and sure enough the issue came to the surface.

 

The outcome

As people took turns to get things off their chest including their feelings of disappointment in the team the paralysing weight of blame seemed to lighten and people came alive again to their responsibilities. It took around three hours to work through and we realised that coming to terms with the MD falling asleep had delivered a wider benefit by raising the core issue of why performance had been slipping, and the way this was sapping motivation. As people expressed these feelings the mood started to lift and around 11am they reported feeling better and ready to move on.

 

Some lessons

In the safe setting of a Dialogue workshop it is possible to get the issues out on the table, explore them, express feelings including negative ones, and then naturally the energy can rise again. Without this opportunity unexpressed doubts and blames become blocks in the way of performance.

4. The impossibility of closure in the executive meeting

______________________________________________

 

The setting

The new executive team consisting of 12 directors was formed from several previously separate skill centres. The Executive VP heading the team was eager for the team as a whole to be more than the sum of the parts. He wanted to give his people tools that would help them collectively gain “traction” and he recognised immediately that taking his top team through a coaching and dialogue workshop could be a great opportunity to do just this.

 

What happened?

After the first day of practising one-to-one coaching skills in role-plays involving actors we came to the final session in which the team had to decide on 2-3 real topics for practising real conversations in the group the following day. This proved a real challenge that is illustrative of difficulties any large team faces in the apparently simple process of exploring options, and making decisions. Some people became animated and vocal while others held back keeping their powder dry. One moment things were heated and combative, while a second later the conversation lacked energy and seemed to drift. Just as an issue was reaching closure someone came in and took it off in a different direction. Everyone started to get tired. The Executive VP, previously eager for everyone to contribute their voice, started to work very hard to hold things together, pulling out themes and proposing solutions, while others seemed to alternative between compliance and rebellion. Finally after quite a frustrating 45 minutes the team applauded themselves for getting three topics on the flipchart and retired exhausted to dinner.

 

On the second morning, which was the final part of the workshop, the facilitators introduced a series of experiments that would enable the team to experience working in a different and much more productive way, applying in a group setting the coaching skills they had been practised the previous day.  Each experiment involved members of the team taking turns to sit outside the main discussion as observers, then in regular “five minute time-outs”, the main discussion would be stopped while the observers shared their observations and coached the group to take decisions on how to make their conversation more productive.

 

The outcome

Behaving differently was clearly a struggle but everyone worked at the challenge of bringing conversations to proper closure and the impact was dramatic. After first conversation the learning points were:

-         Coaching isn’t painful: it provides more structure to the meeting

-         Need to change our current meeting room dynamics

-         Allow more time/ limit topics during meetings

-         Get better at helping each other create better discussions

-         Clarify the issue a person is raising

-         Summarising very powerful

-         Insights come at the oddest moments

-         Meetings with coaches are more effective

-         Can you observe and participate?

-         Coach around the process and not the content

-         Time for closure required: focus on closing down

-         Coaching works but will take time to practice and work effectively

-         We have only just started – a long journey

-         Review team process after meetings – how can we do it better?

 

The second and third topics were then taken by sub-groups who were trusted to develop proposals while continuing the practice of using “five minute time-outs”. The subgroups reported back to the whole group and next steps were agreed. 

 

Before we closed, participants reviewed the whole workshop and concluded:

-         Very powerful 2 days

-         Very interesting teambuilding/bonding

-         People do hide their concerns and we need to get concerns on the table

-         Having an external observer means you can’t hide

-         Let’s have an experimental phase for a month of two

 

The team decided they would appoint a chair and a coach in every meeting and the Executive Ps emphasis would move to summarising, coaching and achieving closure. All agreed to nominate their own people to attend coaching workshops. As we discussed these points it was apparent that old habits die hard: each time someone spoke the team teetered on the edge, almost slipping back, until someone remembered and recovering things by drawing attention to what was happening.

 

Some lessons

After this workshop I made contact with the Executive VP to offer follow-up support. Some organisational changes were afoot and pending these my offer was put on hold. This week, some four months later I received an urgent phone, can I facilitate a three day meeting with the team next week as “bad behaviour is creeping in, doubts are surfacing over the proposed new organisation, we need to lance the boil… “ etc. Once again this is a reminder that people and teams forget, and under pressure they fragment and revert to old habits. For this team becoming more than the sum of the parts will be a challenge for some time to come. Dialogue Workshops may be needed as a regular practice at least through the immediate period of transition.

 5. Dealing with the MD’s emotional outbursts

______________________________________________

 

The setting

A pretty hotel in the German speaking part of Switzerland. Robert the MD had assembled his new team of 12 managers. Usually this global team has highly structured quarterly meetings, with items submitted for discussion in advance, then a strictly timed agenda with clear decisions and outputs enabling a large volume of business to be completed within the two days available. Once a year they set aside an additional day for teambuilding. This is a sunny August day, and the second time a teambuilding has happened. Four team members have recently left and four new ones have joined. The declared purpose of the day is to assist the new team to form.

 

What happened?

Robert stood up at the front to introduce the day, in his direct, no nonsense way, using a series of PowerPoint slides…

I want you four new guys to know a bit more about how we operate. I won’t go through all the slides, but here’s a pack for each of you. The most important thing to know is the buck stops here, in this room. We each have obligations – that’s what we’re paid for. We deliver results – that’s our job. Nothing immoral or unethical but remember we take responsibility for thousands of other people and jobs.

 

Then as if remembering this is a teambuilding day and eager to display his values he allows himself a reflecting and revealing aside…

You know I hate sacking a 30,000 a year guy who has 4 mouths to feed while some people in the company are travelling in private jets at 100,000 a trip.

 

Then returning to the message…

When we meet 4 times a year like this, the doors are closed – and when they do open nothing goes out.

 

Darkly…

Last year we said that, we said nothing leaves the room in the management development discussions then one person went back and leaked. He’s not here any more. When the doors are closed you can expect to be ripped apart. Good work is applauded, but we cannot be complacent and if you don’t prepare or if you deliver poor results, then expect to be confronted.

 

Quite suddenly then we’re sucked into reviewing an incident which took place 6 months ago and leaves the new people trying to figure out what happened while the established people using this opportunity to get things off their chests…

Some of you said my confronting you about our results last January was a low point in the year.

 

And quite suddenly the introductory monologue shifts into a dialogue as a real question starts to arise in the MD’s mind…

 I’m not sure what you really think of that because some of you also said the impact was positive, and our results are better 6 months later than the other divisions which did not confront their bad results early. Other divisions will do nothing and if you look out in 6 months time… they’ll claim the credit when the exchange rate turns in our favour making their results come good. They have done nothing to address the challenge of making the company perform better.

 

Sits down

 

New member from USA:  It’s OK to confront if it’s not personal. What was it like? Was it personal or not?

 

Established Member 1 giving information: This was the full Monty: shouting, stamping, throwing all the toys out the pram!

 

Established member 2 cautiously and gently unmasking the difficult: Everyone felt pretty odd when Robert was marching up and down the corridors, ranting and raving. Everyone notices it but doesn’t know how to react. It’s awkward.

 

Facilitator: Do I get the impression it is worse than odd? Maybe quite scary and confusing for people who aren’t involved in the problem. Disrupting their work.

 

People nod or show their agreement in their eyes.

 

New member from USA seeming concerned about abusive behaviour that Robert is being allowed to get away with here: Did it also happen in the leadership team meeting?

 

Established member 1 almost matter of factly, being careful not to criticise or judge Robert: Yes and that was pretty scary too. You won’t know till you’ve seen it.

 

Robert starting to uncover his side of the story justifying his out of control behaviour: When we asked you all for agenda items last January for the coming 2 day global meeting in February we only got half a day’s worth of items back and I thought God, we’re in the shit but no one is putting anything on the agenda to talk about. They’re all hiding. What’s going on here? The results are crap and no one is taking responsibility.

 

Established member 3 revealing his strong reaction to Robert’s outburst, spoken directly with passion): I don’t think you realised some of us care just as much about the business as you do. I had been away and not seen the results yet. We didn’t have a process for reviewing our results. When you exploded like that I was ready to throw it all in and get out of here…(Pause letting this sink in)

 

Robert concerned and checking up with this person: But you and I went through this together afterwards didn’t we?

 

Established member 3 underlining his point whilst also making peace: I was honestly ready to throw in the towel until Robert came to speak to me about it afterwards and we cleared things up between us.

 

Established member 4 as if struggling and deeply  uncomfortable at the way Robert had behaved but not willing to say so openly addressing the facilitator: But what do you think about emotional outbursts? Are they healthy?

 

Facilitator avoiding the risk of playing judge and passing the question out to the group: Would your colleagues say this was healthy?

 

Robert with great intensity: I know I’d rot if I held it inside. It’s called kick the cat and every organisation operates like this. My boss kicks me, I kick you and you go home and the cat gets it. That’s normal isn’t it?

 

Robert turns to a team profile chart: When you look at that profile of the team on the map here, you can see what happens. Under pressure you all get analytical, you’re all engineers aren’t you, heads up your arses looking for some perfect answer…. But I go into bull-dozer mode, driving harder, pushing for delivery, caring about the results. That’s me doing my job and…

 

Willingly taking another diversion into his personal background that for many leaders would be off limits…

… it goes back to how your family was growing up, me and my Dad. When I go into the Executive Committee meetings with my boss I put on my best behaviour, maybe when some of you come to our meetings, you come on your best behaviour, a bit formal like I do to the Executive.

 

Several people nodding then silence before Robert continues…

But at home it’s different right? If I hit my thumb with the hammer I shout and swear and break things. All hell breaks out.

 

Taking another leap back into the past experience that made the man today…

My father used to argue with me all the time and beat me down, imposing his opinion on me. I’d try to stand up to him but he argue and persuade me why he was right and I’d leave the room in tears. It took years before I realised that I could hold my opinion back. I didn’t have to expose what I thought to my Dad and if I held it back he couldn’t break me and I could walk out of the room intact. Then I’d won, not him.

 

Then perhaps it was the extremely personal nature of this that we did not know how to receive because the discussion seemed to miss that Robert had learned as a child to restrain himself and reverted to the earlier point he made about rotting if he held something inside.

 

New member from USA seeming now to understand and accept the deal that Robert was asking for: You can’t hold it inside, that would be wrong.

 

Established member 5 with great concern: People get changed by their position. I like it that we can still see you, that you continue to be who you are, strengths and weaknesses. We have an expression in German which roughly translated means “Power corrupts, absolute power corrupts absolutely”…

… continuing seeming afraid to lose the capacity to know and trust Robert and spoke directly to him:  Most people in senior positions are corrupted and you cannot recognise them any more. This hasn’t happened to you. Don’t let your position corrupt you. Don’t be changed by the position you occupy.

 

Established member 6 with wry humour: In Singapore we talk about guided democracy… that another way of saying autocratic. That’s what we have here.

 

Robert easier, lighter now as if emerging from confession the issue dealt with, responding to the group’s acceptance of him warts and all: Someone has to set the rules. Someone has to give the bollockings. Today I want us to have an open conversation, less formal. I want to lift this team to the next level of performance, financially, emotionally and in teamwork. That’s what today is about. OK.

 

The outcome

A natural pause after a dialogue of stunning intensity. A block has been shifted out of the way. The stage had been set. Everyone seemed ready now to engage with one another and get on with the day.

 

Some lessons

This was most unexpected. I understand it is entirely uncharacteristic of any other meeting the team has. The opportunity provided by this teambuilding day was grabbed by Robert in the first few minutes to get right down to business on the emotional agenda. I was quite unsure whether I supported what was being said at times. Was I and were we condoning an abusive regime? Was Robert a bully, or just a sincere guy who had decided to be upfront in everything? In the midst of this difficulty I felt the facilitator’s job was to hold the space, not to try any neat moves or to summarise conclusions, or to put in a personal or professional opinion but to let what had to happen happen and to stay out of the way!
