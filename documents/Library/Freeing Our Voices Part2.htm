Part 2: The Voice Workout

 

The Voice Workout is a kind of adventure. A kind of discovery zone. It is real not theoretical. It will involve you in doing stuff beyond just reading: finding out what happens and learning from it. What kind of adventure will this be? That's up to you. My contribution is to pose a series of tests. Through undertaking and reacting to these tests, and noticing your reactions, you advance to still more challenging tests.

 

Through the tests you can find your voice and develop a certain wisdom about how you use your voice in relation to the voices of other people around you. Through this Voice Workout you can raise the performance of your team to a new level.

 

The workout entails unearthing and developing your own proposition, which is a sort of statement to your team about the central work challenge you and they are embarked on at this time. Using the proposition as provocation, you draw reactions from your team members, and as you react authentically to their reactions you weave together a shared proposition, in which you and your people recognise your voices. The workout  gets you all ready to enact the proposition in the world.

 

The tests I give you may appear crude and simple at first, but don't let this mislead you - you start with the basics and the tests get harder quite quickly!

 

Some way into this, as with a workout at the gym, you might start to find yourself slowing, and having difficulty. So then what can you do?  Have a break. Pause and come back. Take time out. Turn to a friend or coach. Get a learning group together to check out your reactions, and support each other as you complete the workout. Don't give up. Find a way to get through the workout to enjoy the pay-off in part 3!

Test 1 Checking out your reactions

Pinch yourself.

 

I just did. On my right forearm. I took a fold of skin between my left forefinger and thumb, and I squeezed. Hard. It hurt. Back of hand. See the white circle and the colour return. Tummy tuck. Chest. Love handles. Each time a slightly different sensation. A different reaction.

Do you ever avoid other experiences in this way? Do you ever pay proper attention to how you are reacting? Now go back and do it. This is about paying attention to yourself and how you react.

Test 2: Now I am aware of…

 

There's a simple game. It's called the awareness game*. In this game you simply speak out what you are noticing moment by moment, following your attention wherever it goes.

 

Here goes. This is my turn. Now I'm aware of… sitting here typing, the sound click clack of the keys, a tram clattering over the bridge outside my hotel window, a door slamming in the corridor, sunlight glinting off river, peculiar calm here while news from the wider world is so scary, aeroplane engine roar swooping low outside, bells, neck a bit stiff, head a bit achy, feeling excited writing this, alert now, not wanting to stop, how much is coming in through my ears…

 

Try it. On your own. Now.

 

What did you notice? Try speaking it out. Write it down if it helps.

 

Were you able to follow your attention wherever it led?

 

For how long?

 

When did you find yourself taking charge and directing?

 

You can play this game with a partner. Try it. The reactions tend to be different. When I have tried this my attention sort of oscillates to and from the other person. Finding myself drawn, but not wanting to be impolite, or too "personal". So I move my attention away to the world around us, then I feel like I'm being rude by leaving them out, and behaving as if they are not there, and as I wrestle with this I find myself following thoughts, floating away, following an interesting strand of something, as if it is real and important, when really it is probably avoiding voicing something else, keeping my attention and my voice away from something more hot or embarrassing.

 

If you try this you might find quite quickly which reactions you tend to voice, and which you censor or hide away, and for what reasons. What is it you avoid talking about? What could be undiscussable? Your worry about doing it right? Sex? Gender? Skin colour? Something embarrassing? What are you afraid to voice? Do you notice yourself trying to get closer or further from the other person? What else is going on for you?

 

Above all be honest with yourself here. You don't need to tell anyone else.

 

Notice how the Now I am aware of… test encourages you into direct language. Not theoretical. Not distant. Not "there and then" but "here and now". When you and your partner speak in this way notice how lively, interesting and relevant it feels.

 

Notice also the range of things that you can become aware of:

 

_________________

1.      The emotional domain (internal) - your mood, emotional predisposition and feelings: anger (from mildly irritated to incandescent with range), fear (from slight prickling anxiety to white and physically shaking), sadness (from slight unhappy twinge to torn apart by grief), joy (from mild pleasure to ecstasy)

 

2.      The bodily domain (internal) - all that is happening in your body, guts, breathing, blinking, pulse, skin temperature, aches and pains, where in your body your attention is drawn to, the feeling you have about how you are holding yourself

 

3.      The thinking domain (internal) - remembering, imagining, thoughts and ideas

 

4.      The external domain - all that you notice happening in the world around you

 

5.      How the internal and external domains interact

 

6.      How much you direct or follow your awareness

_________________

 

How many of these six domains were you aware of in the test. Repeat the test and see which you notice now. You can come back to this test time and again, both to relax and to develop your self-awareness.

Test 3: Finding the gap

 

Each time you react to something, like a pinch on your arm, or someone speaking to you, you may notice either an inner reaction (which is private and known only to you), an outer response (which is public and observable by anyone nearby), or both. This test is to raise your awareness of both inner reactions and outer responses, and importantly to the gap between them.

 

For example, when I pinched my arm, the inner reaction was:

 

Ouch, pain, stop it, this is stupid!

 

The outer response was:

 

1.      Silence. I was sitting alone in my hotel room. No one to scream to.

2.      Stopping. I stopped pinching. I relaxed my forefinger and thumb, then examined the damage.

 

When you played the Now I am aware of… game, there was little or no gap between inner and outer. The game was to speak out your inner experience: which is something we rarely do in a completely uninhibited way in real life, for good reason. If you have watched the films Liar Liar, or What Women Want, you see Hollywood stars getting into all sorts of trouble at work and home by speaking out directly everything they are experiencing, without regard to the interests of the people with them. You always have a choice about what to voice! This test is about making that choice more conscious, and better informed.

 

In considering this choice I want to give a health warning in the form of a story about my father's mid-life crisis. It is about boundaries between people, and the bond of intimacy that can develop when voicing happens in pairs. My father, who died last year, was a lively, religious, man married for over 15 years, with 4 children, when in the late 1960's at the age of about 44, he changed from a "conformist risk averse" life and career as a statistician, to an "experimental" life with a new career in management training. He became very involved with the newly emerging personal growth movement, spending his weekends in T-groups, encounter groups and gestalt sessions. The stress and conflict this new phase in his life produced in him was visible around his eyes: a deep frown above them and black bags beneath. To remind you about the spirit of the times, some hit songs were The Beatles "All You Need Is Love", and Stephen Stills "Love The One You're With". Although I did not know it at the time, my father had become involved in a series of close relationships (some of them sexual) with women at work.

 

In one sensitivity training group participants (most of them managers from a large British company: I think it was ICI) were put into pairs, my father with a younger female manager. Each partner had a day observing their other half, in silence. Then when it came to feedback discussions, I recall my father describing the delicious liberation of speaking to each other and the strong bond of intimacy that developed between them. He was knocked over by this and talked freely about it, but insisted it was not sexual, not love and not threatening to us. My father deluded himself that he as being called to "help" the other person, in a professional or religious sense, or at least this is what he told my mother.

 

I remember, on holiday at a campsite in Norfolk, his waking up in the early hours of the morning with a dream that for him seemed to resolve all the tension he was carrying. He had dreamt about triangles, and the world seemed to him to be all arranged in sets of three. The triangle that was key to it all was God the father, God the son, and the Holy Spirit. My father's part in this was as vehicle for the Holy Spirit, which perhaps removed any guilt about those close relationships. Also the dream told him, like the blinding light on the road to Damascus, that it was "meant to be" that he, my mother and his current woman should move in together. Needless to say neither my mother nor Sheila (the other woman who he later married) thought it was such a good idea.

 

From this phase onwards my father's life seemed to fall apart and those around him experienced enormous confusion, helplessness and pain. I remember numbness, no real sense of anger, defiant independence, and a false pretence at adaptation: "I'm almost grown up and I don't need you to be a father to me anymore, but I would like us to remain friends". Behind this I now realise was a terror of losing him altogether and an undeclared but powerful sense of rejection: "If I/we really mattered to you, you would not be doing this!" In the decades that followed this zealot, slightly deflated and bemused by others reactions to his dream, went to Africa for several years, then returned to go through two further marriages, and became more and more taciturn and unreachable. I found it impossible, in spite of trying my best on more than one occasion, to get through to him and draw out any proper account of this bizarre series of events.

 

In summary, this unsettling story of a mistake etched its mark deeply into me and my family. My father's silence of the next three decades was to me like a foot thick reinforced concrete boundary, like a fear of any contact at all: an excessive counter-reaction to the earlier mistake.  Sometimes I felt he was trying with a hug or a stab at conversation to break through this barrier, and I tried at times to the point of showing him in tears him how confused and upset I was. But we were unable to connect, until I forgave him a few minutes before he died last year, and by then he was unable to talk.

 

Perhaps this accounts for the mixed messages colleagues sometimes get from me: interested in being authentic, tongue-tied, awkward, holding my feelings and information back from them, physically at a distance, not huggy, lacking in expressiveness.

 

Now I've spilled these beans to you, I hope it doesn't not frighten you off this workout, or confuse you or undermine what I'm trying to put across in this book. It is a ghost in my cupboard that I am laying to rest, and somehow highly relevant to this work. It is a story about understanding boundaries: the line where one person meets another.

 

During the last 18 months or so I have been attending on the first Monday of each month an advanced facilitation run by Toni Gilligan and Tony Fraser, and during these sessions I have been learning about boundaries. The way I have it now is that it helps to have a strong sense of who you are, and of being OK with who you are, able to include yourself and your emotion, or belly voice in any situation, knowing clearly for example that "I'm Tony and I'm feeling angry at you". Once I have chosen to voice this to the person I'm angry with, it is up to them to deal with their reaction, and they have a choice about whether react back to me or hold their reaction in, or take it to someone else. This is being clear over boundaries: knowing what's mine and what's yours, and being aware enough to know what I need from you and able to exercise choice how I make contact with you to get my needs met.

 

Often the boundary gets blurred as in my father's example, and the people involved get mixed up about who they are, what they are really there for. They lose touch with their true needs, they start to misunderstand each other, strong uncomfortable feelings can get stirred up ranging from the depths of fear and anger to the heights of joy, and the relationship tends to get strained as both parties start to blame each other for the feelings that are arising but not owned and not voiced.

 

I am learning there are five things that happen when relationships get strained in this way, causing you to fail to "connect" and get your needs met, and there is a technical word for each:

______________

 

1.      PROJECTION - when you see something that is really yours, like your anger, as being outside you and residing in someone else eg. you experience the other person as being very angry when you are feeling angry yourself but not knowing and owning your anger

2.      INTROJECTION - when you take in something from outside you, unquestioningly, and start to believe it is yours eg. blindly following rules set by others, swallowing an argument hook, line and sinker

3.      DEFLECTION - not noticing what someone is trying to say to you, missing the point eg. unconsciously changing the subject to something less disturbing

4.      RETROFLECTION - becoming divided inside, with one part of you that is trying to do something, feeling perhaps angry at another part of you which is trying not to eg. trying to lose weight and getting angry with yourself for failing

5.      CONFLUENCE - losing a sense of being separate from what is outside you eg. not having an opinion, letting others speak for you

______________

 

Working intensively with colleagues on consultancy projects I experience hot and strained moments in the working relationship. When I look back at these to question how I'm managing boundaries it can be quite revealing. I realise I and everyone, you, my colleagues, my clients, my family, regularly fall into the five traps above. For example I might send a confusing and difficult message along the lines of: "I'm angry with you, but I'm not going to tell you because underneath it all I want you still to like me and I'm afraid you are going to find me unlikeable and so reject me". When the other person senses my irritation they ask me if I'm OK and however hard they and I try, this simple pattern is buried and hard to own up to.

 

Having learned about these five traps, I have become a lot clearer about my own boundaries. Curiously this framework helps me better understand what I was trying to say in my earlier writings on the subject of being authentic (How to Spot a Faker, 1992) and on flow experiences (Change Management for Real? 1996?). I realise in these writings and in my work with colleagues I have been challenging myself to got out of a comfort zone, to explore, to grow, to risk something, to go beyond a deep fear I hold inside which goes right back to my childhood, and at times it looks like I may have unconsciously been acting out some patterns similar to my father's. It is hard to avoid the link between the way I described "flow" and my father's quasi-religious/sexual experience of intimate conversation. It comes across as if I had to challenge myself to go there and sort of get through it instead of falling into his trap.

 

So I suppose, in a nutshell I'm saying to you, don't expect this boundary stuff to be easy. Be careful. Be sure of who you are. Expect and be prepared to confront and unravel some mixed messages and deep seated patterns. And if the stakes are high, for example in change programmes where jobs and livelihoods are at risk, instead of complaining how irrational everyone has become, expect the difficult dynamics just to become more prominent and exaggerated, and own up to your own part in all this.

 

A lot of this difficulty about how much to voice to whom and when goes away when you work from a principle that your happiness and success is ultimately intertwined with all the people around you. When you voice something true, even if it is to say "Back off and leave me alone for a bit", or "Sorry I'm not happy about the work you have been doing here lately", it is in the spirit of a gift to the other person, making a move to put the relationship on a sounder footing.

 

To conclude and lead up to your test: you get by in life exercising judgement about how much of your inner experience gets spoken out. The Voice Proposition says when you use your voice, uncorked with direct language, this often produces better outcomes for you and others. To come to know and trust this requires practice in finding out what you are not voicing, and challenging yourself to better serve yourself, others and the situation by saying more of what is unvoiced, giving others more of your belly voice.

 

Try it.

In exploring this gap, it is noticeable how often in work teams the emotion is what is edited out. An inner reaction of joy, excitement, frustration, worry, concern, fear, or anxiety would often remain hidden, masked, covered over. The outer response would be rational while the overwhelming personal sensation was emotional.

 

Sometimes this editing is for good survival reasons. Sometimes it is just a habit, and a self-defeating one at that. As a grown-up you must take responsibility for the choice and the consequences. As a grown-up who wants to lead and participate in an effective team you will pay attention to what you are editing out, and you will be aware that when emotion gets edited out the team spirit dies, the fun and the energy goes away.

Test 4: Spotting Stuck Conversations

 

Look out for conversations that get stuck, embarrassing and difficult. Explore what is unvoiced and blocking. This is the test. Find out what is going on. Notice your part in it.

 

Here are a couple of examples to get you started.

 

Imagine a pretty 18 year old, from Turkey, comes to London to brush up her English working as an au pair in a wealthy home. You are the mother she has come to help. You work 4 days a week. While you're at work the au pair will look after your two children and do the cleaning, washing and ironing. What this amounts to is you gaining low cost help. After a few months the arrangement is not working well. She stays out late, wanders round the house looking just a little bit too alluring in front of your partner, and on occasion forgets to do the kids' packed lunches. You've tried to speak to her about these shortfalls but somehow the conversation always gets stuck. Then you both feel embarrassed and let it drift.

 

If we delve to find what is blocking we find unvoiced and undiscussable power "gradients". As the mother, you have the power to hire, fire, set wages, provide food and a roof. This makes the au pair feel somewhat oppressed and vulnerable. It is the first time she has been in a paid job. She is far from home. But hold on. There's a power gradient going the other way too. You do not feel at all powerful yourself. Quite the contrary. You feel extremely vulnerable too. What if the au pair fails to take good care of your children? You feel bad anyway about leaving your children in the care of another. And hang on. Don't you also feel a duty of care towards the au pair when she stays out late at night in the big bad city? Can you stop her? No. Vulnerable again. What if the au pair flirts with your partner: isn't it irritating how she looks so much younger and sexier than you? And on top of this the conniving bitch is consistently late, holding back information and on occasions lying to you about what the children are up to. It's a can of worms. Buried resentments masked behind a façade of restrained politeness. Trying to make the best of an imperfect situation.

 

The apparently powerful person (mother) felt vulnerable and depends on the other person's support. The apparently oppressed person (au pair) had a hidden source of power to get back (stay out late, flirt, break rules, be unreliable) which serves as an unwelcome reminder to the mother/power figure of her vulnerability. The power dynamics driving the drama made both parties feel uncomfortable, embarrassed and remain unvoiced. With this vivid truth unspeakable and unvoiced the conversation becomes stuck and the relationship is blocked. Not functioning. On hold. Both parties feel frustration and anxiety. Poised atop a precipice, an emotional outburst could knock you over at any time. The tension attached to holding the emotion in weighs heavy on both au pair and mother. It is likely the au pair will leave or the mother will ask her to go.

 

OK. That's an example from outside work. Nothing like this ever happens in your workplace does it? Sorry. No. I'm not having that! I spend my life facilitating meetings where there are strong undercurrents, and no one wants to say what is happening, mostly I think through some sort of fear of exposure. After all information is power. Vulnerability is an invitation like blood to sharks. The undercurrent often is undiscussable and being both strong and unvoiced blocks the conversation. Sometimes as a leader or a facilitator you confront the blockage, and other times you make a mental note and let it go. Here's an example.

 

Imagine you're the MD in this hotel company concerned about the level of accidents being reported throughout the hotel chain, and you decide to raise it in a team meeting.

____________

 

MD:

We have more incidents in this company than any I have worked in before. Why is that?

 

 

(Silence)

 

MD:

What about the lawnmower incident last week?

 

Fred, Regional Director:

 

 

(Slightly smirking) Which one?

 

MD:

In one of your hotels Fred. Ran over a guest didn't it?

 

Jack, HR Director:

 

(Sensing tension, seeking to lighten things up) It only ran over his foot!

 

 

(Ripple of laughter)

 

MD:

(Looking cross and moving his eyes down to the floor) That's what I mean. Who is going to take responsibility?

 

 

(Fred looking unruffled and seemingly oblivious of MD's anger. Group relaxing as if relieved the issue is being avoided).

____________

 

What was unvoiced and avoided? So much. Why? So many reasons. This conversation was not really about the lawn mower accident was it? What else was going on at the time? Results were falling badly behind plan. 2 members of the team have just been sacked. Everyone felt vulnerable including the MD. This context had been talked about already, but maybe not really properly addressed and no one wanted to return to a depressing subject. The MD felt angry that his team were letting him down, not taking responsibility the way he needed them to. But had he told them he felt angry? The accident record was just another example to him of the same old problem. Is this why he raised it, to try to say I'm angry with you? What was stopping him from expressing his anger directly. Maybe an understandable concern about upsetting the team and further depressing their performance.

 

The MD had the power to fire Fred, but did not want to: Fred was older and more experienced, the MD had no replacement and needed Fred to deliver results. Fred may not have known he was needed and so felt vulnerable. The others in the team feared that the MD might direct his anger at them.

 

So everyone seemed to be stuck in a vulnerable, fearful place, but instead of being able to voice and include this in the discussion, the subject was avoided, the inner reality became split off from the conversation and buried. The outer actions and words became false, incomplete, covering up rather than revealing what was truly going on.

 

Once again, just like the mother, the apparently powerful person (MD) is feeling weak, vulnerable and dependent on his team. The stakes are high and everyone feels the risk of failure and exposure. The apparently oppressed team have their own hidden sources of power (weight of numbers, silence, make light of it, avoid discussion, shirk responsibility, make no promises). The unvoiced interdependency of the MD and the team blocks the conversation. They both need each other. Everyone to varying degrees feels frustration and anxiety. The story is not over.

 

Now I've given you two examples. This test is to find an example of your own.

What REQUEST do you ask of me?

Test 5: Awakening your voice

 

Awakening the voice is pretty much about bringing your inner reaction out, closing that gap. You take a risk. Maybe you'll feel embarrassed to admit what is going on in you. Maybe others will ridicule, attack or reject you. Maybe others will be upset at what you have to say. Let me give you my own experience of this before describing your voice awakening test.

 

When I was writing Diary of a Change Agent I seemed to find my voice, and when you find your voice, it is a great feeling, freed from inhibition, because it, your voice, seems to take charge and speak for you. But this was my written voice, not my spoken voice. Of course I knew how to speak, but the idea of speaking to a gathering at the launch of my book made me clam up. The book was quite personal and I felt vulnerable to attack, so unprepared to speak in a heartfelt, sincere and confident way.

 

To overcome this I invited Andy Harmon, an actor friend to put on his Actor's Mind presentation training course for me and a few colleagues. The course was very successful…. for the others, but left me even more painfully aware of my awkwardness. The group were asked to shout "break" each time I broke eye contact and let my eyes drift up to the ceiling to find my words. It was through working with Andy subsequently that I have learned so much about giving voice, my own blocks to speaking and how to overcome them. Interestingly, in the research project Andy and I recently undertook together, he has been learning about writing while I have been learning about speaking!

 

I got through various book launch meetings OK, although I remember someone telling me I spoke in a way that was gawky, lacking in polish, like an eleven year old in short trousers with a catapult shoved down his sock! Thanks very much! Thankfully I received lots of praise and encouragement too. But there was an element of truth in this witty but cutting remark. I did feel awkward, and the truth hurts doesn't it?

 

Only several months later while facilitating a workshop (alongside two other facilitators Vyla and Jeremy) with the 30 member council of the Institute of Management Consultants (IMC) at Sunningdale, did I really find my spoken voice. Here is my recollection:

___________

 

On Friday evening Council Members were unhappy, saying their meetings were very frustrating. As facilitators the three of us experienced a lot of direct anger, hostility, confusion and negativity. I felt they were saying "let's not talk about this" and questioning our role, saying "we don't need facilitators". At different points each of us sunk under this weight, feeling unwelcome or inadequate, beginning to doubt if we were being effective and whether we should even be there.

 

Around 5am on Saturday I awoke, my head filled with thoughts, questions and ideas about the day ahead. I knew what to say and do. I needed to take charge and speak to the whole group at the start of the day about the emotions people get in the process of change, validating and giving a context to what we had all been experiencing the previous day, then setting out a plan for the day. As I spoke you could hear a pin drop. There was rapt attention. The anger seemed to have lifted. We were in the groove. Time seemed to have slowed down. People were agreeing with what was happening and ready to be led.

___________

 

Since then I have found my voice on several occasions and I have realised there is a bit of a pattern. Slowed down, I experience it something like this:

___________

 

I look around me at the people present. I feel a little anxious, then as my thoughts get clear, the words just come to me, often just the first few words I need to get started. I realise I know what I have to say. I reach inside to check, bringing out my mood and feelings. I look out around me and notice the moment, the space for me to speak, the rapt attention of others.

 

I feel at ease but at the same time alert, engaged fully, not having to concentrate hard.  I feel an energy moving through me. It's as if the words are cleansing me. I feel connected inside with outside, as if I am speaking not just for me but for others in the room. There is as much time as I need. I feel fluent and gradually emptying.

 

When others seem to shuffle a little, the spell is broken and spontaneously the moment ends. 

 

Afterwards, I feel light, as if I could float up to the ceiling, happy, relieved, hugely satisfied. The afterglow can last all evening and into the next day.

In short it's a delicious experience! The effects can be powerful in unblocking what needs to happen. At the IMC workshop by lunchtime I remember Barry, the incoming president saying "the cork is out of the bottle now". There was a sense of embarking on something hugely exciting and also a bit risky. During the next 12 months the institute was completely reinvented, with a new slimmed down council, a changed constitution, new membership categories, a new more relevant approach and a new name!

 

In spite of the blocks to voicing all around us, it feels like voicing is exactly what NEEDS to happen when a situation is stuck, when putting into words what everyone is noticing and feeling is just what everyone WANTS to avoid at all costs. What serves the "whole system" is to voice, when the people making up the system are feeling defensive and clammed up. The stakes are high. Putting your head above the parapet may not seem sensible. But when you remember the simple underlying dynamics that we all tend to forget when the situation gets hot, it is a lot clearer why voicing is what you have to do. I'll restate them now just to be really clear.

 

Failure to give voice to what’s at stake is a cover up, avoiding what is really going on. This can happen deliberately, but often happens by mistake, without realising, unconsciously.

 

Let's remember how stuck those conversations we looked at were. Let's notice how weak those stuck conversations made everyone feel. The powerful people were feeling very vulnerable, and although the less powerful people had discovered their power to hit back, they themselves were feeling weak and not directing their power positively. Wouldn't it be worth finding something that could bring the strength, the power, the confidence and the potential back? This is why we are awakening our voices.

 

The effect of awakening your voice is to include and face up to what is really going on. The prize is valuable. It releases energy, shares and shifts responsibility to where it belongs, transforms the situation, creating a positive mood and a path forward.

 

Learning the art of voicing is key to this. Recently Julian Burton and I put on a one day workshop at Questors Theatre in Ealing for a small group of colleagues run by Anthony Johnston, who teaches voice and creative expression. After some warming up, moving and breathing exercises, we found ourselves stepping out of our comfort zones and singing to each other, learning without words how to communicate and have an impact on each other. Here is how Anthony describes the process:

 

_______________

 

The voice is our most powerful and direct means of communication, but for many reasons it remains unexplored, locked behind imaginary veils of fear. Fears of being judged, shamed or criticised all help to keep our true voice locked away, often for years. Without a voice the very core of our being becomes hidden and separate.

 

This is an opportunity to experience your natural voice. Both powerful and effortless, it is the sound that wells up from deep within and is none other than who you are. To discover your natural voice is to find something that cannot be forgotten, to express who you are is to celebrate our very existence. This is an invitation to sing out beyond fear and self-doubt, beyond right and wrong, to discover a voice that truly satisfies.

_______________

 

What was most memorable was finding in each person in turn, a strong and unique voice, that when it found its pitch vibrated the eardrums and resonated through the whole room. At the end I felt unburdened and filled with new confidence: knowing I had a voice I can trust.

 

Of course when we return to the political workplace, it is possible to make gaffs, making yourself vulnerable, voicing too suddenly, hitting out without care towards others present. Equally you can become too concerned for the reactions of others and diverted from the truth that is in you. Genuine interest in serving the whole team, with commitment to dialogue with the other people present - these are some of the preconditions. Like a good relationship, good voicing is reciprocal, two-sided, gradual and progressive.

First example. Andreas, a new Chief Executive was hired into a struggling international industrial company. Everyone, inside and outside the company was demanding a strong leader, and the recruitment brief reflected this. This seemed to strike a chord with him and everyone else around, but if we look behind the request for strong leadership, if we unpack this to find the real meaning of "strong leadership", the result 25,000 people are seeking but not finding a way to voice certainly includes:

-        a confident and stable direction rather than vacillating

-        access, influence, a voice, a sense of contact and trust. To be reacted to as if we exist, we matter in the scheme of things, we make a difference

-        someone who understands our sense of outrage at previous senior management behaviour over the last 2 years: the way we felt about the spin-off, the overpaying for an acquisition, the lining of your own pockets, the heavy emphasis on shareholder value, to cost-cutting that inflicted pain without delivering benefit.

 

Success for Andreas going forward would seem to depend on picking up the unvoiced messages, then refining his proposition from the crude offer of "strong leadership" to something that reflects more precisely what is needed here. Until he does this, and I hope he does, then he will be unsupported by the 25,000 people who do the real work: making and selling and delivering the performance Andreas needs.

 

Second example. My role in Andreas' company is to help senior managers with managing change. Two or three times a year I take a group of 12 managers through a programme called Navigating Change in which they identify a change they need to make and work on it together as a project. Hearing the Andreas story had a strong effect on me. It challenged me to clarify what exactly the Navigating Change workshop is about. In other words what was the Navigating Change proposition? I reflected on the projects that senior managers were bringing to the workshop and the difficulties they seemed to be having. I concluded they had an ambivalence about strong leadership: they wanted it, and at the same time they didn't. They were supposed to be leaders themselves, and they were experiencing strong leadership from Andreas and others above them, and they were unclear and uncomfortable about the part they were to play as senior managers in bringing about change. So how could Navigating Change help them with this?

 

Out of this reflection came a new Navigating Change proposition which I have started to use within Andreas' company.  Here it is:

____________

 

1.      Change is an unreasonable challenge

-        to perform beyond precedent

-        requiring a new way of thinking and working

-        a journey into the unknown

 

2.      It requires strong leadership

 

3.      Strong leadership produces strong reactions

 

4.      Reactions need to be voiced and reacted to so that people can adapt inside (themselves)

 

5.    People must adapt inside (themselves) before change in the company can take root

____________

 

The proposition does not have to be "right", or even original, but it must carve out a clearing for the workshop, giving a clear context for detailed work on the change projects,  providing a challenging space for informative dialogue and learning. In other words it needs to be provocative. In essence it is my voice, provoking the voice of participants. Out of such provocation, and the resulting dialogue comes meaning.

 

Third example. For a long time I have been interested in finding a suitably provocative proposition that relates to my broader purpose and intention in the work I do. In other words, why do I do what I do? How do I know I'm doing it well?

 

This proposition has evolved over recent years, from reflecting on a number of formative experiences: flashes of the blindingly obvious (which I described in Diary of a Change Agent), through coaching with Tessa Bradon in which she encouraged me to draw pictures of my vision, through successes and learning coming out of each workshop I facilitate and each person I coach. Recently I explored the theme of my own anger and this put me in touch with early, less happy patterns I experienced growing up in my family which still influence me today. Around this time the Don't Try To Shut Me Up poem (in part 1 of this book) sort of erupted in me one morning as I was waking up.

 

My resulting proposition is framed as a declaration of possibility*. Although it took a long time to evolve. I still feel a little foolish and uncomfortable coming out with these words. They seem to me a bit pompous, and a bit grand, but that means they provoke me to raise my game:

 

I declare the possibility of creating successful teams and organisations

that people WANT to work in,

where everyone knows they have a VOICE.

 

The proposition rests more easily with me when I notice its opposite: that it is possible for me to damage team and organisational performance, through leaving people out, rejecting their voice, making them want to not be there. I feel I'm trusted by my clients not to do this kind of damage. In fact my offer to clients is to create value and success through bringing the best from their people. My intention and my work is well captured by the slightly cringe-making words above and the proposition plays itself out in countless small, and sometimes quite visible, ways each time I work with a team and a leader.

Test 8 Confronting the bad guys

 

The act of making and voicing a proposition is provocative. It raises your voice, and seeks a response. Often, instead of a calm and measured voice speaking back, you may find you have a fight on your hands, and much as this challenges my belief in pacifism, I've come to realise that is OK, in fact it can be good and exactly what you might want as a sign of interest, passion, and the chance to make progress. I used to be shy about argument and debate, and a fight still doesn't feel altogether wholesome to me. However it would also be wrong for me to ignore the way I have often worked successfully alongside a colleague prepared to stand and fight: good cop - bad cop.

Just as in the boy and mother example, polarisation had happened, lines had been drawn, I had made up my mind that the other Tony was out of order, bad and wrong, and then our self-appointed facilitator was wrong too. But this time I started to seriously question my deeply held view that fighting, even the verbal form, is always dangerous or usually wrong. Something new and important was being and about to be voiced. Not to say it can be more wrong than to have it out.

 

Now I think fighting is wrong if it inevitably escalates to war. But how often is a situation truly irretrievable? And might a fight sometimes do a lot of good? Fighting would be wrong, like bullying, if one side had so much power they massacre the other. But isn't there a large empty space between walking away and massacre? What would be the positive value of surfacing conflict, as opposed to burying it? What happens when two roughly equal opponents, or partners even, start to spar? I realised in the exchange with Tony Williams, when I stood my ground, that a  passionate, honest, heartfelt and true belly voice can come out of me, that I did not know was there and we would not have known about otherwise. I was not going to allow Tony to slap me down. I believed in this experiment. I did not so much choose to fight him: it felt like the fight chose me. I NEEDED him to know he made my blood boil! I NEEDED to work it through with him. I wanted to get through to him.

 

This brings me to the intriguing proposition that Andy Harmon has been developing with his wife Anita and their work partner Matts Thorin. Last autumn, I attended a pilot of their new course on conflict called "Here Be Dragons". We spent a day learning to do stage-fighting and explored the concept of shadow (the worst and the best parts of us that we can't admit to so we deny).

 

Through all the pretend shouting, kicking and punching, I learned that even in stage fighting you must hit hard at the target. It is wrong to pull your punches because it denies your opponent the opportunity to respond at his/her best (and, as a secondary point, in film and theatre it spoils the show for the audience!).

 

I'm still grappling with the issues this insight raises for me. Where do I draw the line, the fence or boundary that divides me from you? When am I OK speaking over the fence to you? When is my speaking intrusive, abusive? What do I do when I want to kick down your fence: do I let myself and apologise afterwards, or stop myself and respect the boundary? Perhaps you've noticing that the answers here may not be universal, or set in advance but negotiated, constructed social through direct communication and feedback in real time, as in: "get your foot back over that line!". But I also realise that deep respect for one's opponent is part of it.

 

As I write this I wonder if I'm voicing questions relevant only to specialist facilitators. Am I falling into the change management double speak that Tony W is wary of? No. I don't think so. Granted you never need to ask such questions if you know where you are and everyone around you, and all your stakeholders are comfortable and happy. But since most leaders like you are in organisations facing tough challenges, high stakes, forging new relationships, you are fighting and redefining your boundaries.

I noticed how fragmented this team was, how a couple of people thought they were being honest, but the effect of confronting each other aggressively was to turn the meetings into frustrating, humiliating bunfights (their term), in which the less aggressive people felt attacked and blamed. Somehow this team had gone to extremes with the confrontation principle, crossed a line, splitting themselves between aggressors being "direct" and the others watching from their bunkers with horror. Once this aggressive behaviour had been confronted and named, and a groundrule of "Supportive" was explored and adopted, then it started to feel more like a team.

 

Underlying these examples of confrontation, and the lessons from the Dragons course, a map is emerging, for having a necessary and beneficial team-serving fight with a bad guy, which looks a bit like this:

______________

 

1.      NOTICE, whenever it happens, a strong feeling that someone else is the "bad guy". This shows you're getting close to something unvoiced in you that needs to be voiced. What is unvoiced is your shadow: a feeling or desire about what is happening that is living inside you but almost unconscious and not admitted to

 

2.      HOLD onto the feeling. We know you can explain this "bad guy stuff" as identification and projection, but that is no excuse to ignore the strong feeling: unless you voice and work with it, nothing happens, the voice remains buried, the situation remains blocked

 

3.      Choose your place and time and CONFRONT the bad guy. This is how you bring out your voice, and his/her voice, releasing the blocked energy. If you're the boss then don't humiliate your bad guys in public, and don't bully or threaten them. Ask for them to be authentic back, and try to make it safe for them. You could call this wearing the warrior mask, standing your ground, penetrating the bullshit, cutting to the chase. The proposition you developed is what brings up the voices, provokes, identifies the bad guy and enables you to work through a confrontation. Both of you know that although this might appear to others to be a fight, it is something entirely different: you are both serving each other and the team by giving voice to what is unvoiced and blocking. Through such confrontations, you close the gap, strengthen the relationships, enliven and energise the team. Without such confrontations, the unvoiced pressures increase over time, leaking out inappropriately as bitterness, cynicism and passive aggression, and ultimately demand expression through unexpected, unhelpful and possibly violent means.

 

Hmmm! Now my pacifism includes the possibility of a useful fight! But there is a purpose to confrontation. It is not about needless aggression, violence or bullying. It is about creating conditions for voicing what is unvoiced, including what is being left out, reaching out and making contact.

Test 9 The spontaneous shift

 

There are no bad guys! We know this, and in spite of knowing these we sometimes experience absolute certainty about another person being bad and wrong. Sometimes we allow ourselves a fight, voicing what was held in…. and here we encounter a difficulty. Will it become a fight to the death? Do we get taken over by an overwhelming need to obliterate them and end their life? This is what frightens me when my children fight. I'm afraid first that one of them will be hurt and, as often happens, it will end in tears. Second I'm afraid about their developing a taste for fighting that seeks ever more violent and destructive expression, starting perhaps with cartoons, computer games, BB guns, then crossing the line into real life armed conflicts. In trying to keep the peace I end up fighting them, sucked into that ridiculous downward spiral. Seems to happen too in the world at large doesn't it? Look at the struggles of the United Nations forces in Bosnia, Indonesia and Africa. Feels like trying to stop the river.

 

But there is another way of looking at this, which again arises from my work with Andy Harmon and the Here Be Dragons workshop last autumn. This is that if you and your opponent give yourself up 100% to the fight, then something spontaneously will happen. A seismic shift will occur: a moment when you both at the same instant stop…. and notice what is happening… maybe laugh at yourself and the situation… put down your weapons… take off your warrior masks and see each other anew, as people who depend upon each other, who need to be partners, sharing responsibility for moving the situation forward.

 

If you apply harsh tests to this proposition, such as the fighting between rioters and police in Bradford, or Ireland, or Israel, or the war against terrorism, I am not sure it completely stands up in every situation. Getting to the transformative moment is not by any means guaranteed and requires both good timing and considerable outside help. However I'm becoming convinced that a fight is often necessary on the path to an answer.

 

So I want you to hold onto the idea that if you avoid the fight altogether, you may block the path to a lasting solution. I want you to re-examine how you fight, and to be open to redefining your purpose in fighting.

 

Your test this time will be about your ability to disengage from the fight, to step back at the right moment, letting go when all your instincts and reflexes are demanding you stay in the ring, slugging it out. See the pattern. Rejoin with your opponent as partner. Stop destroying each other. Choose what to create together. In other words let a spontaneous shift occur.

 

The big difficulty with fighting is it heats you up, narrows your attention thus blinding you, and promises resolution through producing a winner and a loser. The special wisdom you will bring to the fight is: it is not what it appears to be. There will be no winner and no loser. There is no good guy and gad guy. The winning is in the voicing of what was previously unvoiced, getting the truth out of both of you. When you both simultaneously seize the moment to disengage this releases energy, shifts responsibility and transforms the situation.

 

In the heat of the fight this special wisdom often gets forget, and opponents are tempted to slug it out to the death. You may forget you are playing something out here. Insults wrongly taken may inflict lasting damage, putting holes in a neighbour's fence which are not easily repaired.

 

How can you make sure you disengage? There are no easy answers here. At home I notice that when without realising I have become stressed and heated up, usually over something trivial like the cap not being on the toothpaste tube or the dishwasher not being unloaded, my 11 year old daughter catches me with a simple expression: "chill pill Dad!". Works brilliantly. Snaps me out of it, with only slight annoyance at her cheek! So I suppose I'm wondering if we couldn't give chill pills out during confrontations in the workplace, and I notice that in a sense this is what facilitators do: they make it safe for people to have little spats and skirmishes in workshops, knowing the facilitator can supply chill pills.

 

Here is an example from the health and fitness executive team you met earlier. It is an example of how voicing what is unvoiced can release energy and transform a situation. The role of the facilitator was not to get between the "fighters", but to assist them in getting their voices out and reacted to by the other one.

______________

 

X:

We said that this team sets the culture for the company. But of the 9 of us here, only 5 of us will have to live with what we decide going forward, I think we should face up to this

 

Y:

(loud, aggressive) I object to that. I have to carry this out into the wider company

 

X:

That's not what I'm saying. I'm saying P is leaving in November, Q is going overseas, R is being replaced with S…

 

Y:

(cutting in) You're not listening to me. I'm saying…

 

X:

(talking over): What I'm saying is…

 

Facilitator:

Hold it!  Stop! Slow down! You've both missed each other twice now. Y tell me, what is your message to X. I'm going to ask X to listen and play back what you're saying before responding. Y.

 

Y:

I'm saying that I do have to carry forward what we decide here

 

X:

Y is saying he does have to carry this forward.

 

Facilitator:

What's you're reaction to this X?

 

X:

Well I accept that but what I've really been trying to say is Y has only been to 3 of our meetings in the last 2 years, so does our code of conduct really concern him?

 

Y:

I thought we all decided I would only come to the meetings when required for a specific reason

 

X:

Did we? I don't remember that. And what I'm saying is I want your input at all our meetings

 

Y:

Ahh. (sighing). I thought you didn't want me here, and you were trying to keep me out.

 

X:

No

 

Facilitator:

So Y, far from being pushed aside as you believed, X is drawing you in

OK. I feel much better now.

______________

 

At the end, the two "fighters" who believed they were being straight forward and direct with one another, have realised they were speaking without listening, and were missing one another completely. Behind their surface discussion was an unvoiced need which when voiced could be included and responded to. This created the spontaneous shift to "we" and the voices of the two players started supporting one another and moving together.

 

Here the fighters needed a little assistance. Starting from good intent, with passion for their work and wanting to communicate honestly, they habitually find themselves fighting and they sort of lose each other and their intent on the way. Put another way they seek to "contact" and get through to each other but they fail. The aggression seeking "contact" or understanding kicked down the neighbour's and failed to get it, producing aggression back. The facilitation brought both parties to the fence without violence and restored "contact".

 

This situation gives a clear brief for the facilitator and the leader. Our job is to get through to people and to assist them in getting through to each other. Through assisting this kind of "contact",  you build trust, relationship, partnership and shared intent. You create a team. We have used the term "Voice", giving and receiving voice, as a shorthand for "getting through".

 

It's not all about fighting to bring the voices up. It's to do with providing space too. One difficulty for a leader or facilitator is in working to the meeting agenda: giving voice almost by definition allows people to depart on tangents from the agenda. How do you decide when to allow or disallow a person's voice?

 

This depends on what kind of meeting: a quick daily or weekly operations review, or an annual teambuilding, or a quarterly strategy session. Whichever kind of meeting the facilitator seeks to provide a clearing, enough space for voices to come out, and for people to react to one another. Somewhere amongst all the formal meetings you need to make spaces for unvoiced stuff to come up, not to pack every agenda and stick tightly to it. When you do it can be surprising what comes out, and often it's really important stuff.

 

Here's an example of someone in a struggling retail organisation speaking into one of those spaces. Even she maybe did not know she was carrying this heavy load …

______________

 

It was going to take 3 weeks, but it's 10 weeks already and no announcements yet. I do know my part of the company is being split up and disbanded. It's so awful. Having to treat people like this. Like letting go of something. It's not that we used to be so wonderful, or that I'm losing my department, or not knowing if I'll be given another job, or even the prospect of leaving after 21 years as I'll get a good enough package….it's the loss of something else…. Or the belief that we could get through this…and it's the loss of a dream. It's become such a strain to go through this every day and I'm just not sure now there's space for me to be me there anymore.

______________

 

As soon as she had tearfully voiced this, the person seemed happier and lighter and to know more clearly what she wanted to do. My point is no one knows what is there waiting to be spoken, blocking the path forward, until you give space for it, then the unvoiced pushes up, claims the space and a shift can happen.

 

The divisional president in a chemical company told me how he hit by accident on the need to provide informal space for people to speak:

______________

 

People normally trust me quite well. They believe  me, if I say something. I am not an actor. I am pretty honest and people feel this. But a couple of years ago we had trust issues in the exec committee, we also had it in my division and in my team. There were reports of people hating each other and fighting. This was a big problem, lots of this is mine and this is yours.

 

We established principles and put up a big chart: do's and don'ts in meetings.  Don’t shoot from the hip… the interest of the division ranks higher than the business segment - some very important things which people sometimes forget . But then I had an idea, I don't know where it came from.

 

I said to my management team of ten or so, every Monday morning at 8.30 am come to my office, we will have coffee but no agenda. This was amazing. Very very successful. I never expected so many people to come. People come running in to my office, I don’t have enough chairs but this environment is important, they even come there now when I'm away. Some talk about holidays, problems with kids, how to buy a good car. Sometimes it develops a little bit into a business meeting because I asked everybody what is new here or somebody has some problems, somebody has a big opportunity, personnel matters, business matters, competition.  There are thousands of topics which we discuss. I can also release news from the executive committee immediately. 

 

There are no minutes and everybody talks then just very loose. I ask people do you  want to say something or do you have maybe a problem, whatever. Everybody has something to tell us. Then people felt we need to have minutes because I am unable to come next Monday.  I said "no, no", this would have moved us from improvised to organised and taking the informality away from it, so we didn’t. Without minutes and it is interesting because it develops not only into small talk but we really interesting things sometimes.

 

They just felt this is great. There was never a challenge why is he doing this.  I can’t even tell you why I started this.

 

So I translated this into the executive committee and said let’s have lunch together and no agenda. We had many good and convincing discussions. OK it is not always feasible, it is not every week and sometimes there are only three people, sometimes four. It creates an environment where people are much more trustful and open, even telling each other "this is wrong what you do, it is really wrong and you should think about it".  In an official meeting and an agenda item I think it is more difficult to develop this openness, trustfulness.

 

Some of my sub groups have started to do an afternoon tea without agenda so it seems this idea  has gained ground.

 

It was maybe the best decision I made, and people still come every Monday morning!

______________

 

Once something is voiced it lets the spontaneous shift happen. In this example the culture from the executive committee down through the whole division shifted a little.

 

To explain how to enable the spontaneous shift, I want to show you the map I use which derives from the Gestalt school (See Ed Nevis in references). It assumes there is a shape to human experience, and we experience things in a cycle.

 

When you or I or anyone wakes up to the desire to do anything, there is a sequence of events. This applies to any human need arising and being addressed, whether I am cold and need to put the heating on, hungry and want food, tired and want to sleep, dirty and want to wash, or needing for some reason to talk to someone. The cycle is like this:

_______________

 

1.      You're relaxed and open to something new but nothing is happening

2.      You feel something drawing your attention (eg. you shiver, or your stomach rumbles, or your eyes flicker….) but maybe don't know why

3.      You realise what you need (heat, food, sleep…)

4.      You figure out a way to meet your need (light fire, buy food, lie down & close eyes…)

5.      You do it, making direct "contact" with the world outside you, taking in what you need (heat, food, sleep…)

6.      You feel satisfied, or maybe frustrated still and a further need arises in your awareness, or maybe you just review in your mind what happened, find the meaning, then let go and move on.

7.      Back to 1.

_______________

 

Since we're exploring voicing, facilitating and leading, our focus here is on a particular kind of need: not heat, food, or sleep, but the need people have to talk with one another. Just think of all the reasons you need to talk to people: to invite, gossip, plan, problem-solve, or just have their company… the list goes on. Notice how we all have our own habits and patterns of talking: some of us more insular, others needing to be with people more often, some needing seriously to talk a lot of thoughts through, others happier with jokes, banter or gentle chat.

 

In your workplace all these people with all these different talking habits may be present. What all these people have in common is they work for your organisation, and there is a "relationship of interdependency": each person needs some things from the organisation (money, tools, information, support…) and the organisation needs some things from them (results, creativity, teamwork…). Although you could say an organisation doesn't really exist, only people do, I tend to think of an organisation as a cluster of needs and relationships, a lot of them unvoiced, and yes the needs and relationships occur between people. As leader you are in a special relationship, in some way representing the organisation to your people, and providing or denying them resources they need.

 

Difficulties happen when the relationship does not help needs to get voiced and met, and we saw earlier how many stuck conversations there are in which these kind of difficulties are arising. Our work as facilitators and leaders is to unstick, to develop relationships that enable voicing of needs. Our work is to get the unvoiced voiced, to get unmet needs discussed, negotiated, met.

 

In terms of the 7 step cycle what is unvoiced arises as a feeling and a need, leading to contact which may or may not fulfil the need. The fighting in the health and fitness team was leading to frustration, an unintended interruption to contact. The facilitation between the two fighters enabled "contact" between them, the unvoiced to be voiced and received, then we had a sense of satisfaction, completion and readiness to move on. Sometimes a bit of a fight helps to bring the need up strongly enough for what was buried and behind the defences, difficult or embarrassing to say, to be said and responded to. Sometimes providing the space is enough. Sometimes a leader or facilitator needs to notice the block and do a little of the to-ing and fro-ing voice work (as with the two fighters example above) to restore the contact.

 

Once you have the idea of the need to voice what is unvoiced, then as a facilitator and leader, you have a new awareness, a special wisdom that enables you to read the invisible stuckness in a situation, and know what needs to happen to restore "contact", to release energy, to shift talking into responsibility-taking, to transform what is happening, to open up a new path.

 

Voicing what is unvoiced leads to a spontaneous shift. As facilitators and leaders we know this, and direct our efforts to bring this about.

Test 10: Awakening your team

 

Your final test in this workout relates to your team. Your team can be a crucible for change: a small enough group of people to awaken, experiment with, introduce your proposition, start to grow a new adaptive culture.

 

In this moment, as I'm writing this, I notice I'm quite serious about the importance of teams being well set up and I hope you will be too after doing this test.

 

The word team is often used loosely. It covers all sorts of different groups from a football team, through a board of directors in a public service organisation, to a pack of sales people in a chocolate company. I think of the "work team" as the building block of any organisation. Strong, healthy teams make strong, healthy organisations. An organisation without teams tends to be fragmented and vulnerable in a more and more competitive world.

 

You can't really see or touch a team, but you can feel its heart beat. We talk about team spirit. A team means something to its members: there is a sense of being bonded, "in it together", belonging. Like a mirror, a team shows each member who they are:

-        That they exist as an individual (not a number)

-        That they belong to a unit larger than themselves

These twin sources of sustenance, make you feel strong and your team feel strong. Voice creates this sustenance: speaking out and being heard in the echoes coming back.

 

The power of a team is in the in-built tension between the pull of the individual agendas and the pull of the shared group agenda. Voice is the working through of this tension into productive energy.

 

Many work teams are toxic: seriously unhealthy, driving people quietly mad, making them feel cut-off, manic, depressed, paranoid, obsessive, compulsive, psychopathic. A toxic team's toxic influence can spread far out into the wider organisation and its community of stakeholders. Toxicity arises from failing to meet the needs and values of individuals, in other words by rules and habits of communication which stifle, ignore and distort the voices of members. The whole becomes less than the sum of the parts. You get negative synergy. One plus one equals minus five.

 

As your team develops its Voice it becomes stronger and healthier, and can offer a beacon of light, inspiring and giving direction to the wider organisation.

 

Work teams usually have leaders (like you) and often work with facilitators (like me) whose job it is to awaken and develop the team's potential. Here are a few examples of how to do this before setting your final workout test.

 

Fiona Coffey and I have worked with several public sector teams, in the Home Office, various government agencies, and the Department of Health. Working as facilitators supporting change efforts in these organisations, we have experienced extremes of toxicity and good health in teams.

 

One board of directors, let's call it organisation T, was a model of toxicity. Monthly meetings were a shambles with an unruly group of 15 or so very senior and experienced people sitting far apart round a big boardroom table shouting, arguing, ganging up, blaming, attacking and undermining the executives who work for them, humiliating the staff who came to present reports. They all were leaders of much more effective teams themselves but in spite of this seemed to have trapped themselves in a toxic mess. We were able last November to create a significant shift in the space of one and a half days. How?

 

We helped the Board review its own role in Organisation T (using standards published by the Institute of Directors), define the role and core processes of Organisation T in its wider system, and shape the future work programme for the next 5 years. How did we do all that? We took charge, developed a very clear agenda, led the 10 participants who had bothered to turn up strictly through the agenda by the nose. The mood at the end was upbeat. The sense of achievement was uplifting. WE were told it was their best ever Board meeting.

 

As a second example, the Executive group in the same organisation, one tier down from the Board, did not prove as amenable. We spent almost a year working with them on and off and there was little sign of progress at the end. Why? They needed firm leadership from the Board above them which had until recently been floundering itself. The Chairman was retiring could do little to improve things in hi last few months. The new Chairman seemed to have a different conflicting agendas from the current Chief Executive. Key stakeholders had a low opinion of the Executive, were hostile to them and some wanted the OrganisationT to fail.

 

We tried our best to engender a sense of responsibility in the Executive in several face to face meetings including a special workshop involving stakeholders. But nothing worked. Without clearer parameters and strong support from the Board and the Chairman, the Executive were confused and demoralised. Writing this I'm sorry to admit that as facilitators we could not help the Executive no matter what we tried: much stronger direction needed to come from the leaders above them. This sounds rather old fashioned and against the spirit of teamwork, but what I'm saying is it demonstrates strong leadership is needed to create a clearing in which teamwork is possible.

 

In a third example, the senior management team in another department, let's call it organisation H, managed both to engender a remarkable team spirit themselves and also to spread their influence across a whole organisation of 200 or so highly intelligent (and by nature cynical) civil servants, then out into a wider stakeholder community spread across different organisations throughout Britain. How was this achieved. Sue, the director of organisation H was tasked with delivering one of the government aims. She realised this was challenging and was somewhat daunted by it, particularly as her 200 odd staff were feeling somewhat neglected, understaffed, over-worked and more interested in getting through their existing work than lifting their heads to pay attention to a new challenge.

 

Sue asked us to work first with her team of 6 or so direct reports. We facilitated a workshop which produced strong commitment and a plan going forwards for large group meetings, bringing around 50 staff at a time into contact with outside stakeholders.

 

The initial top team workshop was remarkable. In the beautiful surroundings of a country house we covered a serious work agenda interspersed with lots of fun and games. We heard stories from participants of times when they had been personally challenged:  a moral dilemma over a friend stealing milk bottles, handling the death of a friend's child, earning respect as a female in a male dominated organisation…and a side-splitting hilarious epic story from a past governor of Dartmoor prison.  We played outdoor team games, swarming across the manicured lawns, chasing each other like children. There were party pieces, jokes, poems and a soul searching discussion of leadership over dinner. Through all this frivolity it became clear the team was bonded and up for it. You could feel its heartbeat getting stronger.

 

Gradually a new, more open, team-based culture was spreading in organisation H, creating a new sense of momentum. We were starting to notice we had a real success on our hands when suddenly we were brought up short by an extraordinary incident in the last of the four large group meetings. Someone stood up and in heartfelt voice said this:

________________

 

This feels like yet another initiative. The last 7 or 8 initiatives have failed to arrive. New staff promised to us have not turned up. Things haven't happened. It's another initiative. Why dress it up as something different. It feels like this is coming from the top of the office, but not in a way we can understand. We need more clarity, and to know if we will be given the tools to do the job.

 

We are playing along with this because we have been told to but when we go back to the office it makes no difference because we are already doing what we need to do. It seems like another excuse to give us more to do for less reward. We need decent pay rises to reflect increasing responsibilities, career progression, training and develop and new IT systems to enhance work not cut support staff.

 

We are proud to be civil servants. We value the jobs we do. We are committed to public service, democracy, our staff  and colleagues, human rights, fairness and integrity. Are these values shared by this programme?

________________

 

The person sat down to a spontaneous round of applause, followed by silence, then someone said "OK facilitators, pull it together!". We said nothing, then someone asked then how we felt and if we were surprised. One of us said surprised no, but upset that the group had not found more value in the workshop. We learnt afterwards that this speech had been a watershed, helping to bring things to a head. Voice had drawn voice. Many of those who had been clapping, realised afterwards their true feelings were more trusting than rebellious, that the programme was not just another clever plot to make them work harder, that resources were being provided and that the new priorities and ways of working made real sense. The beacon was shining brightly.

 

In all three examples we were trying to get a team to wake up to its "real work", then to take responsibility, re-ordering its priorities, reaching outwards to make a difference, delivering benefits to customers and stakeholders. This simple aim is often blocked, just as a conversation can be blocked. Once blocked, the conversations and behaviours are contradictory, ungrounded, undirected, lacking focus and fail to make sense, as if we're trying to play football, but no one knows what the ball is, let alone where it is.

 

To unblock in the three examples above, we employed a whole variety of tools and approaches, some of which worked dramatically well, and some which failed, but underlying these we were learning about the conditions we need to create, as facilitators and leaders, for a team to wake up to its "real work".

 

We don't start with a team. First we need a leader. This might not be fashionable, because everyone is talking about teams these days, but it is what we've found. We identify who is the leader and what is the "real work" of the team today. This means we help the leader develop their proposition (look back at Test 7). Refining and developing the proposition we ask: what are the givens, the fixed parameters, the non-negotiables? These are matters the leader needs to think through and decide on, often in consultation with outside stakeholders.

 

Some people talk of this as providing the team with a steel umbrella, to protect them from bullets raining down from above. I prefer to think in terms of a parasol. If the parasol is too high then the team gets burned up in the harsh glaring sun.  This happens when the givens are not clearly stated, making the team become confused, hot headed, anxious, defensive and fragmented, unable to find its real work. When the parasol is too low it cramps the team, restricting their gaze: this happens when the leader makes the givens too narrow and restrictive. As a result the team will not be challenged, and tends to becomes childish and dependent on the leader, failing to demonstrate its fuller potential. The leader has to exercise judgement to frame the proposition and givens properly in-between as an enabling structure providing just enough certainty, but allowing also enough latitude, thus creating a challenging enough space for the team to add its own value, finding its own creative energy, excitement and intelligent answers.

 

Once the proposition is framed, you start working with the team. I have become enthusiastic about the potential of Dialogue because people have been through a few workshops tend to fall into robotic, complacent rituals, saying what they think needs to be said, playing the facilitators' games. Through Dialogue you aim to get through to real honesty. You bring the team together, face to face, in a circle, without formal agenda, papers or flipcharts. What do you do then? You find out what is in their hearts and minds, surfacing the beliefs and assumptions that are driving their talk and actions. How? You use everything you've learnt here in this workout. You tune up your awareness of inner reactions, outer responses and the gap. You voice your proposition. Draw out their voice. Fight at bit. Raise the level of contact and enable the spontaneous shifts to happen. Notice and respond to the energy levels.

 

During the Dialogue process the facilitator is slowing things down so that all the voices in the team get heard by the others and responded to. The process is not all harmonious, and the facilitator deliberately brings out disagreement and dissonance.

 

William Isaacs has described four key moves of a facilitator in a Dialogue session which become in time known and adopted by all in the team:

__________________

 

1.      INITIATE = initiating a proposal for conversation

2.      FOLLOW = agreeing to and supporting the proposal

3.      OPPOSE = challenging what is being said

4.      BYSTAND = with one foot in and one foot out of the conversation, describing what they are seeing and hearing

__________________

 

A healthy dialogue consists of all four moves being used in balance. Typically there are mixed moves with an Initiate like "Let's go to the beach today" hiding an unspoken Oppose underneath which is "And let's not do what you wanted to do". The idea is to become conscious of the tone and the unvoiced dimension which is preventing a deeper level of contact. Typically one or more of the four positions is silenced through a consensus that says, for example: "Opposing is wrong", or "Initiating is the leader's job". Each member of a team may be happier in one or another position, maybe stuck in a position and feel weak in certain positions.

 

Once you have these four moves in mind you'll quickly notice your patterns and preferences and those of people around you. When I'm with Helen, she is most likely to Initiate and Oppose, while I Follow and Bystand. It doesn't mean this is what we always do, and in fact it feels good to me when one of us does something unexpected drawing a fresh move from the other.

 

My son was sitting round our dining table with seven or eight friend and I noticed a spontaneous game arising between them: someone says something, then there is silence, then everyone responds with a slowly spoken "WWW HHH AAA TTT?", leaving the speaker feeling momentarily isolated. Cruel sort of teenage boys game giving each person a strong dose of peer pressure! In terms of these four moves, a person Initiates, then there is a long pause with silence and no moves. The pause could be seen as everyone Bystanding, before then simultaneously Opposing. The absence of anyone Following leaves the Initiator isolated.

 

The dialogue in a team might fail to arise because the parasol has been set too high or too low and the facilitator can help the leader respond to this. The daunting emptiness of the agenda-less Dialogue meeting can sometimes spook a team, then the facilitator can provide an activity which eases the team into heartfelt discussion.

 

In easing the team into dialogue, apart from initiating an activity, the facilitator often needs to play bystander, holding up the mirror to the team. This involves commenting on what they see happening and inviting a response. For example:

__________________

 

Two or three of you have been doing all the talking so far. What do the rest of you think?

 

Have you noticed how we have drifted away from the question?

 

The team's energy seems very low now. What is going on?

 

Each time someone makes a proposal, it either gets ignored or defeated with a yes but…

__________________

 

In due course, a special moment comes when everyone feels heard, they feel they exist and belong as individuals to a team that seems to know itself, what its work is and what it stands for. This is a bit like an orchestra starting to make music: the voices moving forwards with some energy together, after a discordant and directionless period of tuning up and rehearsal.

 

A second special moment occurs during Dialogue when the team's energy naturally moves to the Action question: "what are we going to do about it then?". Here we look for what Fernando Flores called powerful speech acts: words and stories that are intentional, that translate into deeds. This means bold, committed Promises and clear Requests. Flores gives an example. After a day and a half developing a bold promise to cut construction time in half and save $10m in a loss making firm, Flores criticises the team leader:

_____________

 

Let me challenge you. In my opinion you are still complacent. You say you have the best people, and yet you are still losing the war. How is it possible that the best troops in the world are losing the war? That's not an interesting story. Winning the war with the worst troops, as in Dirty Dozen, now that's an interesting story. One element is missing from all your stories: you have to be willing to risk your identity for a bigger future than the present that you are living. Ten millions dollars isn't that much. I think the real number you should shoot for is $30m, and I'm a conservative guy!

_____________

 

Alongside producing a powerful story and a bold promise in your team, we work on the relationships in the team. One ritual I use is a group 360 degree feedback process called the Fishbowl. This recognises that exceptional team performance is rests on strong, truthful, transparent communications amongst team members. They take turns in the "hot seat" to hear their colleagues' assessments and requests of them. Although team members tend to be cautious the first time they undertake this, it can produce a strong bonding and uplifting effect, and if repeated as a practice in the team clears up misunderstandings, lets go of emotional baggage, and sets a team on a path to ever more accurate communication of requests and promises. The team's heart beat gets stronger as a result. They get more trustful, more confident and less anxious.

 

You may have noticed this work happens face to face not over email. That's because only through this "wide bandwidth" real time contact is it powerful.

 

Why do I feel so strongly about teams? Teams are all about a wonderful feeling, but more too: about us being better together than we can be on our own. The magic ingredient is heart. When a team is strong, it is because each team member has placed their trust and their belief in it. In a sense they have entrusted their heart to the team. This is an act that is both courageous and scary. If the team breaks up, or fails, or rejects a person, then they feel a deep hurt, literally a broken heart.

 

Since I have been a small child I have found it very hard to really join and entrust my heart to a team, and perhaps this is why I am drawn to my facilitator work, on the edge of teams, rather than in them. For almost a year I was in a truly remarkable work team, and I woke up each morning looking forward to meeting my colleagues again and getting on with the fresh challenges we faced. It felt exciting and worthwhile, but I became concerned after a while about some of the methods my colleagues were using and I did not feel invited to voice these concerns. It started to feel more and more like a cult, with a right/wrong ideology. I felt subtly imprisoned, tricked, manipulated. A new job offer was the excuse I needed to move on. Maybe one day I'll find a team even I can trust.

 

The facilitator and leader work is about growing the contact and the trust between people, enabling the team to become stronger, more confident, more passionate and more intelligent.
