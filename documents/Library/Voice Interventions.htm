THE VOICE INTERVENTIONS Q&A

 

These questions and responses were prepared for a workshop called Facilitating The Inner Voice which Julia Williams and I developed in association with the University of Surrey.

 

What’s the context?

We’re working in a pressure cooker! Faster results. Improved performance through change including new flexible working, virtual teams, outsourcing, hot-desking, home working. But at a price. Managers say tensions are running high:

-         83% consistently working longer than contracted hours

-         79% report increased workplace conflict

-         60% report increased office politics

(Source: Roffey Park annual survey, Management Agenda 2003).

 

What does this mean for you as a facilitator?

More demand for your services, but more at stake too!

 

Can you give some examples of what happens?

A school aspiring to raise its results with new shorter lessons but an extra class each day, a new behaviour policy, a raft of new initiatives..

… parents are concerned about inconsistent homework and  pupils have complaints too (but enjoy coasting!) while teachers and heads of departments feel huge pressure, a lack of appreciation and another initiative coming. The sheer level of challenge fills everyone with anxiety and doubt.

 

Similarly in a new Primary Care Trust that is commissioning services from local hospitals, in a hotel that is sharing services in a cluster, on a large-scale IT project in an oil company and in every organisation today. People hit barriers as they try to make even simple things happen. Small disappointments and frustrations give rise to flare-ups, blame and undiscussable tensions that break people and teams apart.

 

It’s complex and paradoxical. We’re more conscious than ever of the need to join-up, but people are actually feeling isolated, devalued and lack of respect. Each person is trying to hold it all together: to serve their job, team, organisation and outside stakeholders, to deliver objectives while getting their own needs met.

 

What shall we focus on?

Moments when others look to you. Difficult and decisive moments such as:

-         participants in a meeting are getting troublesome

-         hot (gender, ethnic, religious, power, harassment) issues surface

-         trust has broken down and neglected relationships needs to be renewed

-         something is being avoided, energy is low and no one wants to talk about it

-         a new partnership needs to be forged between people who seem too busy.

 

What do you aim for in such moments (beyond surviving the next 5 minutes!)?

-         To get an agreed decision?

-         To release some energy and channel it positively?

-         To heal wounds, strengthen people?

-         To put back missing respect and create a will to join up?

-         To bring isolated individuals into an energetic, proactive community?

-         To provoke insight into patterns and avoid dependency?

-         To build an organisation that is resilient and adaptable?

 

Why “voice”?

Voice is a metaphor – providing a different way of looking at facilitator work in organisations that is handy when the situation is complex and emotional. It is powerful because:

-         To let a person have their voice, asking for their views, ideas, feelings, needs, is possibly one of the strongest signs of respect we can give them.

-         To “find your voice” is to come into your power and passion. From speaking your truth and being heard, you gain relief and meet your core needs for validation (“I exist and I really matter”) and belonging (“I’m part of something bigger”).

 

How does one person’s need for voice fit the wider interests of the organisation?

No problem because an organisation comprised of people without voice is fragmented, weak and inefficient. Not to allow a person their voice is to diminish, exclude and disrespect them. We depend on each person to:

-         Make strong connections with customers and colleagues

-         Offer ideas for products and improvements

-         Inspire, guide and unlock potential in others.

 

What can this tell us about the role of leader, facilitator and others?

Voice reminds us that a strong, vibrant organisation evolves continuously through a conversation moving to and fro between stakeholders, keeping individuals motivated and aligned. Leaders, individual stakeholders and facilitators all have a part to play in keeping this conversation going:

 

-         A leader’s role is to speak up as “steward” for the organisation as a whole whilst reflecting the needs and contributions of each stakeholder.

 

-         An individual stakeholder speaks up for the needs and potential of their particular group. When stakeholders have complaints (perhaps about stress, workload, isolation, colleagues, moving goalposts or low morale), this points to a broken conversation, or in other words, a failure of voice.

 

-         A facilitator highlights failures of voice and sustains the continuing conversation moving to and fro between stakeholders and with their leaders.

 

Isn’t this really about listening?

Much emphasis in recent years has been given to “taking-in” processes (listening, responding to external change, serving customers/shareholders better, signing up to corporate systems/values/goals/rules). Without permission to “give out” what is heartfelt and true, people feel obligated, stressed and put upon.

 

By bringing your attention to Voicing alongside Listening, you tap into buried energy and reveal practical guidelines for catalysing effective communication, fulfilling partnerships and stronger performance.

 

Is this really new?

No. Voicing is not a new idea, but it raises crucial questions for the facilitator to address:

  1. What can we learn from the ancient process of voicing?
  2. How do we mistakenly violate this wisdom today?
  3. What facilitator interventions are most useful?

 

 

1. What can we learn from the ancient process of voicing?

______________________________________________

 

 “Native Americans often experience the discourse of non-Indians as insistently pushing toward monologue.  They resent being interrupted by people who do not recognize the moments of silence that punctuate a speaker's narrative. 

Scollon & Scollon 1979 

“Kalahari Bushman society simply does not lend itself to a centralized, hierarchical structure with specialized personnel. The band has no chief or leader. Decisions affecting the social life of the band are arrived at through discussions in which all adult and near-adult members of the band, irrespective of whether they are male or female, are welcome to participate. Discussion is informal, is not conducted in any special place, and seldom takes the form of a single, set-piece debate.” AJGM Sanders

“In Balamah, a small West African village, 500 people managed themselves for 4 days with no planning committee in a highly organised, satisfactory and enjoyable fashion. Part of the secret was that the village was laid out in a circle with an open space in the middle. Circles create communication. Place people in rows and it is clear who will talk and who must listen. A circle has no head or foot, no high or low, no sides to take; people can simply be with each other face to face. After all we do not have a square of friends!”   Harrison Owen, Open space Technology

“Hunter gatherers typically lived in groups of 20-40. From time to time the tribe met in a circle. They just talked and talked, apparently to no purpose. They made no decisions. There was no leader. And everybody could participate. There may have been wise men or women who were listened to a bit more –  but everybody could talk. The meeting went on, until it finally seemed to stop for no reason and the group dispersed. Yet after that everybody seemed to know what to do, because they understood each other so well. Then they could get together in small groups and do something or decide things.”   David Bohm, On Dialogue

 

“A community is a group that deals with its own issues - its own shadow. Community doesn't look any different wherever in the world it is. The problem is to get the people into the room, stay there and not walk out. The people in an organization have to be willing to temporarily lay aside their role and their rank. Decisions are arrived at without voting, through a process whereby the issues are fully aired and all members feel that they have been adequately heard. It's much easier among unsophisticated people: a group of diplomats or psychiatrists are really tough, because you have to penetrate their sophistication to get to their innocence.”

Scott Peck

 

New Intermediaries is an experiment harking back to older traditions…

Participants say they appreciate: a palpable, visceral, distinctive feeling, you feel part of something, shared values, the openness of everyone, meetings without agendas, ease, different world views, "buzz”, curiosity, making connections and moving on, diversity of people and ideas, room to explore/risk, informal – emergence, it's on the edge, the chance to share and learn.

 

Some principles implicit in these traditions ….

 

-        Holistic, fluid and organic. Everyone is interested in building not undermining the health of the community. Minimal structure needed. What happens emerges from what is present in people’s minds when they meet.

 

-         Hospitality. All voices need an equal place at the (round) table so that nothing is lost. Suspension of roles and hierarchy. Sharing information. None of us has a monopoly on the truth.

 

-         Respecting silence. Paying attention and being present = contributing to what is happening as witness. Wait until “moved to speak” (eg. Quaker meetings). Nothing is wasted. Everything counts. If we look and listen long enough we find the gold.

 

-        Heart to heart, rather than head to head. Speaking your truth, emptying yourself and opening your heart to others’ truths which although different or apparently contradictory you accept as part of the larger picture.

 

-         Contrariness and challenge. Allowed to speak what is true for you however contrary to the tribe’s central mores or ethics. Speaking the unthinkable, challenging the norms, keeping everyone “awake”. (Native American Heoyokah or “Riding the horse backwards” = crazy wisdom).  Similar to Court Jester in medieval France and Brujo in South American tribes.

 

-         Facing conflict and difficulty. When 2 people have a dispute – disharmony in one relationship reflects a wider disharmony, or can lead to it. Nip it in the bud by bringing the 2 people before the whole community where they speak their piece; the community as a whole then decides what the 2 people must do to repair the situation (Buddhist. Sangha = the spirit of community). An “offender” sits in the middle of the circle and all feel the enormity of the crime together, the pain and separation it has caused, and heal it by sitting with it, by being together.

 

-        Time. As long as it takes to complete, to become of one mind.

 

-         Integrity. Like segments in an orange, like coloured lights combining to make white, like musical notes making a chord, when all voices have been fully heard a conversation is complete. Everyone knows everything so we have integrity: both in the group and in each person. Each person contains within them the full picture, the power and wisdom of the whole group so can lead and represent the group.

 

-         Consensus. Once integrity has been achieved, a decision tends to arise naturally for the greater good of the community, respecting all that has been said. People present can accept, live with and support the group’s decision.

 

 

 

2. How do we mistakenly violate this wisdom today?

______________________________________________

 

Typically:

 

We inhibit life. We impose complicated rules. We suppress contrariness. Orthodoxies and heresies – corporate-speak such as mission/values/vision treated as orthodoxy creating its opposite: heresy (which cannot be spoken about), and fear. We don’t want terrorists here. Compliance with dominant ideology – don’t disagree with the boss, the company values etc. Lip service given to challenge – but applause for “team players”.

 

We fill the silence with noise. Ritual presentations – taking place when presenters have little to say directly and audience have little curiosity = little relationship. Multi-tasking, side conversations, interruptions during meetings alongside forced concentration and struggles to follow groundrules.

 

We rush. “Decisions” and “action” in preference to talking - pretence of progress, busy fools, obsession with results, forced decisions often not supported afterwards.

 

We deny conflict. Masking with banter, leaking as toxic cynicism and passive aggression or dealt with off-line. We protect people from each other, say don’t be negative, don’t deal with differences openly in the circle.

 

We think small. Short term, self interest rules, survival of the fittest, rules of the jungle/market. Pretend to be a team player, but neglect the long-term good of the community. Know whose side you’re on. Aggressive competition is good. Context and inter-dependency is ignored. Respect and trust is damaged. Reality is driven underground.

 

We dominate or defer. Some feel more encouraged to speak, others less. Some feel bullied and afraid of consequences of speaking out (bad performance review, sidelined in next reorganisation). People being “managed” as if they are not in control of themselves. Those doing the managing have huge power over lives of others. We’re attuned to very subtle signs of power (such as Who listens to whom? Style of speech. Body language. Room arrangement). Vulnerable people look for strong parent figures to protect them.

 

We exclude people. The ultimate wound used routinely as part of command and control toolkit. Invite the movers and shakers, listen to your in-group, spend time with supporters, leave others out, blame those absent, victimise and scapegoat the non-performers, cut out the dead wood.

 

We fragment into silos. As relationships with outsiders get strained, we put up the shutters, retreat behind barriers. Reduced communication with stakeholders, reduced respect and erosion of trust. Politics, fear, unequal spread of information and responsibility. Hide your failures, spin, polish your image. Need to know basis.

We’re doing our job, what about others?

 

3. What facilitator interventions are most useful?

______________________________________________

 

The facilitator’s intervention is to sustain a conversation moving to and fro, drawing out the authentic voices, opening people’s hearts and minds to one another. As a summary this usually means:

 

1.  Slowing down

2.  Receiving the individual voices that are present in the group/community

3.  Letting the collective voice of the group/community emerge

4.  Bringing the group’s voice forward to its outside target

5.  Completing an exchange and processing the learning 

 

You do this primarily by asking questions, to individuals and to groups, which make explicit their inner experience. You also have some games, exercises and more formal processes up your sleeve.

 

Questions to individuals

__________________________

 

What are you noticing?

What are you feeling?

What’s behind that?

What do you need or intend here?

 

What are you pleased about?

What was the best moment?

What was happening in this moment?

 

Can I open this conversation out a bit?

Can I raise something I’ve started to notice here?

 

What’s in your heart?

What do you think or feel but not speak?

What stops you saying that bit inside you? What does that tell you?

Where does the voice go? How else does it get expressed?

 

What aspect of yourself are you not honouring (betraying) here?

Who gains or loses by your silence?

What could change if you did speak it?

Who do you need to speak it to?

What would need to be different for you to speak your true voice?

__________________________

 

Questions to groups

__________________________

 

What questions do you want to put on the agenda?

 

What conversations tend to keep repeating round here?

What’s still unresolved?

 

What do you really think and feel?

What do you yearn to say?

What really matters most to you? And what matters most about that?

 

What’s not being said?

What’s not being heard?

Who out there is not hearing it?

 

What are you not hearing?

Whose voice is not being heard?

 

What are the unwritten rules here about speaking or remaining silent?

In what way are the leaders shaping our discussion here?

 

What spaces exist in the workplace for open, uninhibited conversation (– apart from corridor, toilet, water cooler, taxi, pub, private email…)?

What is occurring in the hidden life of this team organisation?

 

What groundrules do we need in order that voices come forward here?

 

What is the collective voice of this group saying?

Who do you need to say it to?

Core Voicing Skills

__________________________

 

The skills we need to develop in ourselves and others:

1.      Giving time and space: unhurried hospitality to all voices inside and around us

2.      Deep listening: opening your heart and mind fully to what is present

3.      Confident speaking: arising from the power and truth you find within you
