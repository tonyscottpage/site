CASE 1

The client: One of three partners in a Personal Travel Business

______________________________________________________

 

Walking into the room: Client seemed intrigued but their issue was vague. A sense of urgency combined with frustration and verging on hopelessness.

 

The issue: It emerged that the client was wanting to open a new web-based budget travel service. The technology was in place but unable to get on with delivery and did not know why.

 

Going deeper: Partners had been skirting round the issue, not committing and not talking about the blockages. 9 weeks had been wasted since finishing the technology.

 

The shift: When the coaches reflected that delivery was “slippery”, the client’s eyes opened as he noticed a conflict between “let’s be the first into this market” and “do we really want to do this?”. He realised that he and his partners were passionate about their core, premium service level business while attracted to an obvious business opportunity to develop a low cost-no frills service which didn’t fit their values and motivation.  They did not want to be known for making a success of the budget business.

 

Going out of the room: Hopefulness. A real sensible, pragmatic solution that client could talk actively through with his partners. “Look guys this is contrary to our values… let’s  hire someone to lead this business”.

CASE 2

The client: 4 directors from  Local Charity for people with learning difficulties

______________________________________________________

 

Walking into the room: Clients struggling with how to bring people with learning difficulties into the community. Worried about the public’s fear of them. Stumped – not knowing what else they could try.

 

The issue:  Clients had opened a home in a residential street but there was neighbourhood resistance. People were being nasty. Relations were bad.

 

Going deeper: People with learning difficulties were doing useful things like cleaning the street, doing people’s gardens. They were artists painting Christmas cards and producing beautiful rugs. Yet people in the street were still behaving nervously towards them.

 

The shift: A new path opened when the clients were challenged about what was really happening here: they had a breakthrough when asked “Who are the disabled ones here?”. Realising that the local residents were struggling with a learning difficulty, opened the clients’ eyes to how they could help neighbours to learn and think in a different way about diversity. They understood for the first time that these people might really be struggling, and gained empathy toward them.

 

Going out of the room: The charity directors were excited as they realised they could bring their skills to bear on the local neighbour group and make a difference at the boundary between the two groups. Since then their centre has grown, become a recognised and accepted part of the street and diversified into a homeless centre, an earthworks project and other services run by people with learning difficulties.

CASE 3

The client: a new Finance Director

______________________________________________________

 

Walking into the room: Client frustrated and confused about why her approach to doing the Finance Director job was not working.

 

The issue: Having difficulty getting things to happen. Staff not responding or carrying out her instructions properly.

 

Going deeper: Client’s whole background had been process, internal audit, doing things properly. She had a high control preference and was a completer-finisher. During the session, one of our consultants departed from the approach agreed with her 2 colleagues. Her colleague challenged her: “Why didn’t you follow the process?”. Initially she reacted defensively, then she admitted she had forgotten the process and effectively got seduced by the client into a different way of working. This interlude produced an eye-opening moment for the client as…

 

The shift: … the client realised she was being seduced by the organisation into following a different way of working. She had tried to operate in a new leadership way in this organisation, but this was far from her preferred personal style and so she did not come across as meaning it. This was confusing to staff and they did not know how to respond to her.

 

Walking out of the room: Client was going to trust herself and find a way of making her natural style work in the new Finance Director role.

CASE 4

The client: 3 directors from an Internet Software Company

______________________________________________________

 

Walking into the room: 3 clients were angry and defensive with each other.

 

The issue: Not getting on.

 

Going deeper: The older guy kept saying I have done it like this and had years and years of experience as a directive leader…the younger guy was really strong on listening, empathy, motivating others and effective as a servant leader. The third guy was their boss and wanted them to get on and understand each other.

 

The shift: The third guy realised that without intending to he was treating the two as father and son, and reinforcing the differences between them. Acknowledging this opened the conversation out to reveal the tensions experienced by each person in dealing with the others.

 

Walking out of the room: The tension was removed. They had started to value each other and had actually enjoyed working together. Subsequently this made a big difference to the business.
