Discord in the Organisation and the Individual

 
Discord is growing both at an Individual and Organisational level. This article explores fundamental reasons for its escalation and argues that in order to address proliferating discord, subtler aspects of Human nature need development at an Individual level, and Organisational objectives need expanding to incorporate this larger view of Human nature. A programme, being developed for this purpose, is described in outline.

 

Discord – Something’s wrong

There can be little doubt that over the course of C20th, organisations have been phenomenally successful in supplying the needs of markets through the provision of products and services. The competitive environment in which they operate has stimulated and driven the development of technologies on a scale unimaginable to previous generations. Access to labour-saving devices, recreational facilities, the abundance of food and drink (in the developed world) is unparalleled in history - only mirrored by the leaps in efficiency and productivity that organisations have made, enabling more and more to be delivered at ever-decreasing cost.

And yet, something’s wrong. Even a brief appraisal of other less tangible measures of success, relating to individuals and organisations, would suggest that many are far from satisfied with the performance of both. Recent surveys reveal that most workers are “disengaged” from their corporate environments. Frustration, lack of fulfilment, anger and conflict are becoming native to the workplace. Corporations are resorting to anger and stress management techniques to keep the lid on pent-up emotions. Facilitation, mediation, counselling and other methods are being used with increasing frequency to counteract the mounting levels of discord prevalent in organisations.

Ultimately, discord influences the performance of organisations, traditionally measured in terms of profit, turnover and return on investment, and threatens their very existence in the competitive environment which has been one of the keys to their development and success.

 

The Roots of Discord – a Proposition

So what is giving rise to increasing levels of discord in the Organisation? The central proposition here is that a fundamental conflict exists between the Individual and the modern Organisation. The conflict is growing as society evolves, as our primary needs are being satisfied, and as organisations are driven by the competitive environment to achieve ever-higher levels of efficiency, growth and financial return.

The Organisation has traditionally sought to add value to its product or service by utilising the skill-sets of its employees, in return for financial remuneration. The skill-sets required are often pre-defined and deterministic. Selection procedures are designed to identify the best match between the job specification and the job candidate. The working environment is designed to encourage the maximum and most efficient use of the skill-set, whilst containing costs, including salary, to a practical minimum. Consideration of factors beyond the immediate requirements of the job are discouraged, in fact the word “Professional” has, amongst other meanings, come to indicate a single-minded exclusion of issues not germane to the job in hand. A venerable, professional institute counts as one of its fundamental principles, Objectivity: “Objectivity is the state of mind which has regard to all considerations relevant to the task in hand but no other”

The quest for “Excellent Systems” to guarantee performance, and preclude the dependence on traditional virtues such as memory, intelligence, commitment and diligence, not only ignores the full spectrum of Human faculties; it actively suppresses their development within many organisations.

Whilst this selective use of human skills creates a machine-like organisation, efficient and streamlined, in mechanistic terms at least, and independent of non-core factors, it pays no heed to the Individual as a whole, and actively excludes Human factors. This approach to any individual needing to interact with an organisation could be supposed to work, were it not for the fact that Human Beings do not exhibit machine-like characteristics in all aspects of their behaviour. And this is exactly where the conflict lies. In seeking to extract skills from the Individual, whilst suppressing other Human attributes and faculties, the organisation not only foregoes the benefit of fuller Human potential, but it also sows the seeds for negative behaviour at an individual and organisational level, ultimately manifesting as discord.

Another fundamental divergence of interests between Individual and Organisation exists at the level of Change. The Individual has an innate need for change, particularly in the form of increased understanding, development and growth. This is particularly so once a basic level of material wealth has been established together with the quality of life that goes with it. The Organisation, on the other hand, in its pursuit of efficiency and productivity at a mechanistic level, has to realise a degree of repetition and specialisation that is fundamentally at odds with Human development on a long-term basis.  

There is a twisted irony lying behind the whole scenario. Organisations have sought to banish the perceived frailty of human individuality through the collective, and create bastions of certainty, stability and determinism, in order to service our primary needs in a predictable and reliable fashion. In doing so they have created environments that throttle and stunt the very people they seek to benefit, denying development and fulfilment whilst stimulating conflict and discord.

 

The Solution – a Proposal

Having established that modern organisations can suppress many elements of human nature by pursuing purely “commercial” objectives, the question remains, how can we create organisations that acknowledge and nurture the Individual as a whole?

Firstly it needs to be emphasised that what is being proposed is not a replacement of commercial objectives with humanistic ideals. The basic financial parameters that are used to gauge an organisation’s performance are a necessary part of survival in today’s competitive environment. It is proposed, however, that these are not sufficient in themselves and need to be augmented by objectives which encourage Human development and growth.

In considering Human development as a means to achieving fulfilment, satisfaction and Individual growth, and in so doing, reducing discord, the question arises, “What is the Individual in totality?” Any attempt to answer this vast question is unlikely to meet with consensus, if only because any answers are by definition personal and will differ, in varying degrees, from person to person.

Instead, what is proposed is an exploration of techniques, tools and theories which can begin to open this question up for all those that it interests, and in particular, managers of organisations.

 

The Mind – Friend and Foe

A direct parallel of the Organisation’s reliance on financial parameters as exclusive measures of performance is the dependence by individuals on the Mind. Both are indispensable factors for survival in the modern world, but both are insufficient for full development. Just as companies would be expected to meet certain responsibilities (e.g. ecological, social) outside their immediate commercial objectives, so would the Individual be expected to bring non-rational faculties (compassion, empathy etc.) to bear in certain situations. The overwhelming preoccupation with the rational, by both organisations and individuals, impedes development of other faculties and qualities to their full potential and, as a consequence, hinders personal growth.

So how do we encourage the development of the full spectrum of Human faculties and qualities, beyond the rational? Fortunately, there are no shortages of theories and techniques that have addressed this challenge throughout the course of history. The common theme that runs through many of them is a focus on temporarily reducing the level of normal mental activity, in order to allow other, less developed faculties to emerge. These techniques can be referred to, in general, as Reflective Practices.

The potential of normal mental activity needs no elaboration here. What is under consideration are the limits of this activity and how we can engage less developed faculties which are no less a part of the Individual. A fundamental attribute of normal mental activity is motion – normally the mind is in constant motion, interpreting, analysing, comparing, assessing and synthesising. The benefits of this activity are clear; the drawbacks appear when the motion continues even when it is not required. Many experience this ongoing mental activity at times when it is not just inappropriate but actually harmful, creating agitation, stress and discord, and interfering with recreation, relaxation and sleep.

Simple reflective practices not only allow the degree of mental activity to diminish when appropriate, and so reduce stress, they also, as a consequence of calming the mind, promote other faculties and encourage a different perspective from that of the habitual view that an agitated mind creates.  

It would be tempting, at this point, to describe these faculties and the fresh perspective that goes with them but this would be to ignore the fundamental of direct experience as the only real source of knowledge and to disregard the individual character of personal observation. However the reflective exercises themselves are described in outline below.

 

Putting Theory into Practice – The Programme

So how can appropriate reflective practices be used on the one hand to encourage understanding, development and growth, promote fulfilment and reduce the effects of mental agitation, whilst on the other hand, to expand the objectives of organisations to incorporate the less material aspirations of the Individual?

The Human Organisation Programme, which is currently being developed, addresses this challenge by using reflective practices for self-exploration, leading to enhanced self-understanding and perspective. Small groups of upto 8 employees practice a variety of simple techniques over the course of a day and discuss their findings. The wider perspectives that are gained are directed at resolving real issues affecting both the individual and the organisation. The emphasis is on direct experience and practicality rather than theory and reason alone. Individuals are entreated neither to accept, nor reject any proposition, but rather test it for themselves. Jargon is avoided as far as possible, reflecting independence from any one school of thought.

The reflective practices involve short periods of guided silence, withdrawal from external observation, and focus on the internal landscape, followed by a full discussion of what was experienced. This promotes, in essence, a third state of mind (as distinct from the sleeping state and the normal waking state) which not only induces deep rest – likened by some to re-charging – but also provides a very natural and simple means of re-balancing the individual, enhancing self-understanding and thereby directly addressing many of the elements of discord, both individual and organisational.

Although opportunity is given for consideration of actual, personal and organisational issues, the emphasis of the programme is on providing the tools for the individual to generate an enhanced perspective and thereby start to engage it independently.

 

A Summary

Problems of a human nature are growing within organisations. A consideration of the basics reveals a fundamental conflict between the material goals of many organisations and the higher aspirations of the Individual, once basic needs have been satisfied. This leads to frustration, lack of fulfilment and discord, which will ultimately compromise the organisation’s commercial performance. The answer is not to replace commercial goals with humanistic ones, but to expand the objectives of the organisation to nurture Individuals as a whole. This is done not by imposing new rules at the organisational level but by encouraging understanding, development and growth at an individual level.

The Human Organisation Programme uses reflective practices to initiate greater self-understanding amongst managers of organisations and equips them with techniques for continued development of their human faculties. The practices allow normal mental activity to temporarily subside, reducing agitation and stress, and encouraging other latent faculties to emerge. The development of managers in this way will inspire the organisation to evolve a less mechanistic and more human environment, which in turn promotes fulfilment and undermines the roots of discord.

 

Biography

Chris Pearse graduated as an Electronics Engineer in 1980 and spent 10 years designing computer-based systems as a Chartered Engineer before taking on a series of Sales, Marketing and Management roles for companies operating in global markets. In 2000, Chris went freelance as a Consultant, assisting companies to build Marketing Strategies and also allowing him to develop a long-standing interest in the nature of the Individual. This has culminated in development of the Human Organisation Programme, aimed at integrating the commercial objectives of companies with the Human needs of Individuals.
