Self-Organizing Leadership
 


(Author)   Dr. Thomas Hench
University of Wisconsin at LaCrosse
Gathering 3,   January 22, 2003
 

 

Self-Organizing Leadership is a quality that manifests itself as a relationship between the leader and the led: in the context of a particular challenge, facing a particular group of people, in a particular moment of time.

Self-Organizing Leadership exists in a "moment" that may be fleeting; but it also may last a lifetime. One may lead once and never again; or spend ones whole life leading. Or, one may never lead at all. It depends. Self-Organizing Leadership is contingent. It is contingent upon the moment, the people and the challenge. One cannot lead if others do not follow. Self-Organizing Leadership, in this sense, is not something any one person, group or body can control in any simple sense of the word. The requirements of a given moment can only be met in leadership; yet, in that moment, they may also be fulfilled.


Similarity, Self-Organizing Leadership is dynamic. Leadership within a self-organizing group can and will change. It is open to all who would fulfill the promise of the moment. It is open to those who would lead in what Tolstoy (1869/1978: 962) in War and Peace, calls "the temper of the moment". Who will be first, he asks, to shout, "Hurrah! Victory is at hand!" and thereby turn the tide of the battle: or instead, shout, "Run. We are cut off!" and run away in panic? Self-Organizing Leadership gives organizations the opportunity to be truly leaderful- for those who would have the courage and the ability to meet the challenge and promise of the moment.


Equally important, because Self-Organizing Leadership is a quality that only manifests itself in relation to the people and the needs of a particular moment, it is not a material thing. That means it is not something someone can pick up or touch or smell or otherwise manipulate in any material way. Nor is leadership something someone can possess or own. Self-Organizing Leadership is not a particular personality trait; nor is it a given competency possessed by some and not by others. In our own way and in our own time, we are each capable of leading.


There is no such thing as "the leadership gene". It does not exist; nor can it. Nor , is there any such thing as a "natural born leader", free and independent of the needs and requirements of a particular group in a particular moment. True leadership is not something for which one can truly screen candidates in advance of hiring or promoting-as in, "we will only hire or promote true leaders". Of course, one will always hire and promote those who have demonstrated leadership in prior settings: but this is never a guarantee that these same people will lead in the futurefor much can and will change, including those who will lead. Every great leader, at one time or another, has also been a great failure.


All of this, then, shifts the focus of leadership, and the challenge of management, from that of managing and manipulating things to that of living and working effectively in-relation with one another. This is the promise of Self-Organizing Leadership. It is the promise of living and working effectively in-relation. It is also the Mission of the Center for Self-Organizing Leadership.

 

Dr. Thomas Hench
University of Wisconsin at LaCrosse
(c) 2003

Developing the Conditions for Self-Organizing Leadership to Flourish

Many times people raise questions about how organizations are the same and/or different when operating in the more traditional and more common Machine Mode as compared to the Living Systems Mode. Beliefs

It is necessary to begin with the beliefs about Self-Organization so that the beliefs about Self-Organizing Leadership can be understood in its proper context.


This is a summary of our beliefs about Self-Organization. It is about how self-organization applies to individuals and organizations, and how Self-Organizing Leadership is necessary for organizations to become coherent and healthy. These thoughts and ideas have been gleaned from many sources including, significantly The Leadership Dance.
 

Beliefs about Self-Organization

       We view individuals and organizations as whole systems with the inherent capability to create their own future.

       People are living systems.

       Organizations behave as if they are living systems.

       Organizations are basically people interacting.

       The behavior in organizations is neither predictable nor controllable. Patterns of behavior are emergent.

       All the parts of whole systems are connected and impact each other. Information flows abundantly through out the whole.

       We believe that people are creative and desire to make a positive difference in their lives and in the lives of those around them.

       There are deep patterns and processes, manifestations of deeper archetypes, in organizations that can be made visible and used to develop a fundamental understanding of whats going on.

       The inter-play of Identity, Relationship and Information is a necessary key to self-organization.

       The individual and the other people in the organization can best achieve their Intention (eg. Vision, Mission, Goals) by understanding and using the deep patterns and processes.

       Simple, co-created Principles and Standards of behavior profoundly influence the performance of the individual and organization.

       Self-Organization is going on all the time at all levels in the organization ranging from the individual to the most complex organization.

       Self-Organizing Leadership occurs in a particular moment of time.

Within the self-organizing paradigm leaders and managers play a critical role. They have a strong influence in helping to set up, create and hold the conditions for coherence and health to develop.

Beliefs about Self-Organizing Leadership

 

                 Self-Organizing Leadership...

 

       helps to create and maintain the conditions needed for people to find meaning in their work, releasing the energy to co-create their future.

       uses the deep patterns and processes inherent in organizations to help the people become more coherent, creative and effective in fulfilling their Intention.

       supports the principle that all behavior is permitted except that which is expressly not permitted.

       supports the emergence of leadership throughout the organization, encouraging people to step forward to meet the dynamically changing needs of the organization.; the organization becomes "leaderful".

       works together with all the people to co-create and live by their shared Principles and Standards.

       keeps the organizations co-created Intentions clearly before everyone and ensures their Work is aimed at fulfilling these Intentions.

       invites and welcomes open, honest feedback so everyone in the entire organization can learn from success and failure. Growth and development are deeply held core values.

       maintains an environment encouraging the open, abundant flow of information, providing space and time for on-going conversation and dialogue.

      This summary of Beliefs opens up the range and dynamics that Leaders and
Managers need to be aware of and to use to successfully lead their organizations to greater
coherence and health.

A Comparison of Approaches for Leaders and Managers

 

These Beliefs about Self-Organization and Self-Organizing Leadership now lead to the question, "What do leaders and managers need to pay attention to and do in order to develop the ground and conditions in their organizations for Self-Organizing Leadership to flourish?"


Using the work presented in The Leadership Dance a comparison of these leadership approaches was developed. It is shown here.


There is a place for both approaches to be used in organizations. In building on Ralph Staceys ideas, when the people in the organization are working where there is good agreement on what needs to be accomplished and where there is high level of certainty about how to do the work, then the Machine Mode Processes are often useful. But when these Processes are pushed too far and become over used, they become the Command and Control Processes which many of us have experienced.


When the people in the organization are working in areas where there is a lower level of certainty about what needs to be accomplished and less agreement about how to do the work, then the Living Systems Mode Processes are often much more useful. But if these Processes are not connected to doing real work of importance to the organization, the effectiveness of the organization unravels.


Moving between these approaches, as the situations demand, is the Leadership Dance.
