Susie Orbach on Psychotherapy

 

"A suitable case for analysis"

Extract from The Observer, Sunday 4th July 1999

__________________________________________________________________

 

Psychotherapy is a dynamic practice. Its essence is not technical (although technical  skills are required), nor is it theoretical (although no therapist can work without rather complex models of the mind). Psychotherapy is about the making of a relationship in which what hasn't been understood can be grappled with directly. Engagement with the problems (whatever they are) ruptures the sense of impotence and despair.

 

But it isn't that the problems can necessarily be solved in the terms in which they are posed. Psychotherapy is far more subversive than that. It disturbs the known, the repeated, the already accepted, by challenging what we think and what we know about ourselves. In that process it invites the bruised or confused individual to think and feel again about her or himself in terms that have eluded them. It encourages them to see the ways in which their own actions, conscious as well as unconscious, interrupt and stifle conflicts, desires and longings. The purposeful engagement with self that marks the therapeutic hour in which one comes to see oneself as a subject who has agency, is still the greatest legacy of the Freudian canon. As we study ourself, so we transform ourself. These acts of transformation, sometimes tiny, sometimes momentous, are the mechanisms by which lives which have been stopped and thwarted can grow again.
