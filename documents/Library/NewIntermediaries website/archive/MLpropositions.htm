Some propositions about organisation, work, innovation and change

By Martin Leith

 

I am sharing these propositions with you because I want you to know my recurring thoughts about organisation, work, innovation, change and related topics. (For the purposes of this document, innovation is defined as: ‘Creating new stuff that adds value.’ It can be any kind of new stuff, not just business stuff, including new products, services, customer experiences, business concepts, ventures, visions, strategies, processes, ways of thinking, ways of relating, ways of living.) Please note that I do not want to enter into a debate about the rights and wrongs of the propositions. Debates are a waste of time and energy. My intention is to share some insights with you, perhaps challenge some of your deeply-held beliefs, and maybe even stimulate some interest in my work – which is helping revitalise organisations so that they become hotbeds of innovation and juicy places that are highly attractive to customers, employees, investors, the media - everyone. If you find yourself reacting strongly to any of these propositions, stick with it, because you’re probably onto something. You can make a note in the right hand column. I very much welcome your comments and suggestions, and promise to credit any contributions you make to future versions of this document, which is a work in progress. I will do my best to respond to your communications as quickly as possible. Thank you for playing.

 

Martin Leith, Brighton, UK, January 2002

 

Proposition

Commentary

Space for your notes

Proposition 1: Change happens in the here-and-now, not in the future, so we should talk about ‘desired present’ instead of ‘desired future’. By using the term ‘desired present’ we open up the possibility of immediate and effortless change, even when circumstances seem to render this impossible, and we galvanise people into action.

I am grateful to Hugh Pidgeon for this insight, which is well known to those who have studied Gestalt Therapy, and to Jim Wilk.

 

Proposition 2: The metaphor of ‘the future is a place and change is a journey’ often limits our ability to create results quickly and easily.

Again, thanks to Hugh Pidgeon. This metaphor is deeply embedded into our language and our consciousness, and it is very hard to move beyond (note journey metaphor) it.

 

Proposition 3: Much of the work done in the name of problem solving changes nothing, and some of it makes matters worse. On those occasions when the desired results are achieved, it can be in spite of the problem solving activities, rather than because of them. If we want to be effective problem solvers, we must develop the ability to define the problem in such a way that it is solvable, and apply the correct type of intervention (e.g. fault correction, resource provision, pattern interruption, context shift) to the problem.

I have a half-developed model with the rather contrived (but accurate) name of ESCAPE – ‘Effective Solution Category’ Approach to Problem Elimination, that attempts to create distinct categories of problem intervention. There is also a system, currently growing in popularity, called TRIZ (Theory of Inventive Problem Solving) that is more developed, more elaborate and grounded in scientific research, yet – in common with my ESCAPE model – still leaves the question “How do I know which method to use?” unanswered.

 

Most of the widely used problem solving techniques are based on first-order change (traffic lights changing within a predictable sequence) and a simplistic model of cause and effect. These techniques are only effective with certain types of problem. When applied inappropriately, they lead to more of the same. Complex human-related problems usually, and perhaps always, require interventions based on second-order change: a change in the type of change (the traffic lights changing to purple, or dancing the fandango). Second order change methods tend not to be mentioned in the business problem solving and change literature. They can, however, be found in the brief therapy literature. Thank you, Jim Wilk, for the traffic lights analogy.

 

Proposition 4: Even in highly complex situations, our ‘desired present’ can be created quickly and effortlessly with the aid of minimalist interventions: small actions that trigger a big result. The small action may appear trivial or even flippant, but it is the difference that makes a difference.

Dr. James Wilk coined the term mimimalist interventions and has done much groundbreaking work in this field. He uses the term ‘kaleidoscopic change’ to describe a small nudge that changes one pattern into a completely different one, such that the first pattern is permanently lost.

 

Proposition 5: All work can be seen as now/new activity, and all organisations are now/new enablers. There are four main varieties of now/new work: innovation (or creating), change, problem solving and development.

I originally used the term ‘now-to-new’ but now feel that the ‘to’ element implies a time delay, which may not be the case, and reinforces the ‘change is a journey’ metaphor.

 

Proposition 6: When we seek to create – and preserve – value for the entire stakeholder system, our success rate improves dramatically, results are achieved much faster, and the system remains viable.

I have developed the Value Matrix, in which all stakeholder groups are listed and for each group three types of value are identified (ideally by talking with the stakeholders concerned!): value to create, value to preserve, and value to sacrifice. In some cases, so as not to trigger a new set of problems, we will need to create or preserve value for individuals and groups whose interests we disapprove of, or even despise.

 

Proposition 7: There are three worldviews that determine human behaviour: Worldview 1 – the world is a machine, Worldview 2 – the world is an ecosystem, and Worldview 3 – the world is energy and consciousness. The more evolved our worldview, the more behavioural options we have and the greater our effectiveness in influencing human affairs. Worldview 2 includes Worldview 1. Worldview 3 includes Worldviews 1 and 2.

More information on my Three Worldviews concept can be found at www.theinnovationagency.com/worldviews

 

Proposition 8: ‘People + Motivators + Enablers = Innovation’ is a useful way of thinking about innovation. Everyone is an innovator, a creator, a now/new exponent, although styles differ. If you can create a sentence then you are creative. Motivators include purpose, vision and rewards. Enablers include organisation (culture is too narrow a term), processes and resources.

Kirton Adaptation-Innovation Inventory (KAI) is a very useful tool for identifying a person’s creative style, and for helping people value the different creative styles of their colleagues.

 

I can let you have a copy of the complete innovation capability model (People + Motivators + Enablers).

 

Proposition 9: Leadership belongs in all three categories: people, motivators and enablers. If leaders want to unleash the creative potential of their people, the single most powerful thing they can do is to be a role model. To paraphrase Gandhi: “Be the innovation you wish to see in your organisation”.

I have a lot more thoughts about the new kind of leadership that is needed to motivate and enable now/new work, but they would take up too much space here.

 

Proposition 10: Organisations that are designed for effective and rewarding now/new work need to include new ways for people to meet, pool knowledge, ideas and perspectives, and reach agreement on courses of action. The message-based form of communication must be replaced with conversation forums such as Open Space meetings. ‘Whole system’ gatherings must be used for addressing complex strategic issues and developing plans for collaborative action.

Open Space, Future Search, Real Time Strategic Change and other large group intervention methods have a vital role to play here. See www.theinnovationagency.com/lgi

 

Proposition 11: During brainstorming and other idea generation (ideation) sessions, the most promising ideas are often dismissed immediately on the basis that they are impractical. What is needed to make these ideas workable is the secondary idea – the idea that makes the idea work. This is revealed through the MFI process (also known as ALO): What are the Merits of the idea? What are the Flaws? What Ideas do we have for addressing the Flaws?

Jeffrey Hyman introduced me to the ALO process. Thank you, Jeffrey.  I changed it to MFI to make it more memorable for British people. (Here in the UK, MFI is a well-known chain of furniture stores.) MFI is built into my Creativator process. See www.theinnovationagency.com/creativator

 

Proposition 12: The now/new process must be designed in such a way that it serves innovators, rather than the other way round. It must not constrain or hinder them unnecessarily. For example, a stage-gate system should be designed with fuzzy (conditional) gates, so that fast-track projects can move towards completion at maximum speed.

This proposition is a paraphrased version of a statement written by Robert Cooper, the originator of the stage-gate system.

 

Proposition 13: Experimentation, playing and rapid prototyping are central to the now/new process. Successful innovators (e.g. IDEO) create a mock-up of their concept as soon as possible, try it out on potential users, and their feedback to enhance, refine and, in some cases, radically change the concept. This is co-creation in action. (See below.)

‘Enhancing prototypes’ is an explicit stage in my Creativator process. See www.theinnovationagency.com/creativator

 

Proposition 14: Many promising concepts never see the light of day, usually because they threaten the interests of key players. The best way to address this is to include these key players in the concept generation process. Many of the concepts that do become reality are emasculated and bastardised along the way. Innovators must therefore act as godparents for their concepts. They must be responsible for the welfare of their creations, making sure that the essence remains intact, no matter what.

Innovators need the support of a coach throughout the entire innovation process, to help them develop and retain a deeply held feeling of unstoppability.

 

Proposition 15: There is a reality – let’s call it the invisible world – that exists beyond time and space, that cannot be experienced through the five senses, and that cannot be measured, conceptualised or described through language.

There is no way of proving this scientifically, because the invisible world is beyond the reach of science. But many people (and I, a non-religious person, am one of them) have a deep inner knowing of the existence of this reality.

 

Proposition 16: If we want our now/new work to be inspired and great, we must learn how to create in partnership with the invisible world.

Edward Matchett’s wonderful little book, Creative Action (now out of print, I suspect), explains how to do this.

 

Change work done under the banner of Orders of Love (a.k.a. Constellations) is an example of this proposition in action. Thank you, Ty Francis and Nick Udall, for pointing me in this direction (journey metaphor again!).

 

Proposition 17: The invisible world is beyond worldviews. Individually we can experience the invisible world, but in the realm of human affairs, the realm of concepts, description and action, Worldview 3 is the closest we can get to it.

My description of Worldview 3, ‘the world is energy and consciousness’, is inadequate but the best that I can do at present. In the third realm, language is very often inadequate.

 

Proposition 18: The concept of resistance to change is a product of Worldview 1 (Newtonian physics specifically), and has no place in Worldviews 2 and 3. What was formerly labelled ‘resistance’ and viewed as a problem to be solved is now seen as nothing more than feedback, useful information, a signal that the intervention is working and that change is happening.

In my experience, organisational change agents have so much invested in the belief that they need to overcome resistance to change that they will reject this proposition and continue to cling desperately to this outdated notion as their organisations disintegrate around them. For a more enlightened view, see Barry Oshry’s book, Seeing Systems. Instead of talking about resistance, he talks about ‘the sound of the old dance shaking’.

 

Proposition 19: In the complex and turbulent environment in which organisations operate, command and control is an ineffective form of organisation and management. A more effective form is self-organisation and co-creation. Co-creation can be defined as: ‘stakeholders working together as equals to bring into being something new that creates widespread value.’

I have found that many people in organisations – leaders and others – know that command and control has had its day, but simply don’t know what they have to do to introduce self-organisation and co-creation. My sense is that they don’t have to do that much, other than convene an Open Space, provide the necessary support and resources needed by the self-managing action teams that emerge from the Open Space, and get out of everyone’s way.

 

The most fundamental principle of co-creation is to include upstream all those whose contribution, co-operation and consent will be needed downstream. Whole system meetings (a.k.a. large group interventions) are a powerful way of doing this.

 

Proposition 20: The concept of ‘buy-in’ is a function of command and control. In the second, third and fourth modes of the ‘tell, sell, test, consult, co-create’ model (i.e. sell, test and consult), managers seek buy-in to their proposals and decisions. If we want to move from command and control to self-organisation and co-creation, we must abandon ‘buy-in’ in favour of ‘join-in’. This is not a game of words. Join-in is profoundly different from buy-in. Buy-in is about me subscribing to your idea. I buy in because I have been on the receiving end of a sales pitch – perhaps a very subtle one conducted over a period of time. Join-in is about collaboratively designing our idea. When we design something, alone or with others, we feel a strong sense of ownership and have a stake in its successful realisation.

I have lost count of the number of times that I have shared this proposition with others and they have agreed that join-in must replace buy-in, yet they continue to talk about getting buy-in. Constant vigilance is clearly needed, along with constant gentle reminders.

 

Proposition 21: In any human system such as an organisation, actions are determined by purpose (reason for existing). If we want organisation members to behave differently, and if we want this new behaviour to be sustainable, we must review – and, most likely, change – the purpose of the organisation. A purpose defined as ‘maximise shareholder value’ will generate certain behaviours, some of them undesirable, whereas a purpose defined as, let’s say, ‘contribute to the prosperity and wellbeing of customers and other stakeholders’ will evoke a different, and more desirable, set of behaviours. Purpose is always about contribution to the greater good.

I am grateful to Nick Udall for the purpose-action insight.

 

Proposition 22: An organisation’s vision is the heartfelt values shared by its members, projected into the future and expressed in an inspiring manner.

One way of uncovering these heartfelt values is through the heart sink / heart sing process.

 

Proposition 23: The heart is the source of passion and courage (coeur = heart), the place where we experience joy, and the gateway through which inspiration flows. For our now/new work to be effective and great, the passion, courage and inspiration of all members must be liberated and harnessed.

Liberating and harnessing these qualities is a key role of new leaders.

 

Proposition 24: One of the key roles of leaders is to liberate and harness the passion, wisdom and imagination of every member of the organisation.

Here I have included wisdom, which is more of a mind thing than a heart thing. We hear a lot about hearts and minds (usually with the Worldview 1 prefix ‘capturing’), yet how often are our hearts really touched in the workplace?

 

Proposition 25: There is a conspiracy in the world of organisations to deny the existence of the invisible world and the fundamental role of the heart, and for rationality to prevail. This conspiracy can and must be broken. We must speak from the heart about the heart, and not allow ourselves to be silenced.

I think of this as ‘The Tyranny of Taylorism’. Perhaps Richard Dawkins (of evolutionary biology fame) is the new Frederick Taylor. Has Dawkins never fallen in love, enjoyed beauty, gazed in wonder at the stars or watched the sun rise?

 

Proposition 26: In an organisation, some activities will make people’s hearts sink, and other activities will make their hearts sing. ‘Heart sinks’ must be acknowledged and the focus shifted to ‘heart sings’, which are the foundation for the future.

One way to minimise ‘heart sinks’ and maximise ‘heart sings’ is to use Open Space to provide an outlet for the grief, and Appreciative Inquiry to identify and build on the many good features of the organisation.

 

Proposition 27: Death is a taboo subject in organisations. Much of the fear evoked by now/new work is a fear of death. If we want to master the art of bringing the new into being, we must also master the art of letting go of the old, and be able to transform the fear of stepping into the unknown into excitement and exhilaration.

I was born and raised in an undertaker’s and have survived several close shaves with the Grim Reaper (for example, I had breast cancer in the late 1980s), so I am probably in a better position than most people to talk about death.

 

I thank my Dutch friend Ton van der Kroon for reminding me of the significance of death in an organisational context.

 

Proposition 28: If our organisations are to survive and prosper, then what they need, more than anything else, is revitalisation. The way to revitalise organisations is to awaken the aliveness, energy, passion, creativity and spirit of every member, and harness these energies for the benefit of the entire stakeholder system. Vital, vibrant, juicy organisations are like sexy people. They attract customers, staff and resources. They are good to be around, and a joy to do business with.

I’m not sure if the right word here is revitalisation or just vitalisation. Was the organisation ever vital in the first place? I suspect that it was, for a brief moment, and that the vitality was soon squeezed out by performance management systems, business process reengineering programmes and other well intentioned, but bureaucratic, constraining, vitality-sapping management processes.

 

Proposition 29: There is a collusion of mediocrity that prevents individuals and organisations from crossing the Rubicon and achieving greatness. Facilitators, organisational consultants and change agents are part of the collusion. If we want to help people achieve the greatness that lies beyond the Rubicon, we must break the collusion. This is a huge risk. We risk becoming unpopular. We risk alienating those we wish to help. We risk becoming a screen onto which people project their darkest fears. Which is the bigger risk: to collude, or not to collude?

I am grateful to Paul Levy and Bob Sang for telling me about the collusion of mediocrity.
