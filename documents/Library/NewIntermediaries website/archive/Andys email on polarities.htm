……

I was trying to address the issue of good guys and bad guys and what I took

as the reluctance of the group to allow that this kind of distinction was

proper. In my view we DO separate people into Good guys and Bad guys,

probably through projection, and certainly through emotional response. Most

things we would call drama are built on this identification / antagonism

conflict. Even if we think this kind of thinking is something we need to

transcend in our work with organisations, or help them to transcend in

themselves - we still end up doing this.

….I was trying to get at … the idea that we all polarise situations willy nilly,

and I think we only have a hope in hell of

transcending the polarity and getting to the place of paradox (This is

Asiogoli's Psychosynthesis) by acknowledging that we do polarise in the first

place. I think one of the problems of liberalism (in the general cultural

sense) is that, out of a genuine desire to achieve a sense of equality and

fairness, it often appears to advocate that we should NOT see people as bad

guys and good guys, and therefore that somehow really enlightened people

will not make these kinds of judgements. The trouble is we do.

Thus the judgements themselves are suppressed (and get projected out anyway) -

something you reflect on when you quote Argyris late in your article.

 

My view is that only by acknowledging the judgement can you begin to

transcend it. Like it or not, distinction, duality and comparison is part

and parcel of how we make sense of the world. The Zen people express the

transcendent view as the paradox of the world being "not two, not one". This

is certainly the thing that draws me to Forum Theatre.
