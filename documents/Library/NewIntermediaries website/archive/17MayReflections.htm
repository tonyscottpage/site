Dear 17th Mayers

 

Thanks for your reflections. Here are mine.

 

Such a lot of good ideas were flowing.

 

I felt sort of full-up by lunchtime and it took some days afterwards for the dust to settle! I'm left with some highly memorable bits and pieces, and also some deeper and less comfortable personal insights.

 

I remember Malcolm telling us a memorable tale early in the day about being in a high quality and enjoyable conversation with an external consultant and then a horrifying, sinking feeling when it sort of fell apart as he realised he was being chatted up with a view to sale.

 

Chatting with Nigel at lunch I realised, and maybe I'm slow to catch on folks but, just as merchant bankers and a host of professionals make money from mergers and acquisitions, external consultants have an interest in selling to client organisations the idea that they have to change. Might it be commercial viable for say a bank to promise to its change-weary customers 'stability in a crazy world of change'?

 

Liked the simple measuring, motivating philosophies we were hearing that support Tirdad in his work in NTL such as "everyday make a little money, have a little happiness, do a little good", "make sure you have passion, vision and action", "create a bond of trust".

 

Some of us discussed the challenge of being 'the real me' with the client. Did this mean Jill has to turn up to meet the client muddy, in her tatty clothes straight from the garden? Sometimes we feel we are jumping through hoops to fit in with misperceptions of the nature of consultancy and how we really add value.

 

Jill and Colston between them did a pretty good job of unpacking Bion's Tavistock work on group dynamics. We heard about the real work and the fight/flight/pairing defenses in groups that get in the way.

 

Kept noticing Paul gently facilitating to go deeper to the real isues using phrases like 'Can we explore that uncomfortableness for a minute?'

 

Some internals (Malcolm) seem to be positioning their practice on the edge of their organisation, nudging in the direction of externals. Others (Tony and Gill) position themselves at the centre. Both are eager to collaborate with externals but the relationship with them as 'internals' is probably quite different.

Is it a one-way traffic/career path from internal to external? Is it a promotion really? Do any externals want to go back?

 

As to the deeper insights, the first was about measurement. I was asked how we would measure the success of the awayday. I realised that for me success would not be measured in conventional hard terms such as did we meet our formal objectives. It was a lot do do with softer questions which underpin our longer term value such as are we all enjoying this, is it making us think some different thoughts, are we learning from and influencing each other, is the process strengthening us or weakening us as individuals and as a larger community?

The other two insights were about personal motivation.

 

First I noticed in me strong underlying need to belong, to join, to be in community with others which seems odd for an 'independent' consultant and perhaps in some way underpins my interest in New Intermediaries. Since this need exists and I have not been fully aware of it before I have no doubt it has been working itself out through all sorts of relationships including those with my clients. I've been relatively good at maintaining long-term client relationships much now I'm challenging myself to make sure I do so in the right way for the right reasons ie. not clinging on or prolonging the work unnecessarily.

 

The second one is about my motivation in relation to fees. Why do I want to maintain a certain level of fee? Is it to fund my lifestyle, shopping and retirement? Partly but since the lifestyle is not so expensive really, and (I may be many things but) I'm not a shopoholic, not completely. Do I want to leave money to my kids? No, not really! Is it because I measure my own value in terms of how much the client is prepared to pay? Ouch! Now we're hitting the mark. Does a decent level of fee support my self-esteem? Does it prove to me I am deeply inherently OK, worthwhile and valuable?

 

I think I'll leave it there for now!

 

Bye for now

 

Tony

I really appreciated the opportunity to meet such a diverse, experienced and questioning group of people.My focus was what makes a successful partnership between internals and externals? and what gets in the way? My thoughts so far coming away from the meeting are;-

 

That there is a need for mutual exploration of 'fit' before focussing on potential project content. Exploring underlying values. Initial meetings often plunge straight into the project brief and get detail oriented too soon.

What are both parties looking for from the partnership? How do they want it to be?

There is a need for overt acknowledgement of the psychological contract.

 

Establishing a climate of trust and positive regard, too often this is rushed through and becomes if they deliver I will trust them. If they give me all the relevant background I will trust them. Feels like shaky foundations for real partnership. Missing pieces may be to do with both parties exposing some vulnerabilities and exchanging 'gifts' generously sharing knowledge/insight/feedback to enable next steps.

 

Liked the idea of levels of dialogue and would like to pursue this line. How can we mine this rich seam of experience?

 

Could be interesting to put a session together along the lines of internals addressing what would we like our clients to say about us?

externals addressing what would we like our consultants to say about us? And the flip side what don't we want to hear? What do we need to say and do to get what we want? Facilitated Plenary sharing .

 

Creating opportunities to learn by sharing experiences.What would we most like to know about each others perspectives. What do we believe we can each offer.Maybe a need to build in non billable sharing sessions.

 

Challenge to myself and the client ; Is this process about enabling or controlling?

 

Thanks to Paul for the Artists Way.

On your Dialogue question - to me it's like some special contact between people, as Jaworski describes in Synchronicity. Jill said she didn't think we 'did' dialogue as we didn't really sort of ladder downwards, finding the underpinning assumptions and ways of thinking behind what we were saying. But I reckon in a session like last week there is so much happening, at a personal and group level, it can be quite overwhelming for me. Although perhaps this doesn't really show up in my conversation at the time, my deeper thoughts, beliefs and assumptions are being disturbed by the conversation and change is sort of happening inside me and I suspect inside you and others there.

 

So is this Dialogue? I don't know! Probably not. It falls short in a sense because what I am describing is not a real time experience - it is a sort of after effect and therefore in a sense more contained and limited. I personally would need more time to build trust in the group, to know that it's safe, before I can get more immediate, more real time into my conversation. I imagine in a group like we had, we're all at different levels of comfortableness. When Tony W arrived in our group after lunch, it set us back as he came in cold and we had to sort of go backwards to include him. Jill commented we got rather TJ in this session and we did not really get deeper.

 

Thinking about facilitation...when Fiona and I went to Prison Dialogue at Blakenhurst we were working with Peter Garrett, who was one of David Bohm's collaborators in several years of dialogue experiments. There was a facilitator in the prison sessions, but not in a usual facilitation role. Bill Isaacs describes the role in his book Dialogue and the Art of Thinking Together. I'm still learning about this, but I think the role is to get an interesting group of people there, create enough safety, "hold the space", encourage diverse thoughts to emerge, feel the heat not run away, inquire into differences and uncomfortableness rather than getting into adversarial debate, share reflections and insights as they emerge.

 

The groups after lunch were slightly imbalanced in that we had only 2 insiders in your group and 4 in the other. I can imagine Fiona drawing out the internal view to strengthen it, making sure it was not submerged under the weight of many outsiders....so this might have come across as more strongly "facilitated" than expected.

 

I really enjoyed talking to you in odd moments during the day. We had an interesting little exchange at lunch with Nigel over why change, could a bank make constancy and stability its offer. My thoughts were going towards ends vs means, destinations vs journeys, values, living the dream today etc. Nigel noticed that it is in the consultant's interest to have fundamental change rather than stability because it lines their pockets. I realise I also enjoyed meeting Nigel but did not really have enough chance to get to know him. I'm so pleased you're getting involved. I'm also struck by how many in the room have some sort of connection with Tomorrow's Company (you, me, Bill, Colston...).

 

Regarding future sessions - it might be interesting to explore SQ further - I would certainly be interested to. Jill Fairbairns wants to do something comparing dialogue and personal construct theory - which is a nice mix of higher order thinking and practical. Having such a mix is I'm sure important. I'm realising since we spoke that I'm not really interested in doing anything practical that lacks higher order thinking!

 

Not sure how to move forward on these things yet and would appreciate any more thoughts, ideas, help etc.

 

Now I'd better get practical and do some work!
