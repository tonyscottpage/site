And it came to pass that it was found written upon tablets of clay, sun-dried skins, and parchments of assorted origin that a god (or possibly a group of gods) had created man in his (her or their) own image.

So God created man is his own image, in the image of God created he him; male and female created he them.

(Genesis 1:27)

In our journey along the bumpy yet elastic surface of the continuum of space-time, respected studies of collective folklore (otherwise known as "written history") have suggested an alternative interpretation of "creation."

Perhaps it was man that had actually conceived of the mental and physical construct of a god (or the gods) rather precisely, all too conveniently, and somewhat self-servingly in man’s own image.

Such was the motive force engendering the birth of Greek mythology.

Within this entertaining and imaginative world of Greek mythology, we encounter a fluttering female butterfly called "Psyche" (meaning "the breath of life" in the sense of "a soul, spirit or mind"). Interestingly, she became the wife of "the little boy with the bow and arrow" known as "Eros - the god of love" (later to be renamed, somewhat less salaciously by the Romans, as "Cupid").

Precisely how a fluttering female butterfly (as attractive and seductive as perhaps she was) could give birth to a child of a pre-pubescent "little boy with a bow" (as precocious and willing as perhaps he was) remains an elusive mystery. (Perhaps a clue to this mystery lies in the cogent observation that both Psyche and Eros were blessed with wings and could hover.)

The study ("logos") of this remarkable union with Psyche ("the mind") has been rather unimaginatively dubbed "Psyche-logos" (later to be rendered somewhat more elegantly as "Psychology" – the study of the mind).

Shortly thereafter, a commonly accepted working definition arose of "psychology" as "the scientific study of the mind and the consequent behavior of butterflies and little boys."

____________________________________________________________________ 

 

Since no one has (of yet) been able to explain the mystical child-bearing union between Psyche and Eros, the field of psychology (unlike the natural sciences of physics, chemistry and biology) is devoid of a grand unifying theory or agreed upon common approach to scientific inquiry.

In lieu of a "grand unifying parental theory," the mystical fertile union of Psyche and Eros has given birth to at least nine identifiable siblings (perhaps more are on the way as Gods can never truly die). Sadly, however, the children of Psyche and Eros can only be known rather cryptically by their technical names (which are not reflective of their more poetic and awe-inspiring mythical Greek origins).

In the "Book of Butterflies and Little Boys" is written:

And it came to pass that butterflies and little boys began to co-mingle and multiply. Behold now these are the generations of Psychology. And young Eros (momentarily) knew his flittering wife Psyche; she conceived and begat nine siblings. And unto young Eros and Psyche were born:

Three stalwart sons of specific (but uniform) schools:

Psychodynamics (1896)

who begat Freud, Jung, Adler, Eriksen, Klein, and Lacan

Behaviorism (1912)

who begat Thorndike, Pavlov, Watson, Skinner, Bandura and the deadly communicative virus "TFSD"

Cognitive Psychology (1912)

(including Gestalt) who begat Wertheimer, Koffka, Koehler, and Lewin

Three demure daughters of broad (but non-consensual) mass movements:

Humanistics (1950)

who begat Maslow, Rogers

Bio-Psychology (1880)

who begat Edwards, Sperry, Newman, and Kamin

Socio-Cultural (Developmental) Psychology (1880)

who begat Kraepelin, Hofstede, Triandis, Mead, Piaget, Bowlby, Rutter, Asch, Binet

Three true hermaphrodites of Germanic (but positivistic) "Zeitgeist":

Experimentalism (1879)

who begat Wundt, Helmholtz, Weber, and Fechner, Ebbinghaus, Muller, & Stumpf

Structuralism (1890)

who begat Titchener

Functionalism (1906)

who begat James, Dewey, Angell, and Carr

But in the heart of one errant child (Behaviorism) lurked the philosophical betrayal of its mother’s legacy (the need to pursue the study of the mind in order to understand human behavior).

This betrayal is echoed openly and consciously in the very words of the more recent offspring of the errant child (Behaviorism):

    • "Never use the terms consciousness, mental states and mind…" (J.B. Watson, 1913)

    • "We do not need to try to discover what personalities, states of mind, feelings … really are in order to get on with a scientific analysis of behavior." (B. F. Skinner, 1971)

Such are the seeds of intra-familial heresy and betrayal of heritage.

____________________________________________________________________ 

 

The roots of this heresy and betrayal by the errant child (Behaviorism) rest with the philosophical concept of "Associationism" (the study of how ideas get linked together, such as to explain the behavior of learning), which was first posited by none other than the post-butterfly Greek philosophers of Aristotle (384-322 BC) and, perhaps, earlier by Plato (428-347 BC) and Socrates (470-399 BC).

"Learning" is viewed thus as a relatively permanent change in behavior due to experience.

This philosophical concept of "Associationism" and "the nature of learning behavior" led an offspring of the errant child (Behaviorism), a Russian physiologist in Moscow named Ivan Pavlov (1849-1936), to study the nature of digestion in dogs. In 1901, he postulated that laboratory dogs would be able to learn (or be "conditioned") to salivate, without food, upon some external stimulus in a controlled environment.

This offspring of the errant child (Behaviorism) received the Nobel Prize in 1904 for this understanding of "Classical Conditioning," which explains virtually all biological reflexes, including heart-rate, perspiration, muscle tension, etc.

Further, since these reflexes are signs of excitement, including fear and sex, they may explain unusual or undesirable social behaviors, such as phobias and sexual deviation. Therefore, Classical Conditioning has become the accepted basis of "Behavior Therapy."

Classical Conditioning, leading to such a powerful tool of Behavior Therapy, is an amazing intellectual accomplishment, but nonetheless it was achieved without reference to "the need to study the mind in order to understand human behavior." For this reason, it remains both intra-familial heresy and betrayal of the heritage and legacy of the spirit and union of Psyche and Eros.

____________________________________________________________________ 

 

Continuing this tradition of heresy is yet another offspring of the errant child (Behaviorism), John B. Watson (1878-1958) who published Psychology as the Behaviorist views it (1913).

  • "Psychology … is a purely objective experimental branch of natural science. Its theoretical goal is the prediction and control of behavior. Introspection forms NO essential part of its methods."

Thus, Watson rejected the need to study the mind (introspection) to understand behavior, the notion of consciousness, and the role of any possible inherited characteristics.

Watson believed that emotions are environmental stimuli and internal measurable (biological) responses. His famous (but ethically disturbing) emotional study (1920) showed that infants exhibit three basic emotions: fear caused by loud noises, rage caused by restricting body movements, and love caused by caressing and rocking. All other emotions are a combination of these. Adult emotions (fears and anxieties) arise from similar childhood experiences.

In 1921, a critical event occurred. Watson had a "scandalous adulterous relationship" with his assistant and was forced to permanently resign from academic life. The offspring of the errant child (Behaviorism) then was employed in "business advertising," thereby inculcating his heretical notions into the business world (to which we are unethically and adversely subjected even today).

Watson spent the rest of his life applying Behaviorism to the prediction and control of consumer behavior. Essentially, he tried to manipulate people’s psychology to make them dissatisfied with what they have for the expressed purpose of making them desire a new product. (Yet again Watson treads the borderline of ethical social behavior).

Following in the footsteps of Pavlov and Watson was another generational offspring of the errant child (Behaviorism), Burrhus Frederick Skinner (1904-1990) who published The Behavior of Organisms (1938) and Science and Human Behavior (1953).

In contrast to Pavlov or Watson, Skinner studied primarily non-reflexive or voluntary behavior. In 1933, he invented an "Operant Conditioning Apparatus" (the Skinner Box) to demonstrate the interplay of Positive Reinforcement, Negative Reinforcement and Punishment concepts on behavior modification.

Skinner believed that some children and adults need to have their behavior "modified" in order to fit into society and lead happier, more fulfilling lives. This can be achieved by Skinner’s Operant Conditioning Behavior Modification techniques without the need to study the mind in order to understand behavior. Yet again, Skinner perpetuates both intra-familial heresy and betrayal of the heritage and legacy of the spirit and union of Psyche and Eros by reducing human behavior to the level of programmable machines.

 ____________________________________________________________________ 

 

Eventually, the offspring of the errant child (Behaviorism) begat a deadly communicative virus so dangerous that it could only be referred to by its coded viral DNA sequence "TFSD." This virus is genetically programmed to manipulate the minds of people by using concepts and constructs within the field of Behavior Psychology to control the human behaviors of (T)hinking, (F)eeling, (S)aying, and (D)oing.

Through the cross-cultural exchange of professional management consultants within the last decade of the twentieth century, this deadly communicative virus ("TFSD") invaded and multiplied throughout one of the world’s leading global professional advisory service firms. Yet, its heritage, as the self-destructive offspring of the errant child (Behaviorism), is cleverly disguised and not appreciated for the threat that it poses to the psychological health of the firm.

Concurrently, in the middle of the twentieth century, the offspring of the child of Psyche and Eros called "Psychodynamics" begat, two (cross-continental) siblings:

  • a charming multi-cultural Clinical Psychologist named "Cornelia" (schooled in the rigours of the Germanic psychoanalytic tradition) and

  • an inquisitive engineer and consultant to management named "Owen" (schooled as well in the evolving American psychotherapeutic tradition).

As staunch defenders of the faith that the practice of psychology must address the study of the mind and its consequent behavior in butterflies and little boys, Cornelia and Owen remained ever watchful for the inevitable seductions of life which challenge this premise.

And it came to pass that throughout the course of business in client-related project meetings, Owen was repeatedly confronted with situations where he detected the presence of various strains of the circulating "TFSD" virus, which he instinctively recognized as the philosophically and psychologically dangerous offspring of the errant child (Behaviorism). Whereupon, he then solicited "the wisdom of the counsel of Cornelia" to reflect upon the nature of the threat to the psychological health, well-being and reputation of the firm.

The following dialogue records "the counsel of Cornelia:"

Owen

Gruess Gott! Ich moechte gerne mit Frau Diplom-Klinische-Psychologin Krefft sprechen – (wenn dass moeglich ist, naturlich)?

 

 

Cornelia

Hello, Owen -- is that you? My name is Cornelia, remember?

 

 

Owen

Hallo, Cornelia! Wie geht’s Dir heute?

 

 

Cornelia

I can sense that you have something of a serious nature to discuss today.

 

 

Owen

Du hast recht, Cornelia! I am having some difficulty trying to both understand and reconcile the way in which we at our firm are asked to deal with our clients?

 

 

Cornelia

Could you clarify that a bit more for me Owen -- I think that I may be missing some nuances?

 

 

Owen

Well, Cornelia, perhaps this time I am the one missing the nuances.

You see, when we occasionally meet together as a cross-functional or cross-disciplinary team to develop a presentation to take to a client, we inevitably are confronted with a particular methodology which is peculiarly troubling to my instinctive and intuitive sensibilities.

 

 

Cornelia

Owen, you risk losing me – keep it simple. What’s the problem?

 

 

Owen

Okay. At some point in the group discussion, we are each asked by the moderator to consider what we would want the client to "think, feel, say and do" in response to our presentation and precisely how we might facilitate making that happen.

You see, the assumption is that we are able to propel the client along this "psychological rollercoaster" experience of alternating emotional highs and lows.

 

 

Cornelia

Sounds reminiscent of the ancient art of manipulation. Strains of Machiavelli’s viral DNA could possibly be circulating throughout your firm. Have you seen any copies of Machiavelli’s classic text "The Prince" floating about? Be aware that the complete text of "The Prince" is cleverly encoded in the viral DNA/RNA’s amino acid sequences (albeit in Latin)!

 

 

Owen

Not yet, but I’m keeping an eye out. This "think, feel, say, do" art of manipulation appears to be an ingrained and somewhat sacrosanct mindset of a particular group within the firm which has been schooled, in years past, in a conceptual methodology known as "Business Transformation."

 

 

Cornelia

Never heard of it, Owen! Why do people want to go around "transforming" businesses anyway? Why can they just appreciate them for what they are – unique "business personalities?" Respect their integrity and let them evolve.

 

 

Owen

Well, Cornelia, it is probably a psychological response to being insecure and uncertain about how to deal with the apparent poor operating performance of the firm. So, they rush to a "transformational imperative."

 

 

Cornelia

Owen, how serious are they about wanting and expecting to be able to anticipate and actively manipulate a client’s "think, feel, say, do" behavioral response to your presentation?

 

 

Owen

Cornelia, perhaps I am too close to the situation to interpret it other than subjectively but all of the signals of which I am aware tell me that they are indeed taking this concept rather seriously.

 

 

Cornelia

And how does this make you feel, Owen?

 

 

Owen

Well, it makes me feel as though I am being encouraged or forced to participate in an unethical and immoral business process, which few if any around me seem to recognize as such.

 

 

Cornelia

And why does this have such a powerful echo within you? Why is your reaction one of repugnance and not one of dismissal – just quietly ignore the irrationality of it all?

 

 

Owen

Why such an echo in me? Perhaps because it is not a transitory theme but a commonly re-occurring theme in each client project meeting.

And, perhaps, it is because I view it as a direct assault or affront to my sense of how I expect to add value intellectually – by understanding the client’s fundamental business model.

And not by resorting to the art of manipulation through the use of ethically disturbing behavioral psychology techniques (of which I have never considered subscribing).

 

 

Cornelia

Owen, do you remember my sharing with you "The Story of the Errant Child of the Butterfly and the Little Boy with the Bow?"

 

 

Owen

How could I forget your parables, Cornelia! The Errant Child is the School of Behaviorism within the field of psychology – the study of the mind and the consequent behavior of butterflies and little boys.

The School of Behaviorism is "reductionist" in that it reduces our understanding of behavior to the mechanistic workings of biology and, sadly, deliberately ignores even the existence or construct of the mind.

 

 

Cornelia

Now can see yourself reacting psychologically to the basic premise, which people around you are believing but not openly articulating or acknowledging, that says that …

man can manipulate human behavior (to serve one’s own ends) by using the techniques of behavioral conditioning without regard for the effect upon the mind, the conscious or the unconscious.

 

 

Owen

Genau, Cornelia!

 

 

Cornelia

As we discussed before, Owen, one must learn to recognize the seeds of the Errant Child of Psyche the Butterfly and Eros the Little Boy with a Bow. Remember, these are the seeds of the progeny of Pavlov and Watson and Skinner.

And especially this character Watson, the adulterous professor who lost his teaching post, developed his behavioral modification and conditioning techniques by subjecting 11-month old babies ("the story of Albert") to the terror of being accosted by live rats while banging steel bars with hammers in the background.

Watson then transmitted this same virus throughout the business world by applying such misguided, unethical, and immoral behavioral conditioning techniques to the manipulation of the minds of consumers through advertising psychology and sales psychology in the quest for business profits and the satiation of human greed.

 

 

Owen

Wow, Cornelia, … can you put things into perspective!

 

 

Cornelia

The modern manifestations of this "Errant Child" can upset me too! Before I studied clinical psychology, I was given the opportunity to pursue "Watsonian" advertising psychology (Werbepsychologie) and sales psychology (Verkaufspsychologie).

Such a path would enhance the likelihood that I would be able to earn a living professionally in hard times by offering my skills to the business community (in effect, prostituting my principles and skills for money and security).

This, out of ingrained conscience, I simply could not do – in effect, to barter with the Devil.

As a Professor of Medicine, my father shared with me "his sense of medical values" which I have evolved for psychology, namely …

the practice of psychology should only be used "to help and to heal people" – never to consciously exploit the psychological weaknesses of people, – the inherent asymmetry of understanding – as for the pursuit of profits and satiation of human greed.

 

 

Owen

I can see that we both have the same instinctive and intuitive reactions, albeit driven by different underlying motivations and life experiences.

 

 

Cornelia

Similar but never the same.

 

 

Owen

Then how would you suggest that I deal with this situation, that is, the re-occurring demands, within client project team meetings, to actively manipulate the client’s "thoughts, feelings, sayings, and doings" by using behavioral modification and operant conditioning techniques?

 

 

Cornelia

If done with great sensitivity, it can be helpful to hold up a "psychological mirror" to others within the firm when you experience such behavior.

 

 

Owen

And remind me again, Cornelia, precisely how do I "hold up" this imaginary "psychological mirror" – especially, if others can not see it?

 

 

Cornelia

This imaginary psychological mirror is held up to others by encouraging them to reflect upon their inner motivations as to "why they feel the need" to resort to this type of questionable manipulative behavior.

 

 

Owen

So should I challenge them to reflect upon "whether they feel so analytically impotent and psychologically insecure about their ability to add real value to a client" that they feel the need to resort to such manipulative Machiavellian "think, feel, say, do" tactics?

 

 

Cornelia

I would not suggest using such aggressive and threatening terminology – remember, I said "if done with great sensitivity, …"

 

 

Cornelia

Consider the wisdom of the metaphorical teachings of historical figures.

Cornelia

Did The Buddha not teach that the "apparent boundary" is imposed by the weaknesses of our own ability to perceive the underlying creative force making them a single entity or manifestation?

 

 

Cornelia

Is not all imagined separateness between perceived entities, as it were, a hallucination – our hallucination – our self-imposed delusory perception?

 

 

Cornelia

Is this not why The Buddha implores us to practice "meditation" to develop our latent abilities to "perceive the underlying creative force" which views the "blade of grass and the meditating woman" as one?

Is this not the meaning in the Buddhist tradition of "enlightenment?"

 

 

Cornelia

If your colleagues and you can begin to reflect upon the wisdom of The Buddha, then might you not some day perceive that "you and your clients are one and the same entity" – thereby eliminating the need and the motivation to manipulate "that which you already are?"

 

 

Cornelia

Did not the Prophet Muhammad teach the same principle as The Buddha taught when he wrote …

"I am nothing separate from or other than the Absolute."

Are not religion, psychology and philosophy all one – reinforcing the same messages concerning the nature of eternal truths?

Owen, do you recall from your study of science at MIT, what Karl Popper taught us about the nature of theories?

Yes, well, Popper said that there are only two types of theories – Punkt!

Vorsicht, Owen! Confucius was not the only devotee of the "I Ching!"

Even our own beloved Carl Jung, eminent Freudian psychoanalyst, regularly consulted the wisdom of the "hexagrams of the I Ching."

I can see that someone failed to hold up the imaginary psychological mirror to Jung to stop that "I Ching" virus from infecting him. ("Why did Jung feel the need to …?)

Ah – so you are learning to hold up the mirror -- my little Grasshopper!

So do you now see religion, psychology, and philosophy as equally valid, non-contradictory, harmonious alternative pathways to the search for truth, albeit in a non-provable, non-scientific theoretical context?

 

 

Owen

But what does this imply?

 

 

Cornelia

All (inherently non-provable) non-scientific theories can only be logically justified through the exercise of "faith" – which, as the Bible tell us, is "the substance of things hoped for and the evidence of things unseen."

"Faith" is essentially the price one must pay for non-scientific insight!

Do you really expect your colleagues to recognize the destructive seeds of the Errant Child of Psyche the Butterfly and Eros the Little Boy with a Bow – or the seeds of their progeny -- Pavlov and Watson and Skinner?

Then how does one encourage a change of behavior?

Holding up this imaginary psychological mirror by asking a "reflective question" is akin to the use of "verbal parables or metaphors" by Christ.

Are not the great truths of religion, psychology and philosophy the same?

And what shall I do beyond "heeding" …?

For that which remains to be known, must be found within you – for you to share in turn with (the oneness of) me.

Bis bald … my little Grasshopper! Adieu! … a Dieu! … to God!
