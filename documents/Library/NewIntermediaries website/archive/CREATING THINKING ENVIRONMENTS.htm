CREATING THINKING ENVIRONMENTS  

How many of us truly think for ourselves?  If people are the real resources in an ever changing business environment then thinking our own thoughts and expressing our own ideas is important for the economic future of the organisation.  This is harder than it seems.  The Oxford Forum for Assessment and Development* invited Nancy Klein author of "Time to Think" to outline her model of a thinking environment.  John Harradence a member of the editorial board of "Assessment Matters - The National Journal of Vocational Assessment" reports on the seminar.

Introduction

Over the course of the day Nancy outlined her model for the creation of a Thinking Environment and gave the seminar participants the opportunity to practice some of the techniques involved. The ideas underlying the model are relative simple. They are essentially a set of behaviors which cause high quality thinking to happen. When put together into a process they take any interaction between people to a more productive and satisfying level. Sounds good? So why aren't we all doing this already? 

In her book 'Time to Think', Nancy Klein asserts "...at this moment in human history, thinking for yourself is a radical act." (page 34). My first reaction to this proposition was somewhat doubtful. Thinking about it a little more I could see her point. How many of us are truly independent minded thinkers who encourage this behaviour in others? Sceptical? - then you might like to ask yourself these questions: how often do I say what I really think rather than what I think my audience wants to hear or what will put me in the best light with them; how often do I ask my friends, partner, children or work colleagues what they really think and make it abundantly clear that I am interested in their opinions even if they are different to mine?

Few of us are actually taught how to think properly for ourselves. We pick up thinking skills as we develop through childhood into adulthood, influenced by a number of different environments - home, community, school, work etc. But few of these environments really encourage thinking for yourself. Many actively discourage it, seeing creative thinking as a challenge to the status quo and those in positions of authority. Individuals who encourage others to think for themselves are rare and often seen as something of a maverick in their organisations. Following the old Yorkshire adage to 'see all, hear all, say nowt' is still seen by many people to be the surest way to make progress up the organizational ladder.  

Nancy is something of a maverick. Her consultancy's mission is to teach people in organisations how to think for themselves after years of conditioning to do the opposite. The Thinking Environment model is the basis of this work. When working with CEOs and company boards, key questions for these senior executives are whether they really want their people to think for themselves and whether they are ready for it. As the Thinking Environment is essentially a set of ground rules for legitimising thinking for yourself, putting it into practice often challenges existing organisational values. Fortunately, an increasing number of organisations are now realising that to make the most of their human assets they must break down the 'right think' mentality. True leadership is recognised as articulating a vision and getting the best from your people in working to achieve that vision. Getting the best from people means getting their best thinking. It means creating the right environment for thinking, which in turn means building teams whose members routinely treat each other well.

The Thinking Environment Model

The Thinking Environment model developed over a number of years. The big idea that started Nancy's thinking off was that "...everything we do depends for its quality on the thinking we do first. However determined or indefatigable or charismatic a person may be, their every action is only as good as the idea behind it." (page 15). And if thinking comes first, then to improve the quality of action we have first to improve the quality of thinking.

The second key principle underlying the Thinking Environment model is that the quality of our thinking depends of the quality of our attention for one another. Attentive and respectful listening encourages people to think for themselves. In the Thinking Environment model, listening attentively goes far beyond what we are normally taught to do in interviewer training or even counselor training. It requires giving people the time and space to think and listening intently on what they have to say. Listening in this way shows that you have confidence in their ability to think for themselves and that you value what they have to say. Once this confidence is established, imagination and constructive ideas will flow. This sounds like a time consuming process and Nancy agreed that it often is at first. But once people get used to working in this way it actually saves time by getting to good solutions to problems much quicker.

Sometimes however listening isn't enough. An individual's think proceeds to a certain point and then becomes blocked and they seem unable to move forward. Experience has shown that it is often an assumption on the part of the thinker that is limiting further progress. At this point, if it is clear that the thinker really is blocked, the attentive listener can help by asking an incisive question designed to help the thinker identify the limiting assumption. Once this assumption is brought out into the open, then thinker can then move forward again by thinking the previously unthinkable.

Over time the two key processes of attentive listening and incisive questioning were developed into the Thinking Environment model by the addition of a number of other essential behaviours. The full model comprises the 10 behaviours listed below:

 1.  Attention   Listening with respect, interest and fascination.

 2.  Incisive questions   Removing assumptions that limit ideas.

 3.  Equality   Treating each other as thinking peers.

·           Giving equal turns and attention.

·           Keeping agreements and boundaries.

 4.  Appreciation   Practising a five-to-one ratio of appreciation to criticism.

 5.  Ease   Offering freedom from rush and urgency.

 6.  Encouragement   Moving beyond competition.

 7.  Feelings   Allowing sufficient emotional release to restore thinking.

 8.  Information   Providing a full and accurate picture of reality.

 9.  Place Creating a physical enviornment that says back to people, 'You matter.'

10. Diversity   Adding quality because of the differences between us.

Applying the model

The Thinking Environment model has been tested in companies, government agencies, universities, schools, political groups and voluntary organisations in the UK, Ireland and the United States. Helping an organisation become a Thinking Environment would normally start at Board level or equivalent. Having got the backing of the Board, individuals and teams can then be helped to develop a new style of working based on the 10 behaviours.

During the seminar, participants were able to try out working in pairs as Thinking Partners and in small groups using a process model which incorporated the 10 behaviours. Everyone I spoke to was impressed by the power of these apparently very simple techniques. Reading Nancy’s book after the seminar reinforced this view. On the basis of many years work Nancy believes that by mastering the theory and skills of the Thinking Environment that people can enrich their work, their lives and their relationships. Organisations produce better ideas more quickly and they achieve greater success by having a more motivated and committed workforce. She ventures that if children were brought up and educated in Thinking Environments their lives would be more rewarding and the world would be a better place.

I recommend you to read Time to Think, published by Ward Lock (ISBN 0-7063-7745-1) and see what you think. It may change your life.
