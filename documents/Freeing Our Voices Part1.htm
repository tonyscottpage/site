That's your belly voice! *

 

Puppeteer: What's your name?

Puppet: I'm your belly voice

 

Puppeteer: That's a stupid name. Why are you called that?

Puppet: Because, when you open your mouth you speak from your head

 

Puppeteer: Yes. That's where my mouth is

Puppet: Now you're being stupid, and I'm getting cross

 

Puppeteer: OK. Seriously though, what is a belly voice for?

Puppet: To tell the truth. How you feel. I say the most exciting things and the most shameful things that you edit out. The things you don't say with your head voice.

 

Puppeteer: Like what for example?

Puppet: Like you're wishing you hadn't just asked that, because you're afraid of what I'm going to say next.

 

Puppeteer: Seriously though

Puppet: Well, when you're chatting there's so much you don't say. What do you choose to leave out?

 

Puppeteer: I leave out the things that might embarrass me or someone else.

Puppet: So you say what's safe

 

Puppeteer: Well, not just that. I try to get at what's true, but there are rules. You could do a lot of damage you know, by just blurting out anything, as you feel it, in the moment

Puppet: Yes, and you could do a lot of damage by giving yourself that excuse. Have you ever thought what happens to the stuff you leave out?

 

Puppeteer: No. What?

Puppet: It rests here, with me, and it gets heavier and heavier. The pressure builds, until it bursts out.

 

Puppeteer: Oh.

Puppet: You're editing! What are you not saying now?

 

Puppeteer: That sounds horribly dangerous. And I'm stuck. Damned if I say it and damned if I don't.

Puppet: Thanks. Want to find out what to do?

 

Puppeteer: Yes

Puppet: Read on.

What was that all about?

 

It was something I woke up with about a month ago and had to write down. I must have been thinking about this uncorking thing, and it sort of came out in the story.

 

Did you notice the eruption of heartfelt truth? Someone getting carried away on a tide of emotion?

 

A confrontation. Yes. Dramatic. Yes. Dangerous. Yes. Over-reaction. Maybe, but emotion can be a bit like that can't it?

 

The point of writing it here? It is an example of Voice. Big heartfelt Voice. Demanding to be heard. Breaking in, shattering the expectations of those in the canteen, and importantly creating the potential for a different kind of future.

 

In a sense, the Voice of the service worker erupting calls forth the Voice of the CEO. It will be the reaction of one voice to another, that creates the potential for a different future

 

Doesn't the CEO have to react in some way? It's not easy just to walk away is it? But how? Get rid of Jo, the worker? It is hard not to imagine some sort of consequence, and after writing the first bit, it took me a while to ponder how Chris the CEO might respond. Here is one possibility.

Bringing out your Voice

 

Did you recognise yourself in either role in that story? Which did you identify with more strongly? Exploding worker? Cornered leader? Or perhaps the bystanders in the canteen? Would you tend to speak out like Jo? Or is it foolhardy and career limiting to speak out in your organisation? Did you notice the leader's inner voice, and the italic parts that were spoken out?

 

While acknowledging the risk you take in speaking heartfelt words, I do not accept that automatic risk avoidance is the right course. As a leader don't you embrace risk? Don't you use your voice to encourage others to engage with difficult challenges they face? This book invites you to address the challenge of being a leader with Big Voice.

 

Reading this book you can check yourself out, and take charge of how you are using your voice in your organisation. Since an organisation is too large to put your arms around, you apply what you learn here first with a team, as a crucible, a small enough place to practice your voice, and for others to begin to hear their voices included in yours. These may be the basic pre-conditions to create an organisation in which people feel big, confident, capable, responsible and passionate. From this starting point, your new organisation, where people WANT to work, can be born.

 

The consequences for you of the Voice Proposition presented here may be far reaching. You may have to confront how you use your power, make decisions, what you mean by leading, and how democratic you set out to be, when to ask and when to tell. You might find yourself compelled to say some unpleasant things and make some tough decisions you would rather avoid. You may be faced with the difficulty of unlearning old habits and learning a new language.

 

This book lets you explore the Voice Proposition, learn a new language, and cope with the consequences of using it. By taking your leadership in this direction you are putting something back into your organisation that has probably been seeping away in recent years. You are injecting a special binding agent, that cuts past corrosive cynicism, smashes politics, replaces passivity, and restores contribution levels, meaning and purpose.

 

You will have realised by now this is NOT a book about how to do mergers, leverage profit or shareholder value, orchestrate a detailed organisational change, or become number one in your industry. But paradoxically, when you let go of those kind of goals and adopt this way of working you and your organisation can become different, better, stronger, more able to control your destiny, and basically, much more successful.

 

Voice is like a gift that enriches you. When you find it you gain a special power, that only sustains itself if you pass the gift on to enrich others. What this means is as a leader you are a voice-giver, and by playing this role fully and well, your organisation is better able to attract, motivate and retain the best people, to deal effectively with all your stakeholders and thus to enact its work brilliantly in the world. The vital but eroded sense of meaning, the on-the-ground, heart-to-heart connection between people and their work, can be restored.

 

This book is not going to be a rant about how you ought to change yourself into a voice-giver. That would be patronising. Instead, it is a short, easy to read, fun, varied, practical, travel guide offering you an adventure, a way to find your voice, and to fulfil your destiny as a leader. While reading you are guided through a conversation and a series of real-life tests, through which your voice can emerge.

 

Are you up for this challenge? Or will you shut yourself up?


 

You and I both know that….

 

You and I both know that this cannot really be a "proper" conversation between us because it's a book, written by me, Tony, in a different place, some time in the past. At this moment, now, when you are reading, I might be sleeping, in a meeting with a client, on holiday with my family, or enjoying a run in the morning mist along the river bank. Separated by space and time from you, how would you communicate your reactions to me? How would I get the message and be able to communicate my own reactions back to you?

 

If we cannot meet in the same room and speak together directly, are there some other possibilities for exchange between us, that might give us something like a conversation? Yes, of course. The old fashioned way would be to write me a letter, then I reply, and we enter a lively correspondence. This opens a channel, albeit a slow one by today's standards, in which we can react to each other, and an energy starts to move. We create a buzz between us.

 

Nowadays, although we have so many more channels open to us for this sort of exchange (and here I'm talking about phone, fax, email, mobile, text messages..), there is still an underlying question about the nature of conversation. Disneyland scripts, PowerPoint presentations, pushbutton queuing on phones, recorded voices on tube trains and lifts ("This is Edware Road. Please mind the doors"), underline the point. We notice a special something is missing, even when it sounds like a human voice. Quantity is not the same as quality, and this book seeks to explore quality of conversation, as an experiment. Through exploration and provocation your awareness and sensitivity should get heightened, to when quality is good, and when it is not.

 

The Voice Proposition we will explore is about reacting to each other. This book seeks to generate reactions in you the reader, which you then note, either mentally, or by scribbling in the margin. Through recording your reactions to what I say, you raise your awareness. Through reading about how I might react to your reactions, the energy starts moving. In fact while I am writing this, it almost feels as if we are in conversation already. We are making ourselves mindful of some bigger possibilities here.

 

The offer I am making to you is to join me in trying out some situations that might generate reactions in you. All of the material here, including the poems, quotes and tests, is offered to provoke and reveal to you your inner reactions to outside events, in one way of another. Some of them include voiced reactions, others include dilemmas over whether to voice something, whether to draw the voice from others or leaving something unvoiced and noticing the consequences.

 

The request I make is that you record your reactions, noticing what makes you happy, sad, angry, uncomfortable, fearful, excited, and so on. With reactions, there are no right or wrong answers, they just happen. The choice you have is either you bring them into your awareness and speech, or you hide them away. Here you have the chance to hold reactions temporarily in your awareness, which is not just an important practice, it is the solid foundation on which you can build up your Voice as a leader.

 

The read whilst enjoyable is also intended to be highly interactive at a personal level, and from the expansion of your awareness to have also expanded your choice, to voice or not to voice, according to how you read each situation you encounter subsequently in your working life.

 

Creating a clearing

 

"Windows!" bawls the adventure camp leader (and he wasn't talking about computer software!), penetrating the bustling, noisy hubbub of 100 eleven year olds gathered chatting, giggling, joshing each other, expectant. Then, as if with one voice and mind, the 100 eleven year olds roar back in unison "Shut it!".

 

A simple drill, used by the leaders time after time, works every time. Their stimulus draws a predictable response which in turn creates a clearing in the dense, wild vegetation of bustling, talking, playing. A space. A chink. A moment. A momentary possibility… for something different to occur: a different kind of speaking and listening. And if not seized instantly, the moment passes. If it is not filled with speaking relevant to 100 eleven year olds, perhaps about FOOD, FUN, or DANGER, then it fails the test of relevance, chattering resumes, the volume rises, the leader's voice is drowned out, the wild vegetation grows back.

 

And here we are. You and me. Right now. In a similar position. We have a clearing in your busy day, with a momentary possibility of… something. You, I presume, a lively, curious person, with lots of different ways you can spend the present moment, have chosen to pick up this book. Maybe just as a distraction from something less agreeable, or the title caught your eye, or someone recommended you try it. Questions may be passing through your mind. Is there anything here for me? Will it be fun? Same old stuff or something new? Worth my time?

 

And here I am. This is my voice. From this clearing, I must seize the moment to say what I have to say. To put a proposition to you, that might meet an important need. We can find out quite quickly like this. Three questions.

 

Who are you? Read on if you work in an organisation, and you are trying to get some sort of result, and you need people, such as co-workers, staff, customers, suppliers, bosses, on your side to go for an opportunity, deliver a result, or tackle a problem, or make a difference in the world. This means, as far as I'm concerned you are a leader.

 

Second question. How do you feel about being in your leader role right now, today? Confident? Excited? Proud? Optimistic? Always positive? Come on be honest! Positive overall perhaps? Stretched but coping? Maybe a few concerns, but nothing major? Frustrated? Or maybe at times seriously fed up and wanting to move on to do something else with your life? Overall though, how big, confident and in charge do you feel in your leader work? Make a mental note.

 

Final question. Have you noticed what your people are complaining about these days? Maybe they're happy, positive and upbeat all the time, but is that likely? What's really niggling and frustrating them? Is it pressure to deliver unrealistic targets? Too many meetings? Email overload? Politics? Stress? Morale? Fatigue? Cynicism? Mistrust of bosses? Fear of job cuts? Work-life imbalance? Poor communication? Uncertainty? Lack of leadership? I've found these things come up in many organisations. But to summarise, how big, confident and in charge would you say the people you are leading feel when they are working with you? Again, make a mental note.

 

Now you have had a chance to think about these questions, I'm ready to put the first part of the VOICE Proposition to you.

 

The Voice Proposition part one - the BIG CHOICE part

 

Ready? It is this. Three short sentences:

 

 

1.   How BIG, confident and in charge you and your people feel matters: when people feel BIG, they can make a bigger contribution.

 

2.   There are two languages being used by leaders today: one which is abstract and makes people feel SMALL, and the other which is direct and makes people feel BIG(*see below)

 

3.  When you know this you gain a new CHOICE about how you speak and listen, and the opportunity to raise your contribution as a leader and as an organisation.

 

 

That's it. Part one anyway. What do you think? Do you agree? Maybe you think this is unlikely. Or it’s ridiculous. Or it’s just too obvious and you're already doing it. Maybe some parts you accept and others you question.  That's fine. Maybe you're not interested in raising your game. If so you can put the book down and go and do something more important. Possibility closed.

 

For the rest of us, let's continue a stage further. Although I have put this proposition simply, what I am saying is not really all that obvious when you have lots of other things to think about. Because as a person and a facilitator I dwell on these sort of things, it became obvious to me. Maybe we’re slow to realise the impact of directness because we are brought up to speak so politely, indirectly, abstractly, not to be aggressive, not to ruffle people's feathers. Could this also be a legacy from all those customer service programmes? We learned to be nice! And maybe we’re proud of our "business-speak”, after all it took long enough to learn.

 

In other words, maybe, like me for a long time, you have not noticed the impact, or resisted the feedback, or denied the truth, or not imagined a different possibility for using your voice. That’s understandable. I only stumbled on this by accident.

Listening to the wake-up call

 

Unknowingly I took a first big step towards the Voice Proposition by writing a diary for three years, getting to know myself differently, through writing out my reactions to life. Doing this I found my own "voice". It struck me quite forcibly, recognising myself in my words. It felt like all of a sudden I had grown bigger and stronger, that I had changed, and I wrote a book about how we can all learn to change using my diary as evidence, and offering it as a tool. It was only recently, after a lot more work and plenty of painful mistakes, did I make the next connection, of "voice" itself being what makes you a leader and what makes your organisation successful. Realising this was like an alarm clock ringing in my head, brrrrng, brrrrng, asking me to wake up and pay attention.

 

Like a heavy sleeper I was able to ignore it for a long time.

 

It was while I was driving down the motorway the day after hijacked planes destroyed the World Trade Centre and countless lives. I was listening to some classical music, trying to take my mind off the atrocious news. Yes I had often felt uneasy about how we lead organisations and implement business plans, both in commercial set-ups out to make a profit, and the ones that are supposed to serve us, such as schools, hospitals, police and prisons. Something about hierarchy, power, and the lack of importance given to people lower in the pecking order. But during this car journey, when the wake-up call came, it just all seemed so obvious. I realised the whole leadership thing is about VOICE. I realised how much we say, that is distorted, untrue, spun with the deliberate intention of misleading and manipulating the way others will respond. I realised how the consequences, mostly bad ones, have played themselves out through history, stretch forwards far into the future and spread out wide across the globe.

 

Realising this makes a huge difference in every situation you are in everyday.

Stopping people being small

 

When I am not allowed to react, I lack voice, which no matter how much I puff myself up to appear in control, in reality it leaves me feeling small, insignificant, incapable, vulnerable, low in self-worth. I know it is not only me, because when I draw out a person’s voice they grow and a new kind of energy comes out of them. When a person hears their voice in my voice, then some sort of bond can develop between us, and I feel I am being trusted then to lead. If they don’t find their voice in my voice, that is when they have a problem with me, the bomb starts to tick, and I only have so much time left... before... one of three things happens to me, and I suspect happens to you and your organisation: the other person walks out feeling resentful, they stay because they have to but become a terrorist, or I decide to walk out taking some damage to my reputation. This book is about creating a fourth possibility through the lesson of VOICE.

 

Maybe this has always been true, but it seems more important today, because of the dangerous, chaotic times we are living in. After recent years, with organisations going through so much change, contrary to the talk about empowerment, many of us have noticed power moving away, to a distant head office, possibly on the other side of the world, or decision-making authority moving further up the hierarchy, or to the architect of impersonal systems, procedures, scripts and brand standards, or to the writer of bossy emails.

 

What is the impact? It leaves everyone feeling... well, smaller. At the same time big organisations have got bigger, gone global and become more powerful in the world, more powerful than governments. Maybe linked to this, a business language has become all pervasive: a certain way of thinking and speaking that we can call "business-speak" is literally creating the world all of us live in. As I listen to the news of disasters, atrocities, riots and wars I wonder, I just wonder… about how different, and how much better the world might be if we had already learned the lesson of VOICE.

 

On a much more modest scale, I have known for a while that my work is about helping create organisations that people WANT to work in, realising that this kind of organisation is likely to be dramatically more successful than a place where people feel cynical, alienated, withdrawn. All the more so in the post-industrial scene, in our service and knowledge based economy.

 

What dawned on me that day driving in my car, was just this: that Voice is the key to making organisations places where people want to work. Voice is what makes people feel big, and able to make a bigger contribution. Voice is what creates the world we want to live in.

 

Perhaps you feel uncomfortable now about where this is going. Is this an argument for a new form of democracy in the workplace, a giving up on your responsibility to lead and direct? No, it's not big P political. But it is about how you react to other people, about how we react to each other and solve our problems together.

 

Facing up to strong reactions

 

I believe organisations facing crises (and at the time of writing I'm thinking of the dot.com crash, the fuel protests, floods, storms, rail crashes, fallout from terrorist attacks, airline bankruptcies…) require strong leadership to get through, but have you noticed how often in these circumstances a breakdown occurs? A strong leader takes a tough decision, perhaps to raise the targets, tighten budgets, reorganise work, and instead of gaining a motivated response and restoring a sense of control, the leader experiences what? Silence, deadness, sullen resentment, complete lack of reaction.

 

Heads go down, energy drains away just when the opposite is needed. Why? Maybe because strong leadership generates strong reactions inside us, not least amongst them but rarely owned up to is fear, and until those strong reactions are drawn out, voiced then reacted to by the leader, no one is able to adapt, or take the situation in or move into an action phase. The situation becomes frozen as if the strong leader did not exist. People continue to go about their daily work, and remain civil and polite to the leader, complaining privately in the toilets and in hushed corridor chats, but the vegetation has grown over, the clearing is no more, communication has ended.

 

It is as if a storm has taken down the telephone wires, and what is worse, no one wants to admit to it, even less do they want to start the repair work.

 

Giving and receiving voice is the repair work. Straight honest, heartfelt talk. People daring to react to one another directly, without covering up, in fact deliberately communicating their emotional response and reacting one to another, restoring connection. In this context voicing a "negative" reaction is not the same as resisting a leadership proposal, in fact, paradoxically, a negative reaction is a reaction all the same and as such valuable: just the starting point you need for unblocking a logjam. All depends on how as the leader you react to the reaction.

 

Perhaps you think this is proposing endless talk when the time for talk is over and decisions and actions are needed. Well, that may be true in the case of your organisation, but have you considered this? Maybe in all that talk people are trying to give voice to something, that needs to be said, but they haven't found the right words yet, or they feel prevented from speaking some unspeakable truth. If you rush people into action in such a situation, don't you just send their heads down again, or get mindless action and lots of mistakes. Isn't the challenge still to create the clearing for the kind of speaking and listening that gets the issue out, voiced, so that human intelligence is released to fix the problem?

 

So what if you want to learn the "voice" language that makes people feel big? Where do you start?

 

Noticing your flight into "business-speak"

 

Through the course of several conversations with Julian Burton, my artist colleague, we hit on the notion of there being two languages: indirect and direct. Since we both seem to enjoy a bit of theory and talking about the ideas in the books we read about communication, it happens in the course of our conversations, that we become a bit theoretical, that is "indirect", losing our impact, or to use the vernacular, "up our own backsides".  Then I notice the energy tends to drop as we lose each other. I've since noticed the same things can and does happen in almost every business meetings I'm in. Then my moist important role as a facilitator is to use and get others using with each other the second and direct language.

 

You can begin by noticing "business-speak". Look for  slightly stilted, abstract, superior, directive language many of us have deliberately learnt, to get us by over the years, fit certain situations, hold onto our power, bolster our egos. Unbeknown to us this language may have been sabotaging our efforts all the time to enrol people and win their support. This includes lots of forcing, have to, should and ought statements, and assumes a lack of choices. Recent research shows that 80% of employees in the UK are not fully engaged at work, with 20% actively disengaged, working against their employer, lacking in desire to collaborate, innovative or adapt, and with almost 11 days lost per person, estimated to cost employers more than £40bn pa in these lost days alone (Gallup, 2001). Could this disengaging language have anything to do with it?

 

This language fills the room with safe words, that don't really touch the people present, covering all sorts of points but avoiding mention at all costs of what is directly happening in the room in the present moment - which most probably is people feeling angry, ignored, frustrated, not trusted to think and talk for themselves, denied permission to say how they are reacting. Corked up.

 

Imagine hearing these statements in a meeting:

"This business plan is about a global ambition to be number one"

"Performance will be enhanced by attending to the basics"

"The change programme will be rolled out to all locations over the next 12 months"

 

Typical business-speak reactions to those statements might be "That's a good point. How do we get the message out? Let's make a decision. What actions do we need to take?". Typical felt but not voiced reactions might be "This person is arrogant. I feel cross but I don't know why. I wish I was that clever. I'm not telling them what I really think. Who the hell are you to say that? This is unspeakably stupid. What a lousy meeting. I wish I could get out of here.". Silence is often used be to signal the unspeakability of felt reactions, or some coded business-speak phrase like "I think there are hidden agendas going on here."

 

How did you react to the "business-speak" statements? Do you notice how they seem sure of themselves, difficult to disagree with? The speaker presumes that people, you and I, will fall in line, believe and do what we are asked to. But maybe you reckon that's just how people talk in meetings. And yes, you're right, but that's the point I'm making: let's look at how we talk to each other in business meetings.

 

Or maybe you're a bit embarrassed that you might have said similar things in the past. Please don't be. How many of us have said such things? I have. We all have. Colleagues challenge me almost everyday saying "We lost you there, where did you go?" or something similar, as if I floated away with my words, leaving them behind, went into a private space in my head. Then I come back into the room, feeling a bit sorry, recompose myself, and remind myself to use the language that keeps me connected.

 

The same thing happens for me as I am writing this. I stay connected by thinking about a few people I know and work with, the ones who criticise me for going into my head (you know who you are!) and how they might react as they are reading this. But every now and then I write a paragraph which although interesting to me, comes across as a bit involved, abstract and irrelevant, then I notice I have floated off and I delete it.

 

Although unlearning old habits like this, and learning a new language may not be an easy path for me or you, I believe it is important. Why? This brings us to…

 

 

The Voice Proposition part two - the CONSEQUENCES part

 

Again three short phrases:

 

 

1.   The most important lesson I've learned is that... I have a voice, and EVERYONE HAS A VOICE, but they don't always discover their voice, and they don't always use it. We don't always listen for a person's voice, and we don't always take notice of it even when it's blasting at full volume in both our ears.

 

2.   When a person doesn't find their voice and use it, then not only do they lose energy and reduce their contribution, but sooner or later TERRIBLE, destructive, painful CONSEQUENCES are likely to happen. Such as big arguments, fights and explosions. Broken relationships. Bullying. Simmering anger. Hatred. Suicide. Terrorism. War.

 

3.   When you do find your voice you get energy, wisdom, your world comes to life and WONDERFUL CONSEQUENCES are likely to happen. Impossible dreams can come true. You can find friends, supporters and all the money and resources you need to put your plan into action.

 

 

This book aims to teach you the basics of the Voice language, and shows you how to find your voice.

 

Learning the second language

 

The "voice" language is DIRECT. It is about NOW. It feels alive and exciting. It contains specifics that are relevant to you, me and us in this moment. It contains questions, reactions, facts. Most importantly it includes feelings. It respects you, the other person, and talks on a level with you as an equal human being, not down. It requires courage to speak the truth. It involves heart, compassion, caring about the impact of my words on you. It is experimental, arising in real time rather than pre-prepared. It is honest, straight talking and listening. A human moment of contact. The opposite of spin. It is relevant, true. It is what has to be said, even if it is difficult.

 

For example:

"I feel sad and disappointed at how things have turned out. What is your reaction?"

"I feel so excited about today's result"

"I'm not sure this plan is working"

"I just wish you could give us some real leadership here"

"I'm feeling angry and I don't know why"

 

When speaking and listening happens in this direct way, although what is said may not be comfortable, and it may be difficult at first to know how to respond, the rules are simple and the impact can be dramatic. The rule is you give your honest reaction, you say what is true. The impact is something positive happens, the energy shifts, you see people grow, the path forward emerges, or at least the obstacle become clear. If you make a mistake, that is OK too, because through the other person’s reaction you get instant, real time feedback, which if you take on board, you get back on track.

 

Perhaps this seems like permission to be rude, bullying and violent to people. Certainly that is not my intention. I'm known by my friends as quiet and fairly polite, and I believe we do need certain social rituals to get to know one another and feel comfortable. At the same time we need to know when it is the right to be direct, to cut through politics, to speak our truth even if it upsets others. This is not about polite avoidance of issues, nor is it about violent abuse of others. It is about effective management of your boundaries to make contact with, or "get through to" other people.


 Creating good conditions for directness

 

At that moment when it's time to get down to business, the choice is simple: faff about or have it out. Often I have noticed to my dismay, that faffing about is my chosen option. Maybe the risks were too high. Maybe the context was wrong. Certainly little gets faced this way, and although the consequences don't seem too bad, longer term the Voice Proposition says the price is high.

 

Surely it could not be so simple. What about hot situations, for example where a Foreign Secretary (or CEO) is needing to be statesmanlike, while representing the interest of a nation (or company)? Is it wrong for them to use phrases like "I'm afraid I can't go into detail" when the world (industry) is on the brink of war. Of course not. Such people must be acutely aware of the damage they could with loose talk, appearing weak, indecisive, overly aggressive or angry. All sorts of modes of conversation are appropriate. Abstract/direct is just one very useful distinction.

 

Occasionally you may run across an impressive leader, who knows exactly how to create a clearing for a real conversation. This always involves taking a risk. For example, one Chief Executive I know in a quoted international retail chain is respected throughout the company for this. We'll call him Fred. Typically Fred may be invited to talk to 50, 100, or 200 staff. It is as if he is talking to them individually, one to one. He invites any question from anyone, on any subject. People will voice their worries from whether profit targets can be achieved, to whether a company may be acquired or sold off, to whether the company should provide specific facilities customers ask for in the stores. Invariably to answer would divulge share price sensitive information. Instead of avoiding for fear of information leaking, this man takes a risk, asking those present to respect the sensitivity of what he is about to say, and to keep it within "these four walls". Then he voices his current thoughts and feelings, totally up front, for example, about whether he wants to buy or sell a company, and why.

 

Through being real, direct, Fred creates respect, trust and a huge sense of loyalty. The bond created with him is palpable. The deal about where responsibility resides is explicit: he is responsible for buying and selling companies but he shows he understand the impact this has on the people in the company, someone else decides about customer facilities in shops, everyone is charged with keeping sensitive information confidential and exercising judgement about what might be sensitive. 

 

By contrast, in some teams, the level of insecurity is so high that no one dares to say what is true, for fear of being made to look foolish, or weak. Talking happens, but rarely does anyone use their voice. Players have to protect their image, or they become vulnerable to attack by colleagues. What is missing in this situation? The clearing has yet to be created. Usually it is the leader who must model this, and show people the way. That is what leadership is. It does not mean being brutal in pursuit of honesty, or ripping people's insides out. The leader names what needs to be spoken about, and models the direct way of speaking and listening. Up to a point they support politeness, avoidance and face-saving, as long as everyone at some point gets down to business. The test is: are people voicing the issues directly, making real decisions and taking responsibility for implementing them?

 

The key difference between the two languages, "business-speak" and "voice", is to do with "contact". "Business-speak" hardly requires the presence of human beings, you could generate it on intelligent machines, running simple routines, one reacting to the other with another stream of business words that follow the basic rules of grammar and business strategy. Anything subjective, or emotional is avoided, suppressed, ignored, swept out of sight under the table. "Voice" is all about human contact, one person reacting with their fuller intelligence, including their emotion, trusting themselves and the other person to say what is true in the moment.
